We propose FusionBERT, a novel multi-view visual fusion frame-work for image–3D multimodal retrieval. Existing image–3D repre-sentation learning methods predominantly focus on feature align-ment of a single object image and its 3D model, limiting their

Extensive image–3D retrieval experiments demonstrate that FusionBERT achieves significantly higher retrieval accuracy than SOTA multimodal large models under both single-view and multi-view settings, establishing a strong baseline for multi-view multimodal retrieval.

## 1 Introduction 

Cross-modal retrieval between 2D images and 3D models has be-come a fundamental problem in computer vision, with applications ranging from robotics and digital asset management to augmented reality and 3D content creation. Recent advances in multimodal representation learning have enabled joint embedding of images, text, and 3D data, bridging heterogeneous modalities within unified latent spaces. Despite substantial progress, existing image–3D retrieval frame-works, including state-of-the-art (SOTA) baselines like OpenShape [ 9]and TAMM [ 24 ], face two primary limitations in real-world applica-tions. First, these SOTA multimodal models predominantly operate under a single-image contrastive learning setting, where only one image is encoded to retrieve a most similar 3D model. In practical scenarios such as e-commerce, cultural heritage digitalization and robotic perception, objects are frequently captured from multiple viewpoints. Multi-view observations provide richer structural and appearance information, mitigating ambiguities caused by occlu-sions, viewpoint bias or background clutter. However, SOTA mul-timodal models lack mechanisms for structured inter-view visual feature fusion. Naive strategies such as multi-feature averaging or concatenation fail to capture the fine-grained complementary rela-tionships across different views. Second, current 3D model encoders typically rely on both spatial coordinates and colors of sampled 3D points which are augmented heavily with texture information. However, many real-world 3D assets, such as raw LiDAR scans or textureless CAD models, usually lack reliable color data, which might causes failures on textureless or color-incomplete 3D shapes for most SOTA 3D encoders with their retrieval performance de-grading drastically. To overcome these limitations, we introduce FusionBERT, an en-hanced multi-view fusion framework for robust image-3D retrieval. Our FusionBERT is built upon an extension of TAMM model [ 24 ]. By significantly improving cross-modal alignment ability through the combination of an efficient multi-view visual aggregator for more powerful feature encoding of multiple images and a normal-enhanced 3D model encoder for more robust 3D representation, our model achieves better image-3D retrieval than SOTA works. An exemplar multi-view image–3D retrieval task of an object from Objaverse-LVIS dataset [ 4] is shown in Fig. 1. With multi-view images as queries, our FusionBERT achieves superior Top-1 re-trieval, outperforming SOTA image–3D retrieval models such as TAMM [24], OpenShape [9] and ULIP-2 [22]. In summary, our FusionBERT framework mainly contributes in the following two aspects: 

• Our FusionBERT enables more reliable image-3D alignment under multi-view observations by capturing complementary evidence across different views of the object. Rather than treating multiple images as independent inputs for visual encoding or aggregating their visual features with simple pooling, our framework leverages inter-view interactions to produce a more discriminative and view-consistent visual representation, leading to improved retrieval accuracy in challenging scenarios with occlusion, viewpoint variation, and background distraction. 

• FusionBERT improves the robustness of 3D representation by strengthening the encoder’s sensitivity towards intrinsic surface structures of the 3D object model instead of only superficial geometric and appearance cues from 3D point coordinates and texture colors. By introducing normal-aware geometric modeling, the learned 3D representation better preserves local surface characteristics and generalizes more effectively for textureless, uncolored, or color-incomplete shapes, thereby alleviating the key limitation of current 3D encoders such as Point-BERT [ 23 ], which strongly depends on that the 3D model should have texture colors for the sampled point cloud. By seamlessly integrating multi-view visual fusion and normal-enhanced 3D encoder, FusionBERT establishes a strong baseline for multi-view multimodal retrieval, which demonstrates consistent and substantial performance gains over existing SOTA multimodal models, as shown in the extensive experiments of Section 4. 

## 2 Related Work 

Existing works related to image-3D retrieval can be broadly orga-nized into three main directions: multimodal 3D representation learning which aligns text, images and 3D shapes into a shared embedding space, multi-view visual representation learning 

which exploits complementary information across different object views, and geometry-aware 3D point cloud encoding which aims to improve 3D representations beyond raw coordinates and color cues. In addition, recent 3D foundation models provide a broader research context for large-scale multimodal 3D learning, although their primary focus is often generation or open-world un-derstanding rather than retrieval. Our work lies in the intersection of these directions, with particular emphasis on improving multi-view visual aggregation approach while focusing more on a more powerful 3D representation by incorporating more surface geome-try information. ## 2.1 Multimodal 3D Representation Learning 

Recent years have seen rapid progress in multimodal models that align text, images and 3D data together inside a shared represen-tation space. For example, Michelangelo [ 25 ] learns a multimodal-aligned 3D latent representation that embeds shapes into a shared space with images and texts. More recent frameworks such as Open-Shape [ 9] and ULIP-2 [ 22 ] leverage large-scale contrastive learn-ing to align 3D representations with vision-language embedding spaces, enabling improved semantic understanding of 3D shapes. TAMM [ 24 ] introduces a tri-modal alignment framework for images, texts and 3D models, serving as a strong baseline for cross-modal retrieval. These methods have established principled foundations for unified multimodal 3D representation learning. However, these methods rarely explore how to incorporate visual cues from multi-view images for image-text-3D alignment, which is exactly the primary focus of our framework. Beyond retrieval-oriented alignment models, recent 3D foun-dation models such as Trellis3D [ 21 ], CraftsMan3D [ 8 ], Dora [ 2], TripoSF [ 6 ] and several other works like [ 10 , 13 ], have extended mul-timodal 3D representation research toward 3D content generation, reconstruction and language-guided understanding. For example, Trellis3D [ 21 ] proposes a structured latent 3D representation (SLAT) that unifies sparse geometry and features, enabling flexible decod-ing into multiple 3D formats. CraftsMan3D [ 8 ] and Dora [ 2] both ex-plore 3D representation learning within VAE/diffusion frameworks, where the former adopts a mesh-centric representation for explicit geometry modeling, while the latter analyzes how latent space de-sign affects representation expressiveness and diversity. TripoSF [ 6 ]presents a sparse and flexible 3D representation that better cap-tures fine details and complex topologies. Although these models highlight the growing importance of large-scale multimodal priors in 3D representation learning, they are generally not designed for fine-grained image-3D retrieval. 

## 2.2 Multi-View Visual Representation Learning 

Learning from multiple views has long been an effective strategy in 3D shape understanding. Early methods such as Su et al. [ 16 ] render 3D objects from multiple viewpoints and aggregate the resulting 2D features through simple pooling operations, such as max pooling or average pooling. More recent approaches like View-GCN [ 19 ]and Wang et al. [ 17 ] employ graph-based or Transformer-based architectures to model interactions among views more explicitly, enabling stronger multi-view feature learning for 3D reconstruction and understanding. In addition, some recent works have explored using multi-view images as an alternative to explicit 3D encoder. , , Li et al. 

For example, DuoDuo CLIP [ 7 ] demonstrates that strong 3D-aware representation can be learned directly from multiple rendered views. Although these works suggest that multi-view visual information can serve as a powerful bridge between 2D perception and 3D understanding, how to fuse multi-view relational information into a unified global visual representation for facilitating image–3D retrieval hasn’t been systematically studied. 

## 2.3 Geometry-Aware Point Cloud Encoding 

Point cloud encoder is a core component for most multimodal 3D retrieval systems. PointNet [ 11 ] pioneers direct learning on point cloud by processing unordered points with shared MLPs and symmetric aggregation functions, enabling permutation-invariant 3D representation learning. PointNet++ [ 12 ] extends this frame-work with hierarchical set abstraction to capture local geometric structures in point clouds. PointNeXt [ 14 ] further revisits this para-digm with improved training strategies and model design, establish-ing a strong and scalable baseline for point cloud understanding. Transformer-based architectures such as Point-BERT [ 23 ], have become standard choices for 3D feature extraction. In SOTA multi-modal retrieval pipelines like [ 9 , 24 ], these encoders process spatial coordinates together with RGB color channels to capture both ge-ometry and appearance information. Although effective for textured objects, this design often leads to an over-reliance on texture cues of 3D models, resulting in pos-sible performance degradation when handling textureless CAD models, raw LiDAR scans, or incomplete 3D assets with missing or unreliable color information. Recent 3D encoders such as Point-Net++ [ 12 ], PointNeXt [ 14 ] and DGCNN [ 18 ] support incorporating additional geometric features (e.g., surface normals) to enrich point cloud representations. Nevertheless, how to effectively leverage normals to encode fine-grained geometric structures and reduce texture reliance for robust image–3D retrieval remains underex-plored, while our FusionBERT explore a novel normal-aware 3D encoder for improved geometric representation.

## 4 Experiments 

We evaluate FusionBERT on image–3D retrieval benchmarks under both single-view and multi-view settings, using Top-K recall rate (Recall@K) as the primary retrieval accuracy evaluation metric. We pretrain our model on Four dataset (no LVIS), a large-scale union dataset used in OpenShape [ 9 ] and TAMM [ 24 ] comprising four major 3D benchmarks: Objaverse (no-LVIS) [ 4], ShapeNet [ 1 ], ABO [ 3] and 3D-FUTURE [ 5]. Specifically, Objaverse (no-LVIS) contributes approximately 800 𝐾 objects, and ShapeNet provides around 51 𝐾 synthetic models. ABO adds roughly 10 𝐾 high-fidelity real-world product scans, and 3D-FUTURE contributes about 10 𝐾 

high-quality CAD models, resulting in a diverse pre-training cor-pus of over 1.4 million image-text-3D tri-modal triplet samples. For evaluation, we conduct retrieval experiments on four benchmarks: Objaverse-LVIS [ 4], Objaverse-LVIS without RGB colors (LVIS no-RGB), ModelNet40 [ 20 ], and our self-captured Industrial Machinery Part (IMP) dataset. Objaverse-LVIS contains 46 , 832 high-quality 3D models across 1, 156 categories for fine-grained evaluation. To further evaluate robustness in the absence of color and texture cues, , , Li et al. 

we introduce LVIS no-RGB, a variant of Objaverse-LVIS where point cloud colors are removed. ModelNet40 serves as a standard benchmark for evaluating generalization across common 3D cat-egories. To support multi-view image-3D retrieval experiments, each 3D object of ModelNet40 is rendered from three random view-points to complete its image–text–3D triplet sample. To assess performance in real-world industrial scenarios, we introduce IMP, a dataset consisting of real photographs, texts and CAD models of 

374 industrial mechanical parts, reflecting the capabilities of the pretrained models in practical part retrieval tasks. 

## 4.1 Qualitative and Quantitative Evaluations 

We compare FusionBERT with SOTA models including TAMM [ 24 ], OpenShape [ 9] and ULIP-2 [ 22 ] as baselines to demonstrate its effectiveness in retrieving the right 3D model with both single-view and multi-view images as input query, with the multi-view aggregation strategy defaulting to mean pooling for OpenShape, TAMM and ULIP-2. From the comparison experiments in Table 1, it can be observed that our FusionBERT model achieves higher Recall@1, Recall@3 and Recall@5 than other SOTA models, in both single-view setting and 3-view configuration with our multi-view visual fusion compared to simple mean pooling of others. Moreover, the retrieval accuracy of our FusionBERT with 3 views shows significant advantage over the accuracy of our single-view setting, thereby demonstrating the effectiveness and robustness of our multi-view visual fusion approach under variant viewpoints. 

## 4.2 Ablation Studies 

We conduct ablation studies on the two key modules of our Fu-sionBERT: the multi-view visual aggregator and the normal-aware 3D encoder, on Objaverse-LVIS dataset. Table 4.2 presents com-parison results among full FusionBERT, FusionBERT with mean pooling (multi-view fusion disabled), FusionBERT with PointBERT encoder (normals disabled), and FusionBERT with both modules dis-abled, with three-view images as queries to get Recall@1, Recall@3, Recall@5 and Recall@10. The results show that both proposed com-ponents independently contribute to performance gains, and their combination yields the best retrieval accuracy. The multi-view vi-sual aggregator outperforms simple mean pooling by fully aligning multi-view visual contextual information with 3D models. Further-more, the normal-aware 3D encoder by incorporates additional surface geometric features to achieve better performance compared to PointBERT without normals. Fig. 6 also gives the ablation study on the number of input views for our FusionBERT, OpenShape [ 9], TAMM [ 24 ] and ULIP-2 [ 22 ] across 1 ∼ 12 input views with Top-5 retrieval accuracies on Objaverse-LVIS dataset, also with multi-view mean pooling strategy for other SOTA models. We can see that the Recall@5 of our FusionBERT steadily improves as the number of images increases. Besides, FusionBERT consistently achieves superior re-trieval accuracies over TAMM, OpenShape and ULIP-2, validating the effectiveness of our multi-view visual fusion strategy. 

## 4.3 Time Statistics 

We evaluate the runtime efficiency of FusionBERT on a single NVIDIA H20 Tensor Core GPU. With three images as input query, the multi-view visual encoding process takes 506 .99 ms in total, including 313 .85 ms for CLIP encoding of the 3 image views and 

193 .14 ms for the multi-view visual fusion module. For a point cloud with 10 , 000 points, normal-aware encoding requires 15 .55 ms, which is comparable to time consumption by PointBERT, demon-strating that our encoder maintains competitive efficiency in 3D feature extraction. 

## 5 Conclusion and Discussion 

We presented FusionBERT, a multi-view fusion framework for im-age–3D retrieval. By introducing a cross-attention-based multi-view visual fusion module combined with a normal-aware 3D encoding scheme, our image-text-3D tri-modal model effectively leverages complementary visual cues and enhances geometric representation robustness for robust cross-modal contrastive learning. Experi-mental results demonstrate substantial improvements over SOTA multimodal baselines, particularly in real multi-view scenarios. In conclusion, FusionBERT establishes a new direction for multi-view multimodal representation learning and highlights the importance of structured aggregation for cross-modal retrieval. Future work includes scaling up to larger multimodal pretraining corpora and exploring adaptive view selection strategies for multi-view visual fusion. Extending the proposed multi-view framework to generative 3D tasks is another possible direction.
