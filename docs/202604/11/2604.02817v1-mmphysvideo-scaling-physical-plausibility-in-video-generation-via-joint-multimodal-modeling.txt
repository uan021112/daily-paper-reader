ABSTRACT 

Despite advancements in generating visually stunning content, video diffusion models (VDMs) often yield physically inconsistent results due to pixel-only re-construction. To address this, we propose MMPhysVideo, the first framework to scale physical plausibility in video generation through joint multimodal modeling. We recast perceptual cues, specifically semantics, geometry, and spatio-temporal trajectory, into a unified pseudo-RGB format, enabling VDMs to directly capture complex physical dynamics. To mitigate cross-modal interference, we propose a Bidirectionally Controlled Teacher architecture, which utilizes parallel branches to fully decouple RGB and perception processing and adopts two zero-initialized control links to gradually learn pixel-wise consistency. For inference efficiency, the teacher’s physical prior is distilled into a single-stream student model via rep-resentation alignment. Furthermore, we present MMPhysPipe, a scalable data cu-ration and annotation pipeline tailored for constructing physics-rich multimodal datasets. MMPhysPipe employs a vision-language model (VLM) guided by a chain-of-visual-evidence rule to pinpoint physical subjects, enabling expert mod-els to extract multi-granular perceptual information. Without additional inference costs, MMPhysVideo consistently improves physical plausibility and visual qual-ity over advanced models across various benchmarks and achieves state-of-the-art performance compared to existing methods. 1 INTRODUCTION 

Fueled by the scaling of compute and data, video generation models have advanced rapidly (Wu et al., 2025; Wan et al., 2025; Peng et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025c), enabling a wide range of applications in content creation (Jiang et al., 2025b; Yang et al., 2026; Wei et al., 2024) and embodied agents (Fu et al., 2026; Zhao et al., 2025b; NVIDIA et al., 2025). Despite remarkable progress in visual aesthetics and fidelity, the lack of physical plausibility in synthesized videos remains a critical bottleneck in the pursuit of real-world simulators. To align visual outputs with physical principles, current studies focus on the integration of phys-ical priors into text-to-video (T2V) generators. One technical route encodes structured physical priors as prompts (Xue et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025c) or guidance (Yuan et al., 2026a; Zhao et al., 2026) for T2V generators. However, a significant domain gap exists when mapping textual physical descriptions to pixel-level distribution sampling (Wang et al., 2025g). Another technical route follows a post-tuning paradigm, which implicitly transfers knowledge from foundation mod-els into generators via supervised fine-tuning (SFT) (Wang et al., 2025g; Zhang et al., 2025b) or reinforcement learning (RL) (Chen et al., 2025a; Cai et al., 2026; Zhang et al., 2026). Alterna-tively, PhysHPO (Chen et al., 2025a) introduces hierarchical preference optimization guided by vision–language models (VLMs) (Wang et al., 2024). Nonetheless, such RL-based methods rely on complex reward design, which often suffer from reward hacking (Liu et al., 2025) and prohibitive training costs (Cai et al., 2026; Wang et al., 2026). These limitations arise a question: Is there a more robust mechanism for integrating physical priors into video generators that ensures explicit correspondence with specific physical content? 

We show the answer is affirmative by recasting perceptual cues, e.g. segmentation, geometry, as pseudo-RGB frames. This strategy provides a data format inherently compatible with the VDMs, empowering them to perform generative world modeling after mastering the perception and under-standing of the physical world. To achieve this goal, we identify and target two challenges: • Designing an effective joint modeling architecture to enhance physical consistency in generated videos. While existing diffusion-based perception methods typically employ a single-stream architecture via channel-wise (Bai et al., 2025b) or spatial-wise (Chen et al., 2025b) concatenation, directly adopting such designs leads to suboptimal performance on physics-centric benchmarks (Bansal et al., 2024) as shown in Tab. 2. • Exploiting physics-driven multimodal datasets . Unlike classic post tuning meth-ods (Chen et al., 2025a; Cai et al., 2026) or conditional generation tasks (Geng et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025b), our joint world modeling requires real-world videos with both rich physical phenomena and paired perceptual annotations capturing multi-granular physical dynamics. To thoroughly tackle these challenges, we propose MMPhysVideo , a framework designed to enhance the physical plausibility of video generation through joint multimodal training. MM-PhysVideo starts with a Bidirectionally Controlled Teacher (BCT) model that processes RGB and perceptual modalities in a decoupled manner. To minimize inter-modal interference, we distribute the two modalities into weight-shared parallel branches, ensuring that each modality utilizes an independent computational stream. We then incorporate bidirectional control links with zero-initialization to gradually learn pixel-wise alignment between RGB and perception modalities. To avoid additional computational overhead, the teacher model is distilled into a single-stream student via relation-based representation alignment. Furthermore, we propose a physics-centric data curation and multimodal annotation pipeline, MM-PhysPipe . Concretely, MMPhysPipe employs a VLM to categorize the physical phenomena in each video and generate the corresponding subject descriptions, which provide a chain-of-visual-evidence reasoning rule for the VLM to assess the physical richness score of each video. Subsequently, MM-PhysPipe leverages the grounding capabilities of segment anything model (SAM3) (Carion et al., 2026) to translate these subject descriptions into semantic masks. Finally, to capture spatial geom-etry and fine-grained dynamics, MMPhysPipe performs pointmap (XYZ) annotation (Wang et al., 2025b) and 3D point tracking (Xiao et al., 2025) within the masked regions. This multimodal mix effectively provides the dense perception supervision necessary for a world model to generate con-sistent physical interactions. 2Under review In summary, our main contributions are as follows: (1) We introduce MMPhysVideo, the first post-tuning framework that enhances the physical plausibility of video synthesis by joint multimodal modeling. MMPhysVideo adopts a Bidirectionally Controlled Teacher (BCT) architecture to pre-serve video priors while effectively learning physical perception dynamics. To maintain inference efficiency, we distill the teacher’s prior into a single-stream student via relation-based representation alignment. (2) We present MMPhysPipe, a comprehensive data curation and annotation pipeline to construct MMPhysData-36k with physics-rich multimodal datasets. (3) Quantitative and qual-itative results show that MMPhysVideo brings remarkable improvements in physical plausibility over various advanced models (i.e., CogVideoX (Yang et al., 2025c), Wan 2.1 (Wan et al., 2025)) and surpasses state-of-the-art physical-target methods on physics-centric benchmarks (i.e., Video-Phy (Bansal et al., 2024), PhyGenBench (Meng et al., 2024)). 

2 RELATED WORKS 

Physics-aware Video Generation. Benefiting from scaled data (Blattmann et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2025) and advanced architectures (Ma et al., 2025b; Peebles & Xie, 2023), video diffusion models (VDMs) (Qin et al., 2025) have made significant strides in visual fidelity and sensory realism (Yang et al., 2025c; Wan et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2025a; Wang et al., 2025f). However, physical consistency remains a formidable challenge even for state-of-the-art models, as noted by users and benchmarks (Bansal et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024; Kang et al., 2024; Motamed et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025a). While existing methods (Xie et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025a; Tan et al., 2024) leverage physics simulators to generate physics-grounded videos, this technical route is often bottlenecked by the simulator’s narrow scope and the inherent sim-to-real gap. Recent research has shifted toward integrating physical priors within VDMs. One paradigm treats physical knowledge as prompts (Xue et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025c) or guidance (Yuan et al., 2026b; Yang et al., 2025b; Zhao et al., 2026; Yuan et al., 2026a) to activate the physics potential in VDMs. Another paradigm (Cai et al., 2026; Chen et al., 2025a; Wang et al., 2025g; Zhang et al., 2025b) utilizes post-tuning to transfer physical insights from foundation (Oquab et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023) or specialized (Wang et al., 2024; Teed & Deng, 2020) models to VDMs. This paper, from a distinct perspective, enables generative models to inherently internalize physical dynamics through joint RGB-perception modeling. 

Diffusion Models for Perceptual Predictions. Beyond synthesis, another line of works (Ravis-hankar et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2025a) utilize diffusion models for various perception-related tasks. Notably, Marigold (Ke et al., 2024) pioneered the encoding of depth data as pseudo-RGB images to enable generative perceptual prediction. Following this idea, VDMs have been generalized by subsequent works to a broader range of perceptual modalities, including segmentation (Wang et al., 2025d; Zhu et al., 2024), surface normals (Bin et al., 2025; Liang et al., 2025), and geometric esti-mation (Bai et al., 2025b; Jiang et al., 2025a; Fang et al., 2025; Mi et al., 2025). Recently, several works (Huang et al., 2025a; Chefer et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025a; Xi et al., 2025a;b) have focused on joint mappings between perceptual conditions and video, achieving multimodal generation. How-ever, simply stacking these modalities often leads to inter-modal interference, which degrades the quality of the generated RGB video. In contrast, this paper explores how joint multimodal modeling can be used to enhance the physical consistency of RGB videos while maintaining visual fidelity.

MMPhysVideo extends video diffusion models (VDMs) (Qin et al., 2025) toward joint RGB and perception modeling to better capture the physical dynamics of the real world. Existing VDMs often suffer from artifacts such as semantic ambiguities, flickering scene structures, and inconsis-tent temporal deformations, all of which compromise the physical realism of generated videos. To address these issues, we incorporate three critical perceptual cues: semantics, geometry, and spatio-temporal trajectory. To improve training efficiency, we consolidate these multimodal signals into a unified pseudo-RGB video representation, Xpercep ∈ R3×F ×H×W , as detailed in Sec. 3.3. 3.2.1 JOINT MULTIMODAL MODELING 

Given a video sequence and its paired perceptual video Xpercep , we follow the LDMs paradigm to unify multimodal tasks: 

ˆϵrgb , ˆϵpercep = u(ztrgb , ztpercep , y, t ; θ) (2) where ztpercep is the perceptual latent encoded by 3D VAE, ˆϵrgb and ˆϵpercep represent the predicted noise for RGB and perceptual modalities, respectively. Unlike methods using perceptual cues as conditional inputs (Geng et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025b) or post-hoc estimates (Hu et al., 2025), our joint modeling paradigm generates both from noise to foster inherent perceptual understanding. Our experiments in Sec. 4.3 reveal that the model architecture for joint generation is crucial for overall performance. Consequently, we analyze several architecture design from the fields of perception and controllable generation, alongside our proposed decoupled architecture. 

Channel-wise Concatenation. Most attempts (Chefer et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025a) concatenate RGB and perceptual latents along the channel dimension, expanding the channels of input layer via zero-initialization to avoid training collapse. While this maintains a constant sequence length, pre-mature fusion and zero-initialized weights often degrade perceptual representation, yielding blurred results as shown in Fig. 7. 

Spatial-wise Concatenation. Recent works (Huang et al., 2025a; Ju et al., 2025; Tan et al., 2025) concatenate latents along spatial dimensions (i.e., height or width), patchifying modalities indepen-dently and using 3D RoPE (Su et al., 2024) for alignment. These single-stream architectures , which rely on concatenation-based fusion, fail to decouple the distinct features of each modality, leading to inter-modal interference with suboptimal performance. 3.2.2 BIDIRECTIONALLY CONTROLLED TEACHER MODEL 

We address these issues in a divide-and-conquer manner. We first fully decouple the computation of RGB and perceptual modalities through parallel branches to prevent modal confusion. Then, we introduce bidirectional control links to establish pixel-wise correspondences between the modalities. 

Parallel Branches. Building upon standard RGB processing, we construct a parallel computational branch for the perceptual modality, forming a dual-stream architecture . Instead of duplicating all DiT parameters, two branches share model weights while keeping the forward computation indepen-dent. This design preserves the pre-trained RGB video priors while seamlessly extending VDMs to perceptual video generation. To further enforce modal discriminability, we introduce learnable task embeddings, ergb and epercep , which are integrated into the diffusion timestep embedding e(t; θ):

trgb = ergb + e(t; θ), tpercep = epercep + e(t; θ) (3) Similarly, we also apply a distinct set of learnable task embeddings to the input latents. Then, the parallel computation branches can be formulated as: 

ˆzrgb = DiTBlocks( zrgb , t rgb ), ˆzpercep = DiTBlocks( zpercep , t percep ) (4) By isolating the computation, the DiT model can independently master the generation of the newly introduced perceptual modality, thereby reducing the overall optimization difficulty. 

Bidirectional Control Links. To capture fine-grained physical dynamics, perceptual modalities such as 3D point tracking should maintain pixel-wise consistency with the RGB stream. For inference efficiency, we distill the dual-stream teacher model back into a single-stream student model that processes only the RGB modality. However, directly performing MSE regression on raw features can be restrictive and degrade generation quality (Feng et al., 2024). Inspired by Vide-oREPA (Zhang et al., 2025b), we align the pairwise token similarities between the teacher’s and student’s representations, ˜ztrgb and zsrgb , across both spatial and temporal dimensions: 

Ldistill = |R spa (˜ ztrgb ) − R spa (hφ(zsrgb )) | + |R temp (˜ ztrgb ) − R temp (hφ(zsrgb )) | (6) where Rspa and Rtemp denote functions that compute token relations in the spatial and temporal dimensions, respectively, and hφ is a trainable MLP projector. Through this objective, the stu-dent model learns the spatio-temporal structural dependencies inherent in the teacher’s fused RGB-perceptual representations. Initialized with teacher weights, the student model integrate dual-stream priors of perception and RGB into a single DiT. This allows the single-stream model to internalize physical priors and generate consistent videos without the auxiliary perceptual branch. 3.3 MMP HYS PIPE 

To bridge the gap in physics-aware generation via joint multimodal modeling, we introduce a scal-able dataset pipeline, MMPhysPipe. MMPhysPipe is designed to overcome two primary bottle-necks: the curation of video samples exhibiting rich physical phenomena from large text-video datasets (Nan et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025e; Chen et al., 2024b) and the production of high-fidelity, multi-granular perceptual annotations. 3.3.1 DATA CURATION 

Given a large-scale text-video dataset (we adopt OpenVidHD-0.4M (Nan et al., 2024) in this work, which is widely utilized for post-tuning), MMPhysPipe first employs a lightweight Video Quality Assessment (VQA) model from (Wang et al., 2025e) to efficiently filter out low-quality samples, 6Under review such as freeze-frame or overexposed videos. The VQA model provides an initial holistic score for each video by integrating multiple dimensions, including dynamics, aesthetics, and clarity. 

Reality. Subsequently, we select raw videos captured by physical cameras in real-world environ-ments using a Vision-Language Model (VLM), i.e., Qwen3-VL (Bai et al., 2025a). We formalize the concept of ”real-world raw videos” through a rigorous set of visual constraints spanning four key dimensions: Style, Context, Graphics, and Temporal Continuity, as shown in Fig. 4, which provide explicit evaluation protocol for Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Zhang et al., 2025a) reasoning. 

Physical Richness. Following prior works (Wang et al., 2025c), we categorize physical phenom-ena into three primary domains, each subdivided into fine-grained primitives, as shown in Fig. 4. MMPhysPipe leverages Qwen3-VL (Bai et al., 2025a) as a physical evaluator to assign an inten-sity score ranging from 1 to 5 for each primitive, where 1 denotes a negligible occurrence and 5

represents high-impact manifestation. To mitigate hallucinations outputs, we implement a Chain-of-Visual-Evidence (CoVE) (Huang et al., 2025b) reasoning rule: the VLM should articulate specific visual cues, such as momentum exchange in a collision, before quantifying the intensity. Crucially, for primitives achieving the highest intensity scores, the VLM performs Physical Subject Extraction , generating concise descriptive phrases (e.g., ”steam rising from pan”). These phrases act as semantic anchors, bridging the gap between high-level physical principles and the subsequent low-level perceptual grounding annotation.

To provide a comprehensive perception of physical content, we perform multi-granular annotation in a top-down manner, spanning semantic, geometry, and spatiotemporal trajectory. 

Instance Segmentation. Based on the physical subject descriptions generated by the VLM, we leverage SAM3 (Carion et al., 2026) with promptable concept segmentation to transfer linguistic noun phrases to semantic masks. By utilizing SAM3’s Presence Head, MMPhysPipe effectively eliminates hallucinated subjects, retaining only masks for physically active subjects. 

Geometry and Point Tracking. Within the masked regions, we employ a unified, feed-forward 3D tracker (Wang et al., 2025b; Xiao et al., 2025) to simultaneously estimate scene geometry and 3D point trajectories {pi(s) ∈ R3}Fs=1 , where s is the frame index. The color ri of each point is assigned based on its normalized coordinates in the initial frame (s = 1) , where we map the 

(x, y, 1/z ) coordinates into the [0 , 1] 3 RGB space. These colors remain constant across all frames to maintain temporal identity. For each frame s, the 3D points are projected onto the s-th camera plane to encode the final perceptual RGB frame. 4 EXPERIMENTS 

4.1 EXPERIMENTAL SETUP 

Baselines. To assess the generality of our joint multimodal modeling framework, we integrate MMPhysVideo within advanced T2V video diffusion models, Wan2.1-1.3B (Wan et al., 2025), CogvideoX-5B and 2B (Yang et al., 2025c). For a comprehensive comparison, we adopt several competitive baselines with open-source code: 1) PhyT2V (Xue et al., 2025) and VideoREPA (Zhang et al., 2025b), which specialize in enhancing physical plausibility 2) OmniVDiff (Xi et al., 2025a), a single-stream multimodal generation method. Comparison results with other RL-based and guidance-based methods are shown in Appendix. 

Implementation Details. Stage I. We first train the teacher models on our curated MMPhysData-36k dataset with a global batch size of 16 for 5K steps. We employ the AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) optimizer with a constant learning rate of 2 × 10 −5. Stage II . For the distillation phase, the single-stream student model is initialized with the weights of the pre-trained teacher. We perform representation alignment on the DiT blocks where control links are inserted. The student model is finetuned for 1k steps with a global batch size of 32 and a learning rate of 2 × 10 −6,following (Zhang et al., 2025b). For the ablation studies, we utilize the CogVideoX-2B backbone under the Stage I configuration as the default setting. 

Evaluation. We evaluate MMPhysVideo on two physics-centric benchmarks: (1) VideoPhy (Bansal et al., 2024), which focuses on interactions across solid-solid (SS), solid-fluid (SF), and fluid-fluid (FF) phases. (2) PhyGenBench (Meng et al., 2024), which covers physical domains includ-ing mechanics (Mec), optics (Opt), thermodynamics (Tdm), and material properties (Mat). For quantitative assessment, we employ VideoScore2 (He et al., 2025) as our primary automated eval-uator with metric across Physical Consistency (PC) and Semantic Alignment (SA). Compared to VideoCon-Physics (Bansal et al., 2024), VideoScore2 leverages CoT rule to generate reasons for its scores, which ensures a more robust, human-aligned evaluations. We also provide results using the VideoCon-Physics evaluator, alongside VBench (Huang et al., 2024) in Appendix. 4.2 MAIN RESULTS

4.3 ABLATIONS AND ANALYSIS 

Variants of Architectures for Joint Modeling. In Sec. 3.2, we introduce a novel dual-stream archi-tecture that leverages bidirectional control links within parallel RGB-perceptual branches to achieve ”pixel-wise” cross-modal interaction. To verify its effectiveness, we conduct a comparative study against alternative fusion strategies, including the ”channel-wise” fusion used in baseline method (Xi et al., 2025a; Chefer et al., 2025) and the ”spatial-wise” fusion in (Huang et al., 2025a; Chen et al., 2025b). For a fair comparison, we only modify the joint modeling architecture while keeping the training data, and training steps identical. As shown by the qualitative and quantitative results in Fig. 7 and Tab. 2, our method yields fine-grained perceptual predictions and significantly enhances the average PC score. Furthermore, we observe that premature modality fusion (e.g., ”channel-wise” fusion) tends to exacerbate inter-modal interference, degrading the quality of both outputs.

To evaluate the contribution of each perceptual modality and the effectiveness of our unified videos introduced in Sec. 3.3, we conduct a break-down ablation for the annotation pipeline in MMPhysPipe. Specifically, we perform separate annotations for each per-ceptual modality on our constructed MMPhysData-36k dataset, including segmentation, pointmap (XYZ), and point tracking. 5 CONCLUSION 

In this paper, we propose MMPhysVideo, a novel framework designed to enhance the physical con-sistency of video generation through joint multimodal modeling. To achieve this, we first develop an innovative dual-stream teacher architecture to decouple the processing of RGB and perceptual modalities, distilling it into a single-stream student via representation alignment. Complementing this, we propose MMPhysPipe, an end-to-end pipeline for curating data with rich physical interac-tions and generating multi-granular perceptual annotations. Both quantitative and qualitative results on the physics-focused benchmarks demonstrate the superiority of MMPhysVideo with consistent gains across advanced models, setting a new state-of-the-art for physics-consistent video generation.

A MORE DETAILS OF MMP HYS VIDEO 

A.1 TRAINING DETAILS 

All experiments are conducted on a single machine equipped with eight NVIDIA L40S GPUs (48GB VRAM each). We perform full-parameter fine-tuning for all models, utilizing the DeepSpeed ZeRO-2 optimization strategy to enhance training efficiency. To incorporate the proposed Bidirectionally Controlled Teacher (BCT), we insert control links within the dual-stream Diffusion Transformer (DiT). Specifically, for the 42-layer Transformer of CogVideoX-5B, the links are placed at blocks 

[3 , 9, 15 , 21 , 27 , 33 , 39] . For the 30-layer Transformers of Wan2.1-1.3B and CogVideoX-2B, we select blocks [3 , 9, 15 , 21 , 27] as insertion points. An additional set of bidirectional control links is implemented prior to the Transformer across all models. Regarding the data configuration, Wan2.1-1.3B is trained with a resolution of 480 × 832 and a sequence length of 81 frames at 16 fps. For the CogVideoX series, we adopt a resolution of 480 × 720 with 49 frames at 8 fps. A.2 INFERENCE DETAILS 

For inference, we employ the DDIM sampler with 50 steps. Benefiting from the proposed relation-based representation distillation, our student model avoid the need for a dual-stream architecture to simultaneously generate RGB and perceptual modalities. As the student model contains no control links, it shares the exact same inference procedure and computational cost as the base model.

Following the taxonomy established in WISA (Wang et al., 2025c), we categorize physical phe-nomena into three primary domains: Dynamics, Thermodynamics, and Optics. Each domain is further subdivided into a set of fine-grained physical primitives, as detailed below: (1) Dynamic :

rigid body motion, collision, liquid motion, gas motion, elastic motion, deformation. (2) Ther-modynamic : melting, solidification, vaporization, liquefaction, combustion, explosion. (3) Optic :

reflection, refraction, scattering, interference and diffraction, unnatural light source 

The final Physical Richness score is derived by aggregating the scores across these three domains. Taking the Dynamic domain as an illustration: for a given video, we first compute the mean intensity score across all Dynamic primitives to obtain the domain-specific score, denoted as rd ∈ [1 , 5] . Sim-ilarly, the Thermodynamics score rt and Optics score ro are obtained through the same procedure. Finally, the total Physical Richness score is calculated as the average of the scores from all three domains. B.4 RESAMPLING

Given a video, the physical subjects descriptions from the VLM are processed by SAM3 (Carion et al., 2026) for open-vocabulary segmentation. Subsequently, 2,500 points are uniformly sampled from the union of all masked regions and projected into 3D space using the camera intrinsic and extrinsic parameters estimated by VGGT (Wang et al., 2025b). Finally, we employ SpatialTrack-erv2 (Xiao et al., 2025) to perform point tracking on these uniformly distributed 3D points, which are then re-projected onto the 2D plane as video sequences. In these sequences, the point size is dynamically adjusted to accommodate varying levels of granularity. B.6 STATISTICAL OVERVIEW
