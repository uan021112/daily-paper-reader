Abstract. Crowd instance segmentation is a crucial task with a wide range of applications, including surveillance and transportation. Cur-rently, point labels are common in crowd datasets, while region labels (e.g., boxes) are rare and inaccurate. The masks obtained through seg-mentation help to improve the accuracy of region labels and resolve the correspondence between individual location coordinates and crowd den-sity maps. However, directly applying currently popular large foundation models such as SAM does not yield ideal results in dense crowds. To this end, we first propose Dense Point-to-Mask Optimization (DPMO), which integrates SAM with the Nearest Neighbor Exclusive Circle (NNEC) con-straint to generate dense instance segmentation from point annotations. With DPMO and manual correction, we obtain mask annotations from the existing point annotations for traditional crowd datasets. Then, to predict instance segmentation in dense crowds, we propose a Reinforced Point Selection (RPS) framework trained with Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), which selects the best predicted point from a sampling of the initial point prediction. Through extensive experiments, we achieve state-of-the-art crowd instance segmentation performance on ShanghaiTech, UCF-QNRF, JHU-CROWD++, and NWPU-Crowd datasets. Furthermore, we design new loss functions supervised by masks that boost counting performance across different models, demonstrating the significant role of mask annotations in enhancing counting accuracy. 

Keywords: Crowd instance segmentation · Segmentation · Crowd count-ing

Crowd Counting Traditional models can be divided into two categories based on whether they generate crowd instance-level information (i.e., bounding boxes or points): density map-based prediction networks and point coordinate-based prediction networks. Density map-based methods predict crowd count by generating a probability density map, where integrating pixel values yields the total number of people. [33, 44,46,48] have improved the prediction accuracy of density maps utilizing multi-layer neural networks and contextual information. Given that traditional density maps fail to distinguish individual heads in crowded areas due to occlusion, [6,14] proposed algorithms that identify potential head locations and then perform accurate counting based on those locations. With the help of head masks, [7, 12, 29, 43] generated the enhanced density map, which can effectively solve the problem of low recall of head detection. After the advent of the transformer, [5,39] attempted to apply this advanced technology to crowd counting tasks. [11,20,24] utilized the cross-modal alignment capability of the CLIP model [27] for crowd counting using text constraints. Abbreviated paper title 5

Point coordinates-based networks directly predict head bounding boxes [23] or head coordinates [19], deriving crowd count by counting these predictions. Re-searchers, such as [31, 35], have completely abandoned traditional density maps and directly estimated the crowd size by statistically analyzing the point coor-dinates. [22] introduced a point-query quad-tree mechanism, which allows the model to proactively generate sparse point queries and adaptively focus on re-gions where targets might exist. [3, 28] achieved more accurate crowd counting by improving the accuracy of model localization. While such networks can lo-cate individuals, they lack fine-grained information like the shape and size of individuals. Therefore, to achieve crowd instance segmentation, it is necessary to combine a segmentation model to obtain more granular detailed information. Conventional crowd counting techniques struggle to predict the shape and scale of individuals within a crowd. Additionally, existing segmentation methods tend to perform poorly in dense scenes. To address these limitations, we propose a Dense Point-to-Mask Optimization with Reinforced Point Selection (DPMO-RPS) framework that generates non-overlapping masks for individuals, resulting in effective crowd instance segmentation.

3.1 Dense Point-to-Mask Optimization (DPMO) 

We propose a DPMO framework for instance segmentation in crowds, using SAM [15] with NNEC constraint. If the image of crowd is directly input into SAM [15] for segmentation, SAM [15] cannot segment individuals one by one because of the small size and occlusion in dense areas. Furthermore, the pixels in these areas are difficult to distinguish as belonging to foreground or background. Therefore, in dense scene of the image, the mask obtained by SAM [15] segmentation may still be overlapping and incomplete. To solve this problem, we propose NNEC algorithm to optimize the scale of segmentation masks in dense regions. The algorithm aims to find the maximum possible range of the segmentation mask region for each point label. Since each segmentation mask corresponds uniquely to the point coordinate, it should contain only its own point. To better cover the head region, we allow circles to overlap and adjust their radii without boundary restrictions. The formula of this algorithm is as follows:

3.2 Reinforced Points Selection (RPS) network with GRPO 

Based on the proposed DPMO, we propose RPS network trained with Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) [32] method to learn better coordinates as point prompts for SAM [15] to improve the instance segmentation in crowds. The RPS network with GRPO [32] consists of two parts: point prediction and point selection. The point prediction network is an improvement based on the PET [22] model, namely Optimized PET (OPET). Unlike the PET [22] model, which matches predicted points with real points and selects the optimal one based on category and distance, our OPET uses a new mask-supervised loss function to restrict predicted points within the mask’s range corresponding to each real point. See section 3.3 for details. Furthermore, we sample four other points around each initial prediction using Gaussian sampling. 4.1 Evaluation criteria 

The evaluation criteria for crowd instance segmentation consider both the quan-tity and quality of the segmentation mask. Our evaluation criteria include IoU, Precision, Recall, and F1, with the specific calculation formulas as follows. First, we use Intersection over Union (IoU) to evaluate segmentation mask quality. Segmentation masks are generated from the image using our DPMO-RPS framework and matched with mask annotations. We compute the IoU be-tween masks and visualize the resulting matrix. Following the Hungarian match-ing algorithm, we use the IoU matrix as a cost matrix to determine the optimal matched mask pairs for each image. The final dataset’s overall IoU is calculated by averaging the IoU values of these matched pairs. Second, we use Precision, Recall, and F1 calculated from the confusion matrix to evaluate the overall segmentation performance. The specific formula is as follows:                      

> Precision =TP TP +FP ,Recall =TP TP +FN ,F1 = 2×(Precision ×Recall )

DPMO In our DPMO paradigm, the NNEC constraint significantly enhances the segmentation capabilities of SAM. The experimental results on ShanghaiTech A [46] dataset, shown in Table 4, indicate that the absence of the NNEC con-straint reduces segmentation accuracy. Furthermore, we also evaluated different NNEC algorithm designs on ShanghaiTech A [46], comparing results with mask annotations, as shown in Figure 6. The experiments show that NNEC design with overlapped and unbounded circles in DPMO can help masks include more effective regional information. 

Prompt for DPMO Our instance segmentation method uses point prompts generated from point annotations to assist SAM in segmentation. Since a few crowd datasets like NWPU-Crowd [40] also include box annotations, we com-pared the effect of box-based and point-based prompts on segmentation results with mask annotations on NWPU-Crowd [40] dataset, as shown in Table 5. The experiment shows that DPMO with point-based prompts has better performance in crowd instance segmentation, because the boxes in datasets are often larger than the human head and lacks human verification especially in dense area. 

RPS We propose RPS to select the best predicted point as a prompt to gener-ate a better segmentation mask. RPS, trained with GRPO [32] method and used for point selection, filters out points in all predicted and sampled points, which can achieve better segmentation results. We compared the difference in instance segmentation performance between models with and without RPS through ex-14 H. Chen et al. 

periments, as shown in Table 4. Results indicate that RPS can help further improve the accuracy of crowd instance segmentation. 

Point prediction network for RPS Our proposed DPMO-RPS framework for crowd instance segmentation improves upon the PET [22] model for point prediction and selection. Table 6 compares its performance when integrated with other point-based crowd counting models. Experiments show that our DPMO-RPS framework with PET can realize better segmentation for crowd than other point-predicted models, and our DPMO-RPS paradigm is helpful in improving the accuracy of SAM for instance segmentation in dense crowds. 

4.6 Hyperparameters 

In our DPMO-RPS framework, two key hyperparameters are the Gaussian kernel radius during sampling and the limitation on the radius range of the circle in NNEC algorithm. The results are shown in Figure 7. The purpose of sampling initial predicted points is to select points more suitable for instance segmentation based on a basic understanding of individual locations. Therefore, the Gaussian kernel radius selected during sampling is crit-ical for the final segmentation result. An overly large Gaussian radius samples points outside the head, causing segmentation errors, while an overly small ra-dius yields points too similar to the initial prediction, diminishing the benefit of sampling. We set it to 1 in sparse area and 1 in dense area based on the results shown in Figure 7. In the NNEC algorithm, the role of the maximum circle is to limit the size of the segmentation mask and ensure that each point has a corresponding seg-mentation mask. If the radius of the maximum circle is too large, it may include unwanted body regions. Conversely, if the radius is too small, it might fail to effectively segment heads in densely populated areas. We set it to 5 of min radius and 200 of max radius based on the results shown in Figure 7. 

## 5 Conclusion 

We propose a novel Dense Point-to-Mask Optimization with Reinforced Point Selection (DPMO-RPS) framework for crowd instance segmentation, including Dense Point-to-Mask Optimization (DPMO) with Nearest Neighbor Exclusive Circle (NNEC) constraint and Reinforced Point Selection (RPS) network trained with GRPO. The NNEC constraint in DPMO ensures non-overlapping masks with one-to-one correspondence to point annotations. Then, the RPS predicts individual locations as point prompts for SAM in instance segmentation. End-to-end training of our DPMO-RPS framework, we achieve the state-of-the-art crowd instance segmentation performance on ShanghaiTech, UCF-QNRF, JHU-Crowd++, and NWPU-Crowd datasets. Furthermore, we design two loss func-tions based on density map prediction and point prediction, which are supervised by robust mask annotations with manual corrections. The improvement of count-ing accuracy across multiple models indicates that segmentation masks also play an important role in improving prediction and counting accuracy.
