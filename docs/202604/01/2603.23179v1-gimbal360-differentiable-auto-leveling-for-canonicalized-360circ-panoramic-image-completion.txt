Abstract. Diffusion models excel at 2D outpainting, but extending them to 360 ◦ panoramic completion from unposed perspective images is chal-lenging due to the geometric and topological mismatch between per-spective projections and spherical panoramas. We present Gimbal360 ,a principled framework that explicitly bridges perspective observations and spherical panoramas. We introduce a Canonical Viewing Space that regularizes projective geometry and provides a consistent intermediate representation between the two domains. To anchor in-the-wild inputs to this space, we propose a Differentiable Auto-Leveling module that stabilizes feature orientation without requiring camera parameters at in-ference. Panoramic generation also introduces a topological challenge. Standard generative architectures assume a bounded Euclidean image plane, while Equirectangular Projection (ERP) panoramas exhibit intrin-sic S1 periodicity. Euclidean operations therefore break boundary con-tinuity. We address this mismatch by enforcing topological equivariance in the latent space to preserve seamless periodic structure. To support this formulation, we introduce Horizon360, a curated large-scale dataset of gravity-aligned panoramic environments. Extensive experiments show that explicitly standardizing geometric and topological priors enables Gimbal360 to achieve state-of-the-art performance in structurally con-sistent 360 ◦ scene completion.

## 1 Introduction 

The advent of large-scale diffusion models [37] has catalyzed major advances in generative computer vision, particularly in image outpainting and comple-tion [31, 59]. However, extending these models to 360 ◦ panoramic completion remains fundamentally challenging. Compared to the abundance of perspec-tive images used to train modern diffusion priors, high-quality omnidirectional datasets remain scarce. Consequently, the most practical paradigm is to leverage pre-trained 2D diffusion models to extrapolate a full panorama from a single nar-row field-of-view image. Yet this task exposes a fundamental mismatch: diffusion models are trained on Euclidean image grids ( R2), whereas a panorama lies on a closed spherical manifold ( S2) parameterized by Equirectangular Projection (ERP). We argue that many common failure modes in omnidirectional com-pletion, including structural collapse, architectural hallucination, and boundary seams, arise from this unresolved geometric and topological domain gap. The first domain gap arises from projective variance . A 360 ◦ ERP forms a continuous spherical canvas, but an unposed perspective image is captured under arbitrary camera pitch and roll. When such an image is naively projected onto the sphere, straight lines in the 3D world become highly non-linear sinusoidal curves. These distortions violate the Manhattan-world structural priors implic-itly learned by diffusion models from perspective imagery. As a result, existing methods for panoramic outpainting [18,30,49,60] typically assume perfectly lev-eled inputs or require ground-truth camera extrinsics to preserve structural co-herence, which limits their applicability in real-world settings. A seemingly natu-ral alternative is to learn the alignment implicitly using spatial transformers [19]. However, coupling unconstrained spatial deformation with the diffusion objec-tive is notoriously unstable. Because diffusion losses emphasize pixel-level re-construction, they propagate high-frequency gradients that encourage non-rigid spatial warping, often resulting in unstable “jelly-like” transformations rather than coherent geometric alignment. The second domain gap stems from topological inconsistency . An ERP panorama exhibits intrinsic S1 periodicity along the horizontal axis, meaning its left and right boundaries represent the same physical location. In contrast, standard gen-erative architectures assume a bounded Euclidean image plane. This assumption is embedded throughout the pipeline: convolutional layers in the Variational Au-toencoder (VAE) introduce artificial borders through zero-padding, while Dif-fusion Transformers (DiTs) employ absolute positional encodings that treat op-posite boundaries as maximally distant. Consequently, the generative process breaks the periodic continuity of the spherical domain. Prior work attempts to mitigate this issue through inference-time heuristics such as circular blend-ing [11] or synchronized multi-view denoising [26]. However, these post-hoc cor-rections behave as spatial low-pass filters. While they reduce visible seams, they inevitably smooth high-frequency details and cannot fundamentally resolve the underlying topological mismatch. In this work, we present Gimbal360 , a principled framework that reformu-lates panoramic outpainting as omnidirectional scene completion under explicit Gimbal360 3

geometric and topological regularization. Instead of forcing diffusion models to implicitly learn complex distortions, we introduce a Canonical Viewing Space that standardizes the spherical geometry. By enforcing a gravity-aligned coor-dinate system in which the environmental horizon coincides with the spherical equator, the diffusion prior operates within a geometrically consistent manifold where structural regularities are preserved. To support this formulation, we cu-rate a large-scale omnidirectional dataset, Horizon360, whose panoramas are rigorously aligned to satisfy these canonical constraints. To bridge the gap between unconstrained real-world inputs and this canonical representation, we propose a Differentiable Auto-Leveling module. This module estimates dense spatial correspondences but constrains them to a rigid trans-formation through a soft-argmin solver. This architectural bottleneck acts as a geometric regularizer that filters out unstable deformation gradients and allows only low-rank rigid alignment errors to propagate during training. As a result, the model autonomously stabilizes perspective features and maps them into the Canonical Viewing Space, eliminating the need for ground-truth camera extrin-sics at inference. Finally, we address the Euclidean boundary assumption by enforcing topolog-ical equivariance directly within the generative pipeline. Through circular latent encoding and a Siamese shift-equivariance constraint during diffusion training, the model learns to respect the intrinsic S1 periodic structure of panoramic im-agery. This design preserves boundary continuity at the architectural level and substantially reduces seam artifacts during generation. In summary, our contributions are threefold: 

– We identify the geometric and topological domain gaps that cause structural failures when Euclidean diffusion priors are applied to 360 ◦ panoramas, and introduce a Canonical Viewing Space together with a large-scale gravity-aligned dataset Horizon360 to establish a unified structural prior. 

– We propose a Differentiable Auto-Leveling module that regularizes geometric alignment by constraining dense correspondences to a rigid transformation via a soft-argmin solver, enabling stable alignment of unposed inputs without requiring camera extrinsics. 

– We enforce topological equivariance in the generative pipeline through circu-lar latent encoding and Siamese shift-equivariant training, enabling diffusion models to respect the intrinsic S1 periodicity of panoramic imagery. 

## 2 Related Work 

Image Outpainting and Completion. Image outpainting aims to extrapolate visual content beyond the original image boundaries while preserving semantic and structural consistency [5,9,53,56]. Early approaches predominantly relied on Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) [12] to hallucinate missing regions [46, 58, 59]. With the emergence of diffusion models, image completion has achieved substantial improvements in visual fidelity and semantic coherence [8, 31, 34, 37]. Latent diffusion models in particular enable flexible conditioning on image 4 Lu et al. 

prompts or text to extend perspective images beyond their original field of view. However, these approaches are inherently designed for planar images defined on Euclidean grids ( R2). When directly applied to omnidirectional extrapolation, they struggle with the geometric distortions and closed-loop topology of spherical panoramas, often producing structural inconsistencies and semantic drift as the generated field of view expands. 

360 ◦ Panorama Generation. Panoramic image generation has progressed rapidly from early GAN-based systems to recent diffusion-based models. Initial works such as BIPS [32], Text2Light [6], and OmniDreamer [1] demonstrated the feasibility of synthesizing HDR or RGB panoramas from textual or visual cues. More recently, diffusion frameworks such as PanoGen [27] and Diffusion360 [11] have enabled higher-quality text-to-panorama generation. To improve struc-tural consistency, PanFusion [55] connects perspective and equirectangular rep-resentations through a dual-branch architecture, while PanoFree [29] introduces correspondence-aware cross-attention for localized view conditioning. Extending a single narrow field-of-view image into a full panorama is a more constrained and challenging problem. Methods such as PanoDiffusion [49] and PanoDecouple [60] explore structural priors to lift perspective images into omnidirectional views. Most diffusion-based panorama outpainting approaches [10, 18, 30, 40, 49, 60] project the input perspective image into an ERP representation using predefined camera parameters and perform diffusion-based outpainting in this space. How-ever, such pipelines often assume leveled cameras or known camera extrinsics, which limits their applicability to real-world images captured in unconstrained settings [42, 54]. 

Geometric Adaptations in Omnidirectional Vision. Unlike perspective images, 360 ◦ ERP exhibits severe latitude-dependent distortions and requires continuity across periodic boundaries. Prior work addresses geometric distortion through either distortion-aware architectures or alternative projection represen-tations. Distortion-aware approaches modify network operators to respect spher-ical geometry, such as spherical convolutions in SphereNet [7] and SMGD [41], or geometry-aware attention mechanisms as in SphereDiffusion [48]. Projection-driven methods instead avoid ERP distortions by reparameterizing the sphere into cubemaps (CubeDiff [22], DreamCube [17]) or tangent-plane representa-tions (TanDiT [3]). Another fundamental challenge lies in preserving continuity across the S1 periodic boundary of panoramic images. Some works modify net-work operators, such as cylindrical convolutions in Cylin-Painting [28]. Others enforce continuity during inference through techniques like circular blending [11], bidirectional guidance [26], or circular padding [45]. While these methods reduce visible seams, they act as post-hoc corrections. Ensuring strict topological equiv-ariance within the generative modeling process itself remains an open challenge.

3.2 The Omnidirectional Domain Gaps 

To successfully integrate Euclidean ( R2) generative priors with the spherical (S2) omnidirectional manifold, our framework is dedicated to resolving two fun-damental geometric discrepancies inherent to uncalibrated 360 ◦ generation. 

Projective Variance. When an uncalibrated perspective image Ip is projected onto an Equirectangular Projection (ERP) grid, the mapping of 3D directional rays is governed by the camera’s arbitrary rotation matrix R. If R contains unconstrained pitch and roll, straight architectural lines in the 3D world are warped into severe sinusoidal curves on the 2D canvas. This non-linear distortion fundamentally destabilizes the latent conditioning of standard Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs), which implicitly rely on Manhattan-world structural priors (e.g., straight walls, flat horizons) to synthesize coherent geometry. To resolve this, we reformulate the generation target around a Canonical Viewing Space , denoted as C. Rather than forcing the diffusion model to im-plicitly invert an arbitrary R, we define C as a strictly gravity-aligned spherical Gimbal360 7

coordinate system where the camera’s pitch and roll are explicitly zeroed out, forcing the environmental vanishing line to coincide perfectly with the ERP equa-tor. Mathematically, we seek a proxy transformation Talign that autonomously warps the input features directly into C:

zcanonical = Talign (zref )

Topological Severing. The ERP format inherently spans the azimuthal angle 

θ ∈ [−π, π ]. This dictates that the left ( θ = −π) and right ( θ = π) boundaries are physically adjacent, forming a continuous S1 periodic manifold. However, standard latent generative pipelines utilize zero-padding during latent compres-sion and absolute positional encodings in DiT generation, which mathematically sever this S1 periodic boundary and induce visible high-frequency seams. To mitigate this, the generative process conditioned on zcanonical is governed by strict Topological Equivariance, ensuring the network natively respects the continuous topology of the manifold. The remainder of our methodology details how we realize this unified paradigm: Sec. 3.3 introduces the Differentiable Auto-Leveling module to estimate Talign ,and Sec. 3.4 formulates the topologically equivariant diffusion process. 

3.3 Differentiable Auto-Leveling 

As established, our framework requires a proxy transformation Talign to warp the unposed input latent zref into the Canonical Viewing Space C. While one could naively attempt to train an unconstrained Spatial Transformer Network [19] end-to-end with the latent diffusion loss, the diffusion objective LLDM is heavily dominated by high-frequency, pixel-level noise variations. Backpropagating these chaotic gradients directly into an unconstrained spatial warper induces non-rigid, “jelly-like” deformations rather than the required global camera rotation. To circumvent this, we introduce a Differentiable Auto-Leveling module that explicitly disentangles dense feature tracking from rigid geometric alignment. Given the perspective input Ip, we employ a SegNeXt [13] architecture with a lightweight adapter to predict a dense 2D correspondence field Pdense ∈ RHp×Wp×2,which represents the pixel-wise shift required to align the image with a gravity-leveled prior. Crucially, rather than warping the features directly using Pdense , we enforce a strict multi-view geometry bottleneck via a differentiable parameter-search soft-argmin solver [23]. The soft-argmin solver collapses the high-dimensional dense flow into a low-dimensional rigid transformation. This solver calculates the optimal proxy transformation that minimizes the global reprojection error of the predicted dense flow against a rigid spherical camera model. The input latent is then warped into the canonical space using this mathematically rigid projection ˆR:

zcanonical = W(zref , ˆR)

where W denotes a differentiable spherical grid sampling operation. 8 Lu et al. 

This specific architectural design is the key to stable end-to-end training. By forcing the dense correspondence field to pass through the soft-argmin bottle-neck, the Jacobian of the rigid projection inherently acts as a structural gradient filter during backpropagation. Because the rigid transformation manifold is ex-tremely low-rank, the Jacobian perfectly projects the chaotic, uncorrelated gra-dients into a subspace of globally coherent, rigid transformation updates. This strictly prohibits the network from learning localized “jelly” warps, forcing it instead to autonomously align the features with the horizontal equator of the Canonical Viewing Space. 

3.4 Topologically Equivariant Generation 

Having successfully mapped the perspective features into the Canonical View-ing Space C, the remaining challenge is to synthesize the outpainted panorama without introducing spatial discontinuities. As identified in Sec. 3.2, standard generative pipelines implicitly assume a bounded Euclidean ( R2) plane. Specif-ically, the zero-padding operations in standard VAE latent compression inject artificial boundary conditions at the left and right edges of the latent grid. This mathematically severs the continuous S1 periodic manifold, manifesting as visi-ble high-frequency seams in the generated output. To natively synthesize a seamless omnidirectional environment, we explicitly enforce Topological Equivariance across both the compression and generation stages of our framework. 

Circular Latent Encoding. We eliminate the Euclidean boundary assump-tion at the foundational spatial compression stage. We replace all horizontal zero-padding operations within the pre-trained Variational Autoencoder (VAE) encoder and decoder with circular padding. This topological structural modifi-cation ensures that the convolutional receptive fields naturally wrap around the azimuthal boundary. 

Siamese Shift-Equivariant Generation. While circular encoding provides the structural pathway for continuous feature propagation, the network must be explicitly penalized for boundary-inconsistent generative logic. To formally guarantee S1 continuity, we introduce a Siamese shift-equivariance training ob-jective. ## 4 The Horizon360 Dataset 

The success of the Gimbal360 framework relies entirely on the quality of the structural prior provided by the Canonical Viewing Space C. While several omnidirectional datasets exist (e.g., SUN360 [50], Matterport3D [4]), they fre-quently contain in-the-wild camera tilts, uncalibrated rolls, and inconsistent hori-zon lines. Training a generative prior on unrectified data fundamentally under-mines the R2 structural assumptions of latent diffusion models. To substantiate our framework, we introduce Horizon360 , a large-scale, strictly canonicalized plenoptic dataset. 

Data Curation and Rigorous Canonicalization. Horizon360 comprises 20k high-fidelity indoor and outdoor panoramic environments sourced from es-tablished open datasets [4, 10, 24, 52], web collections [35], and custom renders via Unreal Engine (UE). To guarantee the canonical properties of C, every raw panorama undergoes a rigorous geometric rectification pipeline. Building upon foundational omnidirectional geometric principles [57, 62], we adapt the vanishing point and zenith alignment strategy [20, 38, 39] to explicitly zero out the original camera pitch and roll for every scene. This mathematical rotation ensures that the environmental vanishing line strictly coincides with the ERP equator and all physical plumb lines remain perfectly vertical. 10 Lu et al. 

Realistic Perspective Sampling. To train our Differentiable Auto-Leveling module and canonicalized omnidirectional generator, we deploy a mixed sam-pling strategy that dramatically improves real-world robustness over standard uniform cropping [21, 43, 44]. 

Variable Intrinsics. Unlike prior works that assume a fixed horizontal Field-of-View (FOV), we adopt a vertical FOV ( θv ) and aspect ratio ( r) driven ap-proach. This simulates diverse physical camera sensors (from wide-angle to tele-photo) by dynamically calculating the effective focal lengths:

## 5 Experiment 

5.1 Experimental Settings Implementation Details. Our model is built upon Flux.1-fill-dev [25] and fine-tuned on 8 GPUs for 10,000 steps with batchsize of 8 and a constant learning rate of 1 × 10 −4 using the AdamW optimizer ( β1 = 0 .9, β2 = 0 .999 , weight-decay= 0.0001 ). The training data comprises 20,000 panoramic samples from Horizon360, each of which produces 3 perspective variations. 

Evaluation Data and Metrics. For evaluation, we construct two held-out test sets: 500 indoor panoramas randomly sampled from Structured3D [61] and 500 outdoor panoramas from CVRG-Pano [33], totaling 1,000 test images for comprehensive benchmarking. We adopt FID [15], KID [2], and FAED [55] to assess visual quality. Additionally, CLIPScore (CS) [14] is employed to measure text-image alignment. ## 6 Conclusion 

We presented Gimbal360, a framework for completing 360 ◦ panoramas from a single unposed perspective image by explicitly addressing the geometric and topological mismatch between Euclidean diffusion priors and spherical panora-mas. ## A Extended Experimental Setups 

A.1 Training Details 

To train Gimbal360 effectively while managing the memory footprint of the mas-sive Flux.1-fill-dev backbone [25], we employ Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) [16] exclusively on the attention matrices (Query, Key, Value) and the feed-forward networks of the frozen base transformer blocks. We configure the LoRA rank to 

r = 64 and the scaling alpha to α = 64 . The target ground-truth panoramas are processed at an equirectangular resolution of 960 × 1920 .Throughout the training process, the pre-trained Variational Autoencoder (VAE) weights are strictly frozen to preserve the stability of the pre-trained la-tent distribution. The S1 continuity is enforced purely by overriding the VAE’s horizontal zero-padding layers to circular padding during the forward pass, mean-ing no VAE fine-tuning is required. The complete end-to-end objective for Gimbal360 jointly minimizes the foun-dational diffusion loss, the topological Siamese regularizer, and the geometric flow anchor. To balance the gradient magnitudes from these disparate objec-tives, we empirically scale the auxiliary losses. The topological weight is set to 

λshif t = 0 .5, and the geometric flow weight is set to λf low = 0 .1.

A.2 Inference Details 

During inference, we utilize the Euler sampler for 30 denoising steps with a Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) scale of 30.0. To leverage our learned shift-equivariance during the diffusion generation process, at each denoising step t, we apply a random horizontal circular shift δt to the latent representation. Instead of immediately reversing this shift after the noise prediction, we allow the spa-tial frame to continuously translate throughout the generative trajectory. This dynamic, step-by-step shifting ensures that the Euclidean boundary of the la-tent tensor is constantly repositioned across the S1 manifold, preventing any static seam from forming. Once all denoising steps are completed, we apply a single inverse circular shift that compensates for the total accumulated trans-lation to realign the final latent with the canonical conditioning. Finally, the denoised latents are passed through the Circular VAE decoder, natively yielding a mathematically seamless, gravity-aligned 360 ◦ panorama.

## C Implementation of Shift-Equivariance 

In Section 3.4 of the main manuscript, we introduce a Siamese shift-equivariance objective to natively govern the diffusion process. In this section, we first provide the formal mathematical proof that this objective strictly bounds the generative denoising process to the continuous S1 manifold. Subsequently, we detail our highly efficient, memory-saving implementation of this mathematical concept via positional encoding manipulation. 

C.1 Problem Formulation and S1 Topology 

Let the latent representation of the equirectangular panorama be denoted as Z ∈

RC×H×W , where W represents the azimuthal spatial dimension corresponding to the horizontal field-of-view θ ∈ [−π, π ].A true omnidirectional environment exists on a sphere S2, and its cylindrical projection onto the azimuthal axis forms a continuous periodic manifold S1.Mathematically, this enforces a strict boundary condition for any integer k:

Z[: , :, w ] ≡ Z[: , :, (w + k · W ) mod W ]

Standard latent diffusion architectures inherently violate this condition, utilizing bounded spatial priors that treat w = 0 and w = W − 1 as maximally distant across a Euclidean plane. 

C.2 Proof of Equivariance 

To enforce S1 topology, we define a circular translation operator Roll δ : RC×H×W →

RC×H×W , which shifts a tensor along the azimuthal axis W by an integer offset 

δ ∈ [0 , W − 1] :

[Roll δ (Z)] :,:,w = Z:,:,(w−δ) mod W

A neural network ϵθ is defined as equivariant to a transformation group if applying the transformation to the input yields an identically transformed output. In our framework, the combined input state is X = zt ⊕ M ⊕ zcanonical .The Siamese consistency objective minimizes the distance between the shifted prediction and the prediction of the shifted input: 

Lshif t = EX,t,δ

Assuming successful convergence ( Lshif t → 0), the properties of the L2 norm imply: Roll δ (ϵθ (X, t )) = ϵθ (Roll δ (X), t ) almost everywhere. 

Topological Consequence: Because the network’s output shifts perfectly in tandem with any arbitrary circular shift δ, the learned receptive field cannot depend on the absolute horizontal coordinate w. If the network had learned an artificial boundary feature ( e.g ., a seam at w = 0 ), applying Roll δ to the input would shift the image content, but the network would still generate the seam at w = 0 , severely violating the equivariance equality. Thus, the network is mathematically forced to process the latent sequence as a closed topological loop. Gimbal360 23 

C.3 Efficient Implementation via Positional Shifting 

While the Roll δ (·) operator provides the theoretical foundation, physically shift-ing high-dimensional latent concatenations in memory during every forward training pass introduces unnecessary computational overhead. Because Diffusion Transformers (DiTs) like Flux-Fill process images as permutation-invariant sequences of tokens, the spatial structure is injected purely via the Positional Encoding function P (x, y ). Therefore, a physical circular shift of the image tensor is mathematically identical to an inverse circular shift of the posi-tional encodings. We simulate the spatial shift without moving a single latent token in memory by assigning each stationary token the positional encoding of its destination coordinate: 

Pshif ted (x, y ) = P (( x − δ) mod W, y )

During training, the Siamese forward passes are executed as follows: 

– Base Branch: Processes the standard canonical inputs using the default positional coordinate grid: ˆϵbase = ϵθ (X, t, P (x, y )) .

– Shifted Branch: Processes the exact same unshifted latent tensor X in memory, but conditions the attention mechanism using the circularly shifted positional encodings: ˆϵshifted = ϵθ (X, t, P shif ted (x, y )) .Finally, to compute the Siamese Consistency Loss Lshif t , we apply the fast memory-level Roll δ (·) operator exclusively to the output of the base branch, perfectly aligning the two predictions for the L2 penalty without duplicating memory allocation for the inputs.
