## Abstract 

Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have achieved re-markable progress in 2D visual tasks but still exhibit limited physi-cal spatial awareness when processing real-world visual streams. Recently, feed-forward geometric foundation models, which im-plicitly extract geometric priors, have provided a new pathway to address this issue. However, existing geometry-aware MLLMs are predominantly constrained by the paradigm of single deep-layer extraction and input-level fusion. This flattened fusion leads to the loss of local geometric details and causes semantic mismatches in the early layers. To break this bottleneck, we propose GUIDE 

(Geometric Unrolling Insi de MLLM Early-layers), a progressive geometric priors injection framework. GUIDE performs multi-level sampling within the geometric encoder, comprehensively captur-ing multi-granularity features ranging from local edges to global topologies. Subsequently, we rigorously align and fuse these multi-level geometric priors step-by-step with the early layers of the MLLM. Building upon the injection of multi-granularity geometric information, this design guides the model to progressively learn the 2D-to-3D transitional process. Furthermore, we introduce a context-aware gating that enables the model to fetch requisite spatial cues based on current semantics, thereby maximizing the utilization efficiency of spatial priors and effectively suppressing redundant geometric noise. Extensive experiments demonstrate that GUIDE significantly outperforms existing baselines on multiple complex spatial reasoning and perception tasks, establishing a novel para-digm for integrating 3D geometric priors into large models.

## 1 Introduction 

Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have achieved ground-breaking progress in 2D visual understanding and open-ended se-mantic dialogue. However, the real physical world is intrinsically three-dimensional. Endowing artificial intelligence with profound 3D spatial perception capabilities is an indispensable cornerstone 

> arXiv:2604.05695v1 [cs.CV] 7 Apr 2026

for realizing cutting-edge applications, such as Embodied AI, Au-tonomous Driving, and Augmented Reality (AR) Navigation. In these tasks, which demand rigorous physical interaction, models must possess highly precise spatial reasoning capabilities. Neverthe-less, despite their exceptional performance in macroscopic semantic abstraction, existing MLLMs often exhibit limited physical spatial awareness when confronted with monocular RGB visual streams— the most accessible sensory input in the real world. They notably struggle with absolute physical metrics, complex instance-level occlusion relationships, and macroscopic scene layouts. To over-come this bottleneck, early studies attempted to directly introduce explicit 3D data (e.g., point clouds, depth maps, or 3D meshes) as additional inputs to provide spatial representations for the models. Although these methods excel when high-quality geometric data is available, their heavy reliance on depth sensors or time-consuming offline 3D reconstruction pipelines makes it difficult to scale them to real-world videos. Recently, the rapid development of feed-forward visual geometry foundation models (e.g., DUSt3R [ 38 ], VGGT [ 37 ]) has broken the hardware barrier of 3D perception. These models dispense with camera calibration, implicitly decoding dense 3D physical features and relative poses via a single forward pass. This has significantly catalyzed the emergence of geometry-aware MLLMs. Although ex-isting methods have explored diverse approaches for feature fusion— for instance, VLM-3R [ 16 ] and Spatial-MLLM [ 39 ] employ cross-attention mechanisms, VGLLM [ 50 ] performs patch-wise residual addition, and SpaceMind [ 49 ] introduces camera tokens as mod-ulation conditions—they are predominantly constrained by the architectural paradigm of Input-Level Fusion and Deep-Layer Extraction , as shown in Figure 1A. Specifically, these methods typ-ically treat the 3D encoder as a black box, extracting features from only its final or penultimate layer, and perform a one-time early mixing prior to the LLM backbone. We argue that this flattened fusion approach harbors two fundamental architectural flaws: First, Deep-Layer Smoothing inevitably dilutes crucial local geometric details (e.g., depth discontinuities and occlusion bound-aries) in the deep latent space. As evidenced by VGGT’s reliance on shallow and mid-level features for dense prediction, isolated deep features cannot independently support fine-grained 3D repre-sentations. Second, the Single-Shot Injection Bottleneck exac-erbates this issue. Geometric encoding is inherently a progressive “Geometric Lifting” from 2D textures to 3D macroscopic topolo-gies. Restricting feature injection exclusively to the LLM entrance prevents on-demand access to multi-granularity cues and fails to align the LLM with this 2D-to-3D evolutionary process, frequently triggering early-layer cross-modal semantic mismatches. To fundamentally overcome the aforementioned input-level bot-tlenecks and the loss of local features, this paper innovatively proposes a progressive 3D spatial injection framework, GUIDE 

(Geometric Unrolling Insi de MLLM Early-layers), while preserv-ing the macroscopic geometric anchoring at the input end. As the name implies, this framework utilizes geometric priors as a “guide,” transitioning the integration paradigm of 3D information from a single-shot static concatenation outside the LLM backbone to a progressive layer-wise unrolling inside it, as shown in Figure 1B. This constructs a coarse-to-fine geometric cognitive scaffolding for the MLLM. Specifically, at the feature extraction end, we delve into the core backbone of the feed-forward geometric encoder. Through uniform multi-level sampling , we comprehensively capture multi-granularity geometric features ranging from local edges to global topologies, effectively recovering the high-frequency spatial details lost due to deep-layer smoothing. At the feature fusion end, we rigorously align these multi-level geometric priors step-by-step with the early perception layers of the MLLM. Through this design, GUIDE perfectly conforms to the internal hierarchical representational laws of the model. It not only injects rich, multi-granularity geometric representations into the LLM but also guides the model to progressively internalize the 2D-to-3D transition process. Furthermore, to ensure the smooth-ness and noise resistance of cross-modal injection, we introduce a 

Context-Aware Gating mechanism. This mechanism learns gat-ing signals based on the hidden states of the LLM to adaptively modulate the injection strength of geometric features at each layer, endowing the model with the capability of on-demand fetching of spatial cues at different granularities. This enables the model to precisely extract the specific geometric cues it truly needs at each reasoning step, thereby maximizing the utilization efficiency of spatial priors and mitigating redundant geometric noise.

• Multi-Granularity Alignment and Context-Aware Gat-ing: We perform multi-level sampling within the geometric encoder to preserve local details. Early works such as CLIP [ 31 ] and ALIGN [ 22 ] con-structed powerful joint image-text representations via contrastive learning. Recently, open-source models based on instruction tuning [ 2] [ 28 ] [ 13 ] have further enhanced the capabilities of open-ended visual question answer-ing (VQA) and temporal video understanding. However, recent benchmarks (e.g., VSI-Bench [ 41 ]) reveal that existing MLLMs still struggle with complex spatial reasoning. ## 2.2 MLLMs for Spatial Intelligence 

To overcome the spatial reasoning bottleneck of current multi-modal large language models, researchers have explored various approaches to endow MLLMs with 3D perception capabilities. One primary line of work directly incorporates explicit 3D rep-resentations, such as point clouds, RGB-D scans, or BEV images [ 8,30 , 45 , 51 ]. While these methods achieve exceptional 3D grounding and reasoning, their heavy reliance on hardware depth sensors or time-consuming offline 3D reconstruction fundamentally limits their scalability to massive, unconstrained monocular videos in the real world. To break free from the reliance on hardware and complex recon-struction frameworks, another research line has shifted towards implicit enhancement methods based on a pure RGB setting. These studies [ 4 , 29 , 32 , 36 , 40 , 44 , 46 ] attempt to implicitly elicit the spa-tial understanding capabilities of the models without introducing additional 3D encoders, either by massively scaling up synthetic datasets or by introducing advanced training strategies (e.g., the re-inforcement learning-based SpatialLadder [ 25 ]). However, although the scaling of data and the optimization of training strategies indeed bring improvements in generalization performance, these methods still struggle to break through their performance bottlenecks when facing rigorous geometric measurement and complex spatial rea-soning tasks, primarily due to the inherent lack of real 3D physical inductive bias. 

## 2.3 Geometry-Aware MLLMs 

To effectively integrate 3D priors without relying on external sen-sors, recent research has increasingly focused on geometry-aware MLLMs. This trajectory greatly benefits from the rapid develop-ment of feed-forward visual geometry models (e.g., DUSt3R [ 38 ], MASt3R [ 23 ], VGGT [ 37 ]). Without requiring explicit camera cal-ibration, these models can infer pixel-level 3D point maps and camera parameters from image pairs or short videos via a single forward pass, providing plug-and-play geometric and viewpoint tokens for monocular videos. Currently, mainstream methods in this domain prevalently opt to fuse 3D geometric features with 2D semantic visual features prior to their entry into the LLM backbone. For instance, VLM-3R [ 16 ]introduces a cross-attention mechanism at the input layer, utilizing 2D visual tokens as queries to dynamically aggregate geometric and camera tokens. Spatial-MLLM [39] and VGLLM [50] utilize VGGT to extract geometric features from multi-view image sequences, which are then injected into the LLM input space via a lightweight MLP or residual addition. Subsequently, SpaceMind [ 49 ] designs a Camera-Guided Modality Fusion (CGMF) module to actively mod-ulate and gate spatial tokens using camera tokens prior to the LLM computation. However, these single-shot fusion paradigm at the input level forces the LLM to internalize complex global geometric information entirely within its initial layer, inherently restricting the model’s continuous perception of spatial priors. In contrast, our method discards this static input-level concatenation. By pro-gressively injecting multi-granularity geometric features into the internal layers of the LLM, we smoothly guide the model in a coarse-to-fine manner to establish a robust comprehension of 3D spatial relationships. 

## 3 The Proposed Method 3.1 Overall Architecture 

In this section, we detail the progressive geometric prior injection framework, GUIDE . By injecting multi-granularity spatial geomet-ric information into the early layers of the MLLM, this framework aims to guide the model to progressively learn the transformation process from 2D pixel textures to 3D physical representations. As illustrated in Figure 2, given an input image sequence and a text prompt, the model first extracts dual-stream representations via a 2D visual encoder and a parallel geometric encoder, followed by strict spatial grid alignment. During the cross-modal fusion phase, rather than relying solely on conventional flat injection paradigms at the input level, GUIDE step-wise injects multi-granularity geo-metric features into the early layers of the LLM, dynamically mod-ulated by a dual context-aware gating mechanism. Next, we detail the dual-stream feature extraction and spatial alignment in Sec. 3.2, followed by the step-by-step alignment fusion and context-aware gating mechanisms in Sec. 3.3.

## 4 Experiments 

In this section, we comprehensively evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed GUIDE framework in progressively injecting 3D geomet-ric priors into MLLMs. The remainder of this section is organized as follows. Section 4.1 details the experimental setup, evaluation metrics, and implementation details. Section 4.2 presents the perfor-mance of GUIDE on spatial reasoning tasks. Section 4.3 explores the 3D perception capabilities of the proposed framework. Section 4.4 provides comprehensive ablation studies to deeply analyze the in-dependent contributions and synergistic effects of the progressive injection depth and the dual context-aware gating mechanism. 

## 4.1 Implementation and Training Details 

Our models GUIDE-9B and GUIDE-5B are built upon two scales of Qwen3-VL (4B and 8B) [ 2 ] and integrate VGGT-1B [ 37 ] as the 3D geometry encoder. Specifically, following our progressive unrolling design, we extract multi-granularity geometric features from the 8th, 11th, 14th, 17th, 20th, and 23rd layers of the VGGT encoder and inject them sequentially into the first 6 layers of the LLM. The training process employs the Adam optimizer with a global batch size of 64 and a warmup ratio of 0.03. During the warmup phase, the learning rate is gradually increased to 1 × 10 −5 before linearly decaying to 0. Throughout the training phase, to preserve the robust pre-trained representation capabilities and constrain computational overhead, the 2D visual encoder of the MLLM, the integrated 3D geometry encoder, and the initial multimodal connector are kept strictly frozen. All experiments are conducted on 8 NVIDIA A100 (80GB) GPUs. For the GUIDE-5B and GUIDE-9B models, training for the spatial reasoning task took approximately 14 and 85 hours, respectively. Meanwhile, for the 3D scene understanding task, we exclusively trained the GUIDE-9B model, which required 37 hours. 

## 4.2 Spatial Reasoning 

Benchmark and Evaluation Metrics. For the core spatial rea-soning task, we adopt VSI-Bench [41 ] as our primary evaluation benchmark. It covers a broad spectrum of multimodal spatial geo-metric question-answering scenarios, ranging from relative direc-tion judgment to absolute distance estimation. To further evaluate the cross-benchmark generalization of our method, we additionally report results on MMSI-Bench [ 43 ], ViewSpatial [24 ], and CV-Bench [54 ]. Following the standard evaluation protocols of these benchmarks, we report Accuracy for multiple-choice tasks, while for numerical answer tasks in VSI-Bench, we use Mean Relative Accuracy (MRA). 

Training Data Construction. We devise tailored data sampling strategies for models of different scales. For the GUIDE-5B model, following the baseline setup of VG LLM [ 50 ], the training set con-sists of a mixture of 234K spatial data sampled from SPAR-7M [ 46 ]and the LLaVA-Hound split of LLaVA-Video-178K [ 48 ]. When pro-cessing video data, we uniformly sample 8 frames per video to form the image sequence. For the GUIDE-9B model, we aim to further elicit its deep geometric reasoning potential. Therefore, we augment the aforementioned data by additionally incorporating the VSI-Bench training set provided by VLM-3R [ 16 ]. ## 4.3 Spatial Understanding 

Benchmarks and Evaluation Metrics. To validate the versatility and generalization capabilities of the GUIDE framework on tradi-tional 3D scene understanding tasks, we conduct comprehensive evaluations on two classic benchmarks: 3D Visual Grounding and 3D Dense Captioning. 

• 3D Visual Grounding (ScanRefer [ 5 ]): Following the eval-uation protocols established by SPAR [ 46 ] and VG LLM [ 50 ], we reformulate this task as 3D spatial-temporal video ground-ing. The model is required to simultaneously localize the target object’s bounding box in the camera coordinate sys-tem and predict its corresponding frame index. For evalua-tion, we report the accuracy at intersection-over-union (IoU) thresholds of 0.25 and 0.5 (Acc@0.25 and Acc@0.5). 

• 3D Dense Captioning (Scan2Cap [ 12 ]): This task requires the model to generate descriptive text for various objects within a 3D scene. Following previous conventions, we prompt the model to generate captions conditioned on the object center coordinates. To better leverage the injected visual geo-metric features, all coordinates are uniformly transformed to the coordinate system of the initial frame. We evaluate the generation quality using CIDEr (C), BLEU-4 (B-4), METEOR (M), and ROUGE (R) scores. The “@0.5” suffix indicates that these metrics are computed exclusively for objects detected with an IoU of 0.5 or higher against the ground truth. 

Training Data Construction. To ensure the model comprehen-sively learns the spatial-temporal and geometric correlations within 3D environments, our training corpus is constructed by amalgamat-ing data from three sources: the ScanRefer dataset, the Scan2Cap dataset, and the 3D Video Object Detection dataset proposed in VG-LLM [ 50 ]. For video data processing across all aforementioned scenes, we adopt a consistent temporal sampling strategy, uniformly extracting 32 frames per video to form the visual input sequence for the model.

However, when the injection depth is further extended to 9 and 24 layers, the performance drops to 51.1 and 51.6, respectively. We hypothesize that this is because the deep net-works of MLLMs are primarily responsible for processing highly abstract, open-ended semantic logic. Over-introducing low-level physical geometric priors into these deep layers instead triggers severe cross-modal semantic mismatches. Consequently, we adopt 6 layers as the optimal default early-injection depth for GUIDE. 

Efficacy of Dual Context-Aware Gating. The data in Table 5 strongly corroborates the absolute necessity of introducing the dual gating mechanism. Observing the experimental groups with the no-gating configuration (all marked with ✗), direct and brute-force injection at the first 6, 9, and 24 layers causes the performance to plummet to 50.3, 48.0, and 47.9, respectively, falling significantly below the input-level fusion baseline. This negative interference fully demonstrates that unfiltered, redundant geometric noise se-verely disrupts the original pre-trained semantic space of the LLM. Conversely, when we progressively introduce the context-aware gating components, the model exhibits remarkable robustness and achieves on-demand fetching of spatial information. Taking the optimal 6-layer injection as an example, integrating only the token-wise semantic gate ( 𝐺 𝑠𝑒𝑚 ) recovers the performance from 50.3 to 52.7, proving its efficacy in suppressing local redundant geometric noise. Furthermore, by superimposing the layer-wise global gate (𝐺 𝑔𝑙𝑜 ), the model can dynamically modulate the overall injection strength based on the semantic state of the current layer, enabling a substantial leap in performance to 55.6. The synergistic effect of this dual gating maximizes the utilization efficiency of multi-granularity spatial priors, thoroughly validating our original design intentions at the feature fusion end. 

## 5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we propose GUIDE (Geometric Unrolling Inside MLLM Early-layers), a novel framework designed to overcome the inherent lack of spatial awareness in MLLMs when processing 2D visual streams. To break the structural bottlenecks of traditional single-shot, input-level fusion paradigms, GUIDE progressively un-rolls and injects multi-granularity geometric priors into the early perception layers of the MLLM. By systematically aligning geomet-ric features ranging from local edges to global topologies, we con-struct a coarse-to-fine cognitive scaffolding for the model, smoothly guiding it to internalize the 2D-to-3D physical transition process. Furthermore, our proposed dual context-aware gating mechanism endows the model with the ability to adaptively fetch spatial cues based on the current semantics. This not only maximizes the uti-lization efficiency of geometric priors but also effectively protects the deep, pre-trained semantic space of the LLM, shielding it from catastrophic interference caused by redundant geometric noise. Extensive experiments on complex spatial reasoning and tra-ditional 3D scene understanding benchmarks demonstrate that GUIDE achieves highly competitive performance. Notably, our framework achieves these remarkable results relying solely on monocular 2D image sequences, completely eliminating the de-pendence on expensive explicit 3D inputs such as point clouds or depth maps. Ultimately, GUIDE establishes a highly scalable and effective new paradigm for integrating 3D geometric priors into large language models, paving the way for the next generation of advanced spatial intelligence and Embodied AI applications.
