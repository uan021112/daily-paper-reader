Abstract. We present a generalizable feed-forward Gaussian splatting framework for human 3D reconstruction and real-time animation that op-erates directly on multi-view RGB images and their associated SMPL-X poses. Unlike prior methods that rely on depth supervision, fixed input views, UV map, or repeated feed-forward inference for each target view or pose, our approach predicts, in a canonical pose, a set of 3D Gaus-sian primitives associated with each SMPL-X vertex. One Gaussian is regularized to remain close to the SMPL-X surface, providing a strong geometric prior and stable correspondence to the parametric body model, while an additional small set of unconstrained Gaussians per vertex al-lows the representation to capture geometric structures that deviate from the parametric surface, such as clothing and hair. In contrast to recent approaches such as HumanRAM, which require repeated network infer-ence to synthesize novel poses, our method produces an animatable hu-man representation from a single forward pass; by explicitly associating Gaussian primitives with SMPL-X vertices, the reconstructed model can be efficiently animated via linear blend skinning without further network

# 1 Introduction 

Novel view synthesis (NVS) aims to generate photorealistic renderings of a scene from unseen viewpoints and has seen rapid progress with neural representations such as NeRFs [29] and 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) [17]. Extending NVS to humans introduces additional challenges due to articulated motion and com-plex non-rigid deformations. Beyond reconstructing geometry and appearance from sparse observations, practical systems must support controllable anima-tion under unseen poses while remaining computationally efficient. In this work, we focus on the sparse-view setting, where only a small set of input views of a human—captured under varying poses—is available, reflecting realistic capture scenarios outside controlled multi-view environments. Existing approaches address this problem through either optimization-based or feed-forward methods. Optimization-based approaches, such as Animatable Gaussians [26], achieve high-quality animatable human 3D reconstructions using 3DGS but require costly per-subject test-time optimization, limiting scalabil-ity. Recent 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) based feed-forward approaches such as Generalizable Human Gaussian (GHG) [20] and GPS-Gaussian [58] predict Gaussian parameters directly from input images, enabling efficient real-time ren-dering while avoiding per-subject optimization. However, these methods rely on convolutional architectures, which restrict them to a fixed number of input views and assume a fixed human pose across the inputs, limiting their applicability in realistic capture scenarios where humans naturally appear under varying poses and viewpoints. Effectively integrating information from multiple input views is critical for accurate human reconstruction and novel view synthesis, particularly in sparse-view settings where only limited observations are available. Recent works there-fore explore network architectures designed to better aggregate multi-view fea-tures. LVSM [16] introduces transformer-based Large Reconstruction Models (LRMs), extending image-conditioned radiance fields with a powerful trans-former architecture that integrates multi-view information for generalizable novel view synthesis. Building on this formulation, HumanRAM [55] adapts the LRM framework to humans by incorporating SMPL-X [34] pose priors, enabling syn-thesis under both novel viewpoints and novel poses. While HumanRAM achieves strong reconstruction and animation quality, it formulates animation as per-frame image generation, requiring a full network forward pass for each target pose and camera viewpoint. This repeated inference significantly increases com-putational cost and limits real-time applications. These limitations motivate Real-Time Human Animation 3

the need for a framework that combines the strong generalization capability of transformer-based LRMs with the efficiency of explicit 3D representations, en-abling reusable human assets that can be efficiently animated without repeated network inference. In this paper, we propose HumanGS, a unified feed-forward framework that explicitly decouples reconstruction from animation through a canonical 3D Gaus-sian representation. Given sparse multi-view images and corresponding SMPL-X [34] parameters, our transformer-based LRM predicts 3DGS parameters aligned with SMPL-X [34] vertices in a canonical T-pose. The resulting Gaussian asset can be efficiently animated via Linear Blend Skinning and rendered using Gaus-sian rasterization, enabling synthesis of arbitrary poses and viewpoints with-out repeated network inference. Experiments on THuman 2.1, THuman 4.0, and AvatarReX demonstrate that HumanGS achieves reconstruction and an-imation quality comparable to state-of-the-art feed-forward and optimization-based methods, while substantially reducing modeling and per-frame synthesis time. Moreover, despite being trained only on fixed-pose reconstruction data, HumanGS generalizes effectively to unseen subjects and large pose variations across datasets. Our contributions are threefold: 

– We introduce HumanGS, a novel feed-forward LRM-based framework that predicts an explicit canonical 3D Gaussian human representation for both reconstruction and animation. 

– We decouple representation learning from rendering, enabling real-time an-imation via Linear Blend Skinning without repeated network inference. 

– We demonstrate competitive reconstruction and animation quality with strong cross-dataset generalization, while significantly improving modeling and syn-thesis efficiency over prior methods. 

# 2 Related Work 

Human 3D Reconstruction. Human 3D reconstruction has been widely stud-ied for applications such as virtual avatars, telepresence, and digital content cre-ation. Early methods relied on dense multi-view capture systems in controlled environments to reconstruct high-fidelity geometry and appearance [5,11]. While these approaches achieve highly accurate reconstructions, they require special-ized hardware and dense camera setups, which limits scalability and practical deployment. Recent advances in neural rendering, particularly Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) [29], have enabled photorealistic novel view synthesis from posed im-ages. A large body of work [10, 14, 15, 25, 32, 35, 36, 44, 47, 50, 52, 54] has ex-tended NeRF-based representations for human reconstruction (avatar genera-tion), often incorporating parametric human priors such as SMPL or 3D skele-tons [28, 35, 37, 49, 50, 56] to model articulation and motion. Despite their strong reconstruction quality, NeRF-based approaches typically require expensive per-scene optimization and slow rendering, making them unsuitable for real-time 4 Devdoot Chatterjee, Zakaria Laskar, and C.V. Jawahar 

applications. To improve efficiency, several works [9, 14] explored alternative neural scene representations such as Instant-NGP (iNGP) [31], with [9] or with-out pose conditioning [14]. More recently, 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) [17] has emerged as an efficient representation enabling fast training and real-time rendering. Multiple methods extend 3DGS to human reconstruction and anima-tion [13, 18, 24, 26, 30, 33, 41, 43], often integrating SMPL-based priors to regular-ize geometry and support pose-driven deformation. Among these, Animatable Gaussians [26] achieve high-quality reconstructions with pose-dependent anima-tion through optimization-based pipelines. Despite impressive real-time render-ing speed, existing 3DGS-based methods require minutes to hours of per-human subject optimization (modeling time), which limits scalability. To address this limitation, recent works explore generalizable feed-forward reconstruction models that directly predict scene representations in a single for-ward pass, reducing modeling time to seconds. Our proposed HumanGS follows this direction and enables fast reconstruction and real-time animation through an explicit canonical Gaussian representation. 

Generalizable Feed-Forward Human Reconstruction. Early approaches learn image-conditioned radiance fields using pixel-aligned features [42, 46, 53] or cost volumes [1, 48] extracted from multi-view observations. These ideas have been extended to human reconstruction [2–4, 7, 8, 21, 22, 57], enabling gener-alization across subjects without per-subject training. Notably, these image-conditioned radiance fields use SMPL as a geometric prior for robust aggregation of multi-view image features. More recently, transformer-based Large Recon-struction Models (LRMs) [12] have demonstrated strong performance in gener-alizable novel view synthesis. LVSM [16] demonstrates the use of LRM for novel view synthesis. HumanRAM [55] adapts LRM-based LVSM architecture for hu-man reconstruction and animation by also conditioning on SMPL parameters, enabling synthesis under novel poses and viewpoints. While the above methods, especially HumanRAM achieves good reconstruction quality, animation is for-mulated as per-frame image generation, requiring repeated network inference for each target view and pose. Several feed-forward approaches instead predict 3D Gaussian representa-tions directly from images. These methods regress pixel-aligned [58] or UV map-aligned [19] 3DGS parameters. However, these existing methods are based on convolutional architectures and therefore tied to a fixed number of input views, while also assuming fixed-pose inputs. In contrast, HumanGS combines transformer-based LRMs with explicit 3D Gaussian representations for animat-able human reconstruction. Our transformer-based architecture supports flexible multi-view inputs and variable pose conditioning, while predicting canonical (T-pose) SMPL-vertex-aligned Gaussian parameters. This canonical representation enables efficient animation through LBS and Gaussian rasterization, avoiding repeated network inference during rendering. Real-Time Human Animation 5

# 3 Method 

We propose HumanGS, a unified feed-forward framework for high-fidelity 3D human reconstruction and real-time animation (see Figure 2). Unlike previous state-of-the-art methods like HumanRAM [55] which formulate animation as a 2D image-to-image translation task conditioned on 3D priors, our approach explicitly predicts a canonical 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) asset. This as-set is intrinsically animatable via Linear Blend Skinning (LBS), decoupling the heavy feature extraction process from the rendering loop and enabling real-time performance.

Intermediate Feature Aggregation Module. As depicted in 2, our pipeline converts transformer-derived “Spatial Tokens” into a dense “2D Feature Map”. Standard vision transformers often over-smooth high-frequency local details if relying exclusively on their terminal layer. To prevent this and preserve the fine-grained information necessary for complex texture synthesis, our transformation explicitly aggregates intermediate spatial representations from multiple depths. This specific sub-module, bridging the spatial tokens to the final feature map, is detailed in Figure 3. Given a transformer encoder with L layers, we extract feature tokens at four uniformly spaced intervals: layers L/ 4, L/ 2, 3L/ 4, and L. These extracted 1D tokens are reshaped into 2D spatial feature maps and passed into a hierarchical fusion module. To construct a dense, high-resolution spatial feature map suitable for vertex-aligned sampling, this fused representation undergoes two progressive upsam-pling stages. Each stage employs a 4 × 4 transposed convolution (with a stride of 2 and padding of 1) paired with a refining 3 × 3 convolution. 4.1 State-of-the-art 

We compare our method against five state-of-the-art approaches: i) LVSM [16], a transformer-based Large Reconstruction Model (LRM) designed for static-pose novel-view synthesis from a variable number of multi-view images; ii) Human-RAM [55], which extends the LRM paradigm using SMPL parameters to enable both static reconstruction and animatable novel-pose synthesis; iii) General-izable Human Gaussian (GHG) [19], a CNN-based feed-forward method predicting 3D Gaussians from a fixed number of views and a UV map for static-pose reconstruction; iv) Gaussian Splatting Transformer (GST) [40], our single-image baseline that utilizes a transformer to predict SMPL-vertex-aligned Gaussian parameters; and v) Animatable Gaussians [26], our per-subject op-timization baseline that models animatable humans by optimizing canonical 3D Gaussians articulated via linear blend skinning.

isolate "valid" patches where the ground-truth human foreground overlap ex-ceeds 50%. We then compute the Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS) difference on these isolated patches by subtracting the LPIPS score of our full model from a baseline variant that lacks intermediate feature aggrega-tion. Figure 7 illustrates both the quantitative and qualitative impact of this architectural choice compared to the setup where spatial tokens are composed using features only from the final layer (Transformer Layer L in Fig. 3) on THu-man 2.1 dataset. # 5 Conclusion 

We presented HumanGS, a feed-forward framework for high-fidelity human re-construction and real-time animation. Unlike methods such as HumanRAM that require repeated network inference for each target frame, HumanGS pre-dicts an explicit canonical 3D Gaussian representation, decoupling representa-tion learning from rendering. This enables efficient animation via Linear Blend Skinning and rasterization, resulting in substantially faster per-frame synthesis. Compared to optimization-based Animatable Gaussians, our approach reduces subject-specific modeling time from minutes to seconds. Extensive evaluations on THuman 2.1, THuman 4.0, and AvatarReX show that HumanGS achieves reconstruction and animation quality comparable to state-of-the-art feed-forward and optimization-based methods while significantly improving efficiency. Despite being trained only on fixed-pose reconstruction data, HumanGS generalizes effectively to unseen subjects and unseen pose and camera view variations across datasets. These results demonstrate that explicit canonical Gaussian representations provide a scalable and practical solution for real-time human avatar generation. Future work will investigate improved tem-poral modeling for complex non-rigid deformations.

# S1 Experiment Details 

Evaluation Setup. To rigorously evaluate our method, we conduct experiments across three diverse datasets. For THuman 2.1 [51], we partition the data into 2,300 subjects for training and 145 subjects for testing. To evaluate cross-dataset generalization and animation fidelity, we utilize the entirety of both the THuman 4.0 [59] and AvatarReX [60] datasets exclusively for testing. This includes all 3 subjects from THuman 4.0 and all 4 subjects from AvatarReX. For our standard reconstruction and animation experiments, we operate under a sparse four-view (N = 4 ) setting. To ensure fair and consistent comparisons against all baseline methods, the specific input context views and the target novel evaluation views are strictly fixed across all experiments. For the ablation study examining the impact of the global token across vary-ing view counts ( N ∈ { 1, 2, 4, 6}), we introduce a modified THuman 2.1 test set designed to stress-test rendering fidelity. To demand finer high-frequency detail synthesis, subjects in this set are positioned closer to the cameras. Specifically, the camera distance is fixed at twice the bounding sphere radius of the sub-ject, whereas our standard evaluation protocol uniformly samples this distance multiplier between 1.0 and 1.75 .

Data Pre-processing. Ground truth 3D scans are first semantically aligned to a consistent orientation using the corresponding SMPL-X joint locations. We then render 60 multi-view images per subject at a resolution of 512 × 512 . The camera viewpoints are distributed by uniformly sampling the azimuth from 0◦

to 360 ◦ and the elevation from −5◦ to 15 ◦, with the camera-to-subject distance dynamically scaled based on the bounding sphere radius of the aligned mesh. 

Training Details. The proposed model is trained on a single 48GB RTX 6000 GPU. To accelerate convergence and efficiently manage memory, we employ a progressive resolution training strategy. The network is initially pre-trained from scratch on the THuman 2.1 dataset at a lower resolution of 256 × 256 for 60 , 000 

iterations with a batch size of 8, requiring approximately 32 hours. Subsequently, the model is fine-tuned at the target resolution of 512 × 512 for an additional 

10 , 000 iterations with a reduced batch size of 4. Throughout the entire training process, parameters are optimized using AdamW with a base learning rate of 

4 × 10 −4, a cosine learning rate decay scheduler, and a weight decay of 0.05 .
