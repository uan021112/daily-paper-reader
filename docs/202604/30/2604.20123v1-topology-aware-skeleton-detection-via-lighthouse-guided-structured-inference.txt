Abstract —In natural images, object skeletons are used to represent geometric shapes. However, even slight variations in pose or movement can cause noticeable changes in skeleton structure, increasing the difficulty of detecting the skeleton and often resulting in discontinuous skeletons. Existing methods pri-marily focus on point-level skeleton point detection and overlook the importance of structural continuity in recovering complete skeletons. To address this issue, we propose Lighthouse-Skel, a topology-aware skeleton detection method via lighthouse-guided structured inference. Specifically, we introduce a dual-branch collaborative detection framework that jointly learns skeleton confidence field and structural anchors, including endpoints and junction points. The spatial distributions learned by the point branch guide the network to focus on topologically vulnerable regions, which improves the accuracy of skeleton detection. Based on the learned skeleton confidence field, we further propose a lighthouse-guided topology completion strategy, which uses detected junction points and breakpoints as lighthouses to reconnect discontinuous skeleton segments along low-cost paths, thereby improving skeleton continuity and structural integrity. Experimental results on four public datasets demonstrate that the proposed method achieves competitive detection accuracy while substantially improving skeleton connectivity and structural in-tegrity. 

Index Terms —Skeleton Detection; Structured Inference; Minimal-Cost Path Inference; Topology-Aware Skeleton Recon-struction 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

The skeleton is an abstract representation of an object’s shape in an image, embodying the collection of local sym-metries of the object [1]. It is a connected shape formed by a single curve or multiple curves. Due to its simplicity and reconstructability, the skeleton can compress redundant boundary information while preserving the topological struc-ture, demonstrating robustness to scale variations [2]. As a result, skeletons are widely applied in various visual tasks, such as human action recognition [3], road extraction [4], text detection [5], and medical diagnosis [6]. Despite recent progress, robust skeleton detection remains difficult because preserving topology is fundamentally harder than detecting isolated skeleton pixels. CNN-based methods [8] have been extensively studied for skeleton detection [9]. Most existing approaches treat skeleton

For instance, SRN [10] and MSB+ [11] enhance feature represen-tation by combining side outputs from multiple layers. Beyond network architecture design, several studies have focused on improving skeleton representation. DeepFlux [7] converts the single-pixel skeleton into a flux field, where each nearby pixel encodes a unit vector pointing toward its closest skeleton point. BlumNet [12] decomposes skeleton curves into a set of short line segments and reconstructs the skeleton by predicting the coordinates of the two endpoints of each line segment. Pro-Mask [13] reformulates the pixel-wise classification problem as skeleton point detection, estimating the probability that a skeleton point appears at each location. It is worth noting that regardless of whether they focus on network architecture

optimization or representation transformation, these methods fundamentally rely on dense estimation, treating the skeleton as a set of discrete points or line segments, without explicit connectivity reasoning. Consequently, even minor local omis-sions can result in skeleton discontinuity. Although BlumNet alleviates sparsity by representing skeletons with overlapping short segments, this reconstruction strategy often produces locally hollow regions and thicker skeletons. In the skeleton generation process, one endpoint is typically chosen as the starting point, and the remaining endpoints serve as destinations. This skeleton generation follows the safest path, that is, the trajectory of the centers of the inscribed circles [2], as shown in Fig. 1(a). When reaching areas with significant shape variations ( i.e. , skeleton junction points), the paths branch toward other directions until connecting to one endpoint or other junction point. Most methods tend to produce discontinuity at skeleton junctions where the detection is more difficult, as shown in Fig. 1(b) and (c). Thus, the inscribed circle naturally serves as a guiding role during the skeleton generation process. However, in skeleton detection, such guidance does not exist. Networks generally rely only on local features around skeleton points to determine their presence (as in DeepFlux). This independent detection of individual skeleton points ignores the overall continuity of the skeleton, which easily results in a fragmented and dis-continuous skeleton. As shown by the skeleton generation process, skeleton generation can be regarded as a path-search problem. In path search, we typically require a target for guidance. For example, in skeleton generation, the inscribed circle radius constrains the path from deviating. Similarly, in skeleton detection, we also expect such a target to guide the formation of the skeleton. Inspired by this observation, we treat endpoints (breakpoints) and junction points as structural anchors, referred to as “lighthouses”. They can guide the network to focus more on the harder-to-detect junction points, as well as to facilitate the detection and restoration of the skeleton curves, as shown in Fig. 1(d)-(f). We reinterpret skeleton detection as a structured inference problem over a learned skeleton confidence field, and propose a topology-aware skeleton detection method via lighthouse-guided structured inference. We leverage endpoints and junc-tion points as structural anchors (“Lighthouses”) to improve the accuracy of the initial skeleton and the integrity of the final skeleton. Specifically, we adopt a dual-branch collaborative framework that jointly learns dense skeleton confidence field and sparse structural points. The spatial distributions predicted by the point branch serve as structural priors, guiding the skeleton branch during both training and inference to focus on regions with high topological uncertainty, thereby improving the detection accuracy of the initial skeleton derived from the skeleton confidence field. Based on the learned skeleton confidence field, we then construct an energy-aware cost map and identify non-terminal breakpoints from the initial skeleton. Finally, by treating these breakpoints together with detected junction points as structural anchors, we perform global minimal-cost path inference on the learned cost field to reconnect discontinuous initial skeleton, thereby improving the structural integrity and continuity of the final skeleton. The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows: (1) We propose a dual-branch collaborative framework that jointly learns dense skeleton confidence field and sparse struc-tural points (endpoints and junction points), where the point branch serves as a structural prior to improve the detection accuracy of the initial skeleton, especially in regions prone to discontinuity. (2) We propose a lighthouse-guided topology completion strategy that formulates skeleton reconnection as minimal-cost structured inference on a learned skeleton confidence field, where detected junctions and breakpoints act as structural anchors to restore discontinuous skeleton segments. (3) We present Lighthouse-Skel, a topology-aware skeleton detection framework via lighthouse-guided structured infer-ence, which achieves consistent improvements on multiple public benchmarks, particularly in terms of connectivity, struc-tural integrity, and robustness on challenging skeleton struc-tures. II. R ELATED WORK 

A. Skeleton Detection 

In recent years, skeleton detection has been extensively studied. Its core objective is to decide, for each image pixel, whether it belongs to the skeleton, essentially forming abinary classification task. With the rise of deep learning, this research has made substantial progress: Tsogkas et al. [14] used classifiers to assess object symmetry and employed multiple instance learning (MIL) to determine the orientation of symmetric structures; Shen et al. [15] extended this ap-proach to accommodate a broader range of object categories. In contrast to these classification-based methods, Sironi et al. [16] formulated skeleton detection as a regression problem by estimating, for each non-skeleton pixel, the shortest distance to the nearest skeleton point. To obtain more reliable features, many network optimization methods have been proposed, most of which can be regarded as improvements over HED [9]. For example, richer convolutional features (RCF) [17] fuses side outputs from multiple layers for skeleton detection; LMSDS [18] learns scale correlations among side outputs; LSN [19] and AlaLSN [20] treat side outputs as basis vectors to con-struct a skeleton feature space. All these approaches process multi-level side outputs to more effectively combine shallow and deep information. With the adoption of Transformers in computer vision [21], Zhang et al. [12] replaced conventional CNNs with Transformer architectures. Such methods empha-size deeper representations and have shown advantages over CNN-based methods. In addition, alternative skeleton repre-sentations have been explored: unlike the traditional binary representation, DeepFlux [22] models skeletons as a flux field, BlumNet [12] represents skeletons as an interleaved set of short line segments, and ProMask [13] adopts a probabilis-tic representation. However, most existing methods improve representation quality but do not explicitly model topology completion or connectivity-preserving inference. 

B. Path Search 

Skeleton connection can be formulated as a path-linking problem. In early object segmentation tasks, Mortensen et al. [23] employed seed points to guide boundary generation, enabling accurate segmentation through path-based contour tracing. Grady [24] formulates boundary generation as arandom walk probability, where each pixel represents the probability of a random walker starting from that point first reaching the target point. Benmansour et al. [25] proposed a minimal-path approach built on anisotropic metrics and Op-timally Oriented Flux (OOF), synchronously tracking vessel centerlines and boundaries in an augmented spatial–radius domain to achieve precise tubular segmentation. Chen et al. [26] integrated dynamic Riemannian constraints within the minimal-path framework, further improving connectiv-ity and robustness for tubular structures. Liao [27] replaces hand-crafted feature–based minimal path search with a CNN and integrates it with the progressive minimal path method, thereby reducing reliance on costly annotations. Liu et al. [28] combine pre-computed boundary segments with globally optimal minimal paths, using the structural cues from bound-ary segments to impose connectivity constraints and reduce the shortcut problem. The above methods generally rely on external seeds/segments or task-specific priors. In this paper, we use the breakpoints and junction points as ”Lighthouse”, and under their guidance, performs path connection to restore the continuity of the skeleton. III. M ETHOD 

A. Preliminaries 

For any object O in an image, its skeleton S can be represented by a set of skeleton branches L, endpoints E,and junction points J, i.e. , S = {L, E, J }. Endpoints E

are typically located near convex protrusions of the object boundary ∂O . Junction points J are intersection nodes where two skeleton curves meet, commonly corresponding to regions with pronounced variations along the contour of the object. 

B. Overview 

We aim to detect object skeletons from a given RGB image. Specifically, a Transformer-based network jointly predicts a dense skeleton confidence field SP and a set of structural anchors {E, J }. The predicted skeleton confidence field SP

is then refined into a single-pixel-wide initial skeleton S0.For each breakpoint in S0, we identify candidate connection points within a sector-shaped neighborhood centered at the breakpoint. Based on the learned skeleton confidence field SP

and point heatmaps, we construct a path cost map and perform minimal-cost path inference to select the optimal connection point for each breakpoint. By iteratively reconnecting discon-tinuous skeleton branches, the method recovers a continuous, smooth, and topologically consistent single-pixel skeleton, as illustrated in Fig. 2. 

C. Dual-branch Collaborative Skeleton Detection Network 

We propose a dual-branch collaborative skeleton detection network, consisting of a skeleton branch and a point branch, which are designed to detect the object skeleton as well as the endpoints and junction points on the skeleton, respectively. Specifically, given an input RGB image I, we first extract multi-scale features using a Swin-Base backbone [29], which is denoted as FB = {f2, f 3, f 4, f 5}. And then, these features are fed into a Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) [30] to obtain a scale-consistent representation FFPN for the subsequent feature reshaping. In parallel, {f3, f 4, f 5} are forwarded to a Deformable DETR [31] to produce a set of learned query features F ∈ R2Q×C , where Q is the number of queries and 

C is the channel dimension. We split the query features into two parts: skeleton features FS and point features FP . The former is dedicated to estimating a dense skeleton confidence field SP , which serves as the basis for extracting the initial skeleton S0 and constructing the path cost map, while the latter is used to localize endpoints and junction points.

SP , we first process the skeleton features FS with an MLP and reshape them into a Q×H ×W spatial representation using the backbone features FFPN , where H and W represent the height and width of the image, respectively. [0 , 1] 1×H×W . Moreover, we introduce an auxiliary point-level supervision by regressing the coordinates of all skeleton points Psk = {(xsk , y sk )}. D. Lighthouse-Guided Topology Completion Strategy 

The initial skeleton S0 is often not simply connected. The reason is that converting SP into a single-pixel skele-ton typically relies on thresholding and thinning. However, when local confidence values are low, true skeleton pixels may be incorrectly discarded, leading to breaks, as shown in Fig. 3(b). Such pixel-level decisions fail to account for global structural continuity. To address this issue, we propose a Lighthouse-Guided Topology Completion Strategy to repair disconnected skeleton segments. We aim to restore the global skeleton topology by finding the minimum-cost connected paths between high-confidence regions in SP . Specifically, we extract the breakpoints E(S0) of S0 by selecting pixels whose 8-neighborhood degree equals 1. C. Implementation Details 

Our experiments were conducted on a system equipped with one GeForce RTX 4090D GPU, an Intel Core™ i9-14900K processor, and 64 GB of RAM. We employed a cosine learning rate scheduler [37] with an initial learning rate of 2 × 10 −4.The models were trained for 200 epochs, with the exception of the SymPASCAL dataset, for which the training was extended to 300 epochs. During training, we applied extensive data augmentation techniques used in [12]. 

D. Comparison with State-of-the-Art Methods 

Table I shows the performance comparison between the pro-posed method and other state-of-the-art methods across multi-ple public datasets. In the table, Bold and Underline denote the best and second-best performance, respectively. Overall, the proposed method achieves satisfactory and competitive per-formance, demonstrating strong generalization ability across datasets with different structural characteristics and levels of complexity. On SK-LARGE, the proposed method obtains an F-measure of 0.822 , which is very close to the Transformer-based BlumNet ( 0.826 ), indicating that our method remains highly competitive even on large-scale datasets. More impor-tantly, on SK-SMALL, WH-SYMMAX, and SYM-PASCAL, our method consistently and clearly outperforms BlumNet, showing stronger robustness on datasets with more chal-lenging skeleton structures. Specifically, on SK-SMALL, the proposed method surpasses BlumNet by 4.5% and ProMask by 4.2% . On WH-SYMMAX, the proposed method achieves an F-measure of 0.911 , which is 3.9% higher than Blum-Net and 4.1% higher than ProMask. On SYM-PASCAL, the improvement is even more significant: the proposed method outperforms BlumNet by 13 .6% , reaching an F-measure of 

0.592 . These results indicate that the proposed method is particularly effective in handling complex structures, weak responses, and discontinuous skeleton regions. Although the runtime advantage is not particularly significant, the inference speed remains comparable to ProMask and MSB+, suggesting that the performance gain is achieved without introducing excessive computational overhead. The main reason for this improvement is that the detection of endpoints and junction points enables the network to focus more effectively on struc-turally vulnerable regions during both training and inference. Meanwhile, the lighthouse-based topology completion strategy helps generate more continuous skeleton predictions by auto-matically repairing local discontinuities, thereby improving the overall structural integrity and detection accuracy.

To address skeleton discontinuity, we propose the Topology-Aware Skeleton Detection Method via Lighthouse-Guided Structured Inference, inspired by structured path inference. This method employs a dual-branch collaborative detection framework that jointly learns skeletons, endpoints, and junc-tion points. It utilizes the spatial distribution of the points as anchors to guide the network’s attention toward challeng-ing detection regions, such as the junction region. Based on this, we propose a Lighthouse-based minimal-cost path connection strategy, which leverages breakpoints in the initial skeleton and detected junction points to provide guidance, thereby completing the repair of discontinuous skeletons on the skeleton confidence field.
