# Towards Instance Segmentation with Polygon Detection Transformers 

Jiacheng Sun , Jiaqi Lin , Wenlong Hu , Haoyang Li , Xinghong Zhou ,Chenghai Mao , Yan Peng , and Xiaomao Li 

Shanghai University, Shanghai, China 

lixiaomao@shu.edu.cn 

Abstract. One of the bottlenecks for instance segmentation today lies in the conflicting requirements of high-resolution inputs and lightweight, real-time inference. To address this bottleneck, we present a Polygon Detection Transformer (Poly-DETR) to reformulate instance segmenta-tion as sparse vertex regression via Polar Representation, thereby elim-inating the reliance on dense pixel-wise mask prediction. Considering the box-to-polygon reference shift in Detection Transformers, we pro-pose Polar Deformable Attention and Position-Aware Training Scheme to dynamically update supervision and focus attention on boundary cues. Compared with state-of-the-art polar-based methods, Poly-DETR achieves a 4.7 mAP improvement on MS COCO test-dev. Moreover, we construct a parallel mask-based counterpart to support a system-atic comparison between polar and mask representations. Experimen-tal results show that Poly-DETR is more lightweight in high-resolution scenarios, reducing memory consumption by almost half on Cityscapes dataset. Notably, on PanNuke (cell segmentation) and SpaceNet (build-ing footprints) datasets, Poly-DETR surpasses its mask-based counter-part on all metrics, which validates its advantage on regular-shaped in-stances in domain-specific settings. Our implementation can be found at https://github.com/Sun15194/Poly-DETR. 

## 1 Introduction 

The purpose of instance segmentation is to identify foreground objects in images and capture their boundary information. Mainstream methods [1, 6, 8, 12, 19, 28] follow the paradigm of Mask Representation, performing pixel-wise classifica-tion on feature maps to generate binary instance masks. However, with the development of acquisition hardware and imaging technologies, input images for instance segmentation are of increasingly high resolution [36]. In this context, dense pixel-wise classification results in heavy computational overhead and infer-ence latency [20], making it difficult to satisfy the requirements of lightweight and real-time applications. In particular, for instances with regular shapes [11, 29], fine-grained modeling of interior pixels is unnecessary [16]. To address these issues, we present Poly-DETR , a Polygon Detection Transformer built upon Polar Representation [30,31]. As illustrated in Fig. 1(b),

Each embedding is parameterized by a starting point and a fixed number of radial distances, which are then assembled into a bound-ing polygon that approximates the instance contour. Benefiting from the global contextual interaction of Detection Transformer [3] (DETR), starting points can be modeled via direct coordinate regression in this pipeline. In contrast, pre-vious polar-based methods [27, 30, 31] select starting points from hand-crafted feature grids based on classification scores, which inevitably limits the flexibility of Polar Representation. Moreover, since bounding boxes can be regarded as the degenerate cases of Polar Representation with four rays, this paradigm can be implemented on modern DETR-like detectors [38] with a straightforward dimen-sionality expansion of the prediction head, without introducing any additional prediction branch ( e.g ., the blue mask-only branch in Fig. 1(a)). However, despite the structural compatibility, polygon detection is not geo-metrically equivalent to conventional object detection. Specifically, as the pre-dicted starting point shifts, the polygon reference used to supervise object queries changes accordingly. This is in contrast to the box-center notion [25] in Detection Transformers [3], where matched queries always share a fixed reference, i.e ., the center and size of their ground-truth box. To this end, we propose a Position-Aware Training Scheme (PATS ) to dynamically update the supervision ref-erence at each decoder layer, conditioned on the current starting-point location. Furthermore, standard Deformable Attention [38] (DA), originally designed for box regression, becomes misaligned with evolving polygon regions, leading to excessive attention budget on box-edge regions. To address this mismatch, we propose Polar Deformable Attention (Polar-DA ), in which sampling loca-tions at each decoder layer are constructed from fan-shaped grids around current starting points, with learnable offsets scaled by radial distance estimates. Another key aspect of this work is a systematic comparison between polar and mask representations for instance segmentation, to explore the scalability and applicability of polygon detection. Considering that modern mask-based meth-ods have accumulated many task-specific designs, we construct a parallel coun-terpart called Mask-DETR to isolate the effect of representation. This trend is further con-firmed on specialized benchmarks, such as SpaceNet [29] (building footprints) and PanNuke [11] (cell nuclei), where Poly-DETR even surpasses its mask-based counterpart in all accuracy, efficiency and complexity. 

## 2 Related Work 

2.1 Instance Segmentation 

Mainstream methods typically formulate instance segmentation as dense pixel-wise classification within Regions of Interest (RoI), to generate binary masks for foreground objects. According to their implementation paradigms, mask-based methods can be classified into two categories, i.e ., two-stage and one-stage methods. Represented by Mask R-CNN [12] and its successors [2, 4], two-stage methods use the detected bounding boxes as RoIs, and then perform pixel-wise classification based on RoI features. In contrast, one-stage methods, such as YOLACT [1], CondInst [28] and SparseInst [8], no longer rely on explicit RoI operations. They construct a shared mask feature map over the entire image and predict instance-specific kernels, which enable direct mask generation via dot product or convolution. Furthermore, some methods extend the one-stage paradigm to Transformer architectures, introducing additional branches for end-to-end mask prediction. MaskFormer [7] and its successors, Mask2Former [6] and Mask DINO [19], employ a Transformer encoder-decoder to parameterize instance-specific kernels with object queries. However, dense pixel-wise classifi-cation becomes computationally expensive and memory demanding at high im-age resolutions, while interior pixels that are not essential for delineating object boundaries are over-modeled. 4 J. Sun et al. 

2.2 Polygon Detection 

Motivated by the aforementioned bottleneck, we view polygon detection as a promising alternative, in which instance contours are represented by a set of predicted vertex coordinates. Some early works [10,17,22,23,34] can be regarded as precursors in this direction. Following the Active Contour Mechanism (ACM) of Snake [15], multi-stage networks are constructed to progressively refine the initially predicted polygons using their vertex features. To avoid the inference latency caused by ACM, another line of work introduces Polar Representation into advanced object detection detectors, extending them to single-shot polygon detection. PolarMask [30] and its variants [31] constitute the first polar-based approach, in which Polar Centerness and Polar IoU are designed for polygon-specific sample assignment and loss supervision. PolarNeXt [27] further reveals that polar-based polygon detection suffers from a Representation Error that is highly sensitive to the placement of starting points. RMask IoU is proposed to eliminate the assessment blindness of Polar IoU on such errors. However, existing polar-based methods typically choose starting points from fixed feature grids based on classification scores. This discrete and confidence-driven selection inevitably limits the flexibility of Polar Representation. 

2.3 Detection Transformer 

To seek a unified remedy for the issues discussed in Sec. 2.1 and Sec. 2.2, we explore Detection Transformers, which reformulate object detection as set pre-diction over sparse object queries and naturally support end-to-end coordinate regression. DETR [3] proposes the first Detection Transformer network, which re-moves the hand-crafted components in classical detectors, such as anchor boxes, region proposals, and non-maximum suppression. However, the vanilla DETR faces two major limitations: inefficient feature interaction and slow training con-vergence. To alleviate these issues, Deformable DETR [38] presents a multi-scale Deformable Attention mechanism to sample and aggregate key features around reference points using learnable offsets. Building on this baseline, subsequent works further accelerate training convergence by modifying both supervision scheme and object query design. For example, MS-DETR [35] introduces a hy-brid supervision strategy that combines one-to-one and one-to-many matching, while DN-DETR [18] and DINO [33] construct denoising queries to explicitly model matching perturbations. Building upon Detection Transformers, we ex-ploit their global contextual interaction to enable continuous spatial modeling for Polar Representation, while leveraging their structural compatibility with polygon detection for mask-free instance segmentation.

3.2 Investigation 

However, polar parameters are not geometrically equivalent to box parameters, despite their structural similarity. Concretely, the same dimensions (x, y ) de-note the fixed box center in box parameters b but a movable starting point in polar parameters p. The remaining dimensions (w, h ) are static size attributes, while radial distances D are position-conditioned and change with the starting point. Therefore, naively reusing the training and inference scheme in DETR-like detectors results in two problems in practice: 

(1) Mismatched Supervision Reference. In polygon detection, static su-pervision ties the geometric reference for distance regression to a fixed starting point throughout training. As shown in Fig. 2(a), when the regressed starting point drifts, the reconstructed polygon becomes systematically misaligned with the instance contour. This suggests that supervision references should be dynam-ically updated conditioned on the regressed starting point, thereby motivating our proposed Position-Aware Training Scheme. 

(2) Incompatible Sampling Reference. In modern DETR-like detectors, De-formable Attention samples and aggregates features via learnable offsets around a box-oriented reference. As shown in Fig. 2(b), sampling locations typically cluster at the box center and spread toward the box edges, forming an effective pattern for box localization and classification. However, when it comes to radial-distance regression, the most informative cues lie around the starting point and along the instance boundary. This sampling reference wastes attention budget on regions that are weakly relevant to distance estimation, thereby motivating our proposed Polar Deformable Attention. 6 J. Sun et al. 3.3 Polygon Detection Transformer 

Poly-DETR adopts the standard architecture design for detection as in De-formable DETR [38] with minimal modifications. As illustrated in Fig. 3, Poly-DETR consists of a backbone, a Transformer encoder, a Transformer decoder, and a group of MLP prediction heads. In terms of network structure, the primary modification lies in each decoder layer, whose regression output is expanded from 4 box parameters to 2 + K polar parameters. This design enables instance seg-mentation to be performed in a detection-style manner, where each object query is directly tied to a compact set of geometric parameters, without relying on ex-tra high-resolution mask features or repeated upsampling. In addition, to better integrate Polar Representation into the DETR framework, we further propose a new training scheme and design a customized attention module to address the supervision and sampling mismatches discussed in Sec. 3.2. 

Query-to-Polygon Pipeline. Poly-DETR retains the backbone and Trans-former encoder of Deformable DETR. The input image is first fed into a ResNet backbone to extract multi-scale features. Channel mapping is applied to the last three stages {C3, C 4, C 5} (with strides 8/16/32), and C5 is further downsam-pled to build an additional level with stride 64. These four feature maps are then flattened into a sequence of feature tokens and sent to the Transformer en-coder. Here, standard Deformable Self-Attention [38] incorporates global context to produce the encoded feature memory as well as a set of query embeddings for the decoder. Polar Deformable Attention (Polar-DA). In the decoder of Deformable DETR [38], cross-attention is implemented by Deformable Attention, which sparsely samples and aggregates multi-scale features. For each object query q[l] at decoder layer l, given a 4D box reference (x, y, w, h ), Deformable Attention pre-dicts a small set of sampling offsets ∆g ∈ R2 for each attention head. Concretely, the sampling location is obtained by adding offsets ∆g to the reference center 

(x, y ), while ∆g is rescaled by the box size (w, h ), i.e ., g = ( x, y ) + ∆g ⊙ (w, h ),and features are then bilinearly sampled at g. This box-oriented sampling typi-cally converges to a box-shaped pattern, where samples cluster around the box center and become denser near box edges (Fig. 2(b)), which aligns well with box regression and classification. However, for polygon detection based on Polar Representation, sampling locations should instead focus on the neighborhood of the starting point and boundary evidence along each radial direction. To geometrically align the cross-attention aggregated features with the regression targets of Polar Representation, we propose Polar Deformable Attention to re-parameterize the sampling scheme in a polar-oriented manner. To be specific, cross-attention reference is shifted from the box center c to starting point s, so that the sampling anchor matches the geometric origin of radial-distance regression, while reducing the systematic bias of box-centered sampling toward interior cues. ## 4 Experiments 

4.1 Experiments and Implementation Details Experiment Settings. In this paper, main results and ablation studies are conducted on MS COCO [21], setting the shorter side to 800 pixels with aspect ratio preserved. Following common practice for fair comparison, data augmen-tation only contains random flip and scale jitter unless specified. In addition, Cityscapes [9] is introduced for high-resolution evaluation, while PanNuke [11] and SpaceNet [29] are used to study modeling of regular instance contours. In terms of evaluation metrics, the segmentation accuracy is evaluated by the standard Mask mAP, with AP 50 and AP 75 also reported. All experiments are conducted on NVIDIA RTX 4090, using 4 GPUs for training and 1 GPU for infer-ence. Moreover, inference speed, model complexity, computational overhead and memory consumption are respectively measured by FPS, Params (M), FLOPs (G) and Mems (MB). Notably, TensorRT or FP16 is not used for acceleration. 

Implementation Details. Poly-DETR is instantiated from Deformable DETR [38] network, using a backbone ResNet-50 [13] pretrained on ImageNet [26]. All models are implemented on MMDetection toolbox [5], optimized by AdamW with a base learning rate of 1e-4. The encoder-decoder Transformer architecture and hyperparameters are kept the same as in Deformable DETR, with only the cross-attention replaced by our proposed Polar-DA. The loss coefficients 

{λcls , λ dist , λ rmask } are set to (2 , 5, 2) , with the inner-cost weight λinner = 5 .The number of rays in Polar Representation is set to K = 32 (2 5) to align with channel allocation. Moreover, hybrid supervision strategy [35] is adopted to accelerate convergence, reducing the training schedule from 50/150 (short/long) to 12/36 epochs with negligible performance drop. More details are provided in supplementary materials.

## 5 Conclusion and Future Work 

In conclusion, we summarize the evolution of polar-based polygon detection as a four-stage roadmap. At the first stage, Polar Representation established an explicit-geometry paradigm for instance segmentation, where classic detectors were linearly expanded in regression dimensionality to enable polygon detec-tion. At the second stage, the optimization focus shifted to the representation errors tightly coupled with starting-point placement, with rasterization tech-niques introduced to quantify these errors. This paper marks the third stage, where Transformer architecture is integrated to optimize starting points in con-tinuous space, together with a systematic comparison between polar and mask representations to highlight the application regime and extensibility of polygon detection. Finally, at the fourth stage, the intrinsic limitations of Polar Represen-tation on fragmented instances and irregular boundaries should be recognized. Here, Polar Representation will be positioned as a coarse-grained polygonal prior and coupled with vertex offsets or adaptive angular sampling, enabling flexible modeling of key endpoints and complex topology. A.2 Representation Error 

PolarNeXt [27] highlights an additional error in polygon detection, termed Rep-resentation Error . Under Polar Representation, the constructed polygons only approximate the groud-truth contours, with achievable quality strongly depend-ing on the placement of their start points. As a result, the uncovered regions con-tribute to the representation error. Moreover, the representation error is highly non-uniform and may change abruptly even under small perturbations of the starting point, making hand-crafted heuristics ( e.g ., center prior) unreliable for starting-point localization. In this paper, we argue that previous polar-based 18 J. Sun et al. 

methods [27, 30, 31] typically select starting points on fixed feature grids based on classification scores, which inevitably limits the flexibility of Polar Represen-tation. Therefore, we introduce the Transformer architecture to model starting points in continuous space by leveraging its contextual feature interaction. 

A.3 Deformable DETR 

To reduce the computational overhead of global attention, vanilla DETR [3] restricts the encoder input to low-resolution features, which is suboptimal for multi-scale detection, particularly for small objects. Deformable DETR [38] ad-dresses this issue with Deformable Attention, where a sparse set of key sam-pling locations is "learned" over multi-scale feature maps for feature aggrega-tion, substantially reducing computation while accelerating convergence. This sparse-attention design has been widely adopted by modern DETR-like detec-tors in both encoder self-attention and decoder cross-attention. Motivated by this generality, we finally choose Deformable DETR as the baseline for Polygon Detection Transformer, whose extension naturally enables plug-and-play transfer to other modern DETR-like detectors. In addition, Deformable DETR provides a query-processing pipeline that is naturally compatible with polygon regression. To be specific, the encoder produces a set of candidate object proposals and selects high-confidence ones to initialize object queries. Then, the decoder pre-dicts layer-wise regression offsets and accumulates them across layers. Building on this pipeline, we instantiate Poly-DETR with two polygon-specific modules, Polar Deformable Attention and Position-Aware Training Scheme.

C.1 Hybrid Supervision Strategy

Concretely, following standard mask-based Transformer designs [6,19], Mask-DETR flattens the last three feature maps from the backbone and feeds them into the encoder to form the global memory. Meanwhile, the higher-resolution feature (at roughly 1/4 input resolution) is retained to preserve fine-grained spatial details. This feature is fused with an upsampled version of the encoder memory to produce the mask feature, from which instance masks are predicted and then upsampled by 4× to input resolution. In terms of supervision, Mask-DETR shares the same box-level losses as Poly-DETR, while additionally us-ing two mask supervisions: a pixel-wise Cross-Entropy Loss and a Dice Loss for shape consistency. Notably, these two losses play roles analogous to those in Poly-DETR: Cross-Entropy Loss is mainly responsible for enforcing interior consistency, similar in spirit to Dist L1 Loss, whereas Dice Loss serves as an exte-rior constraint, aligning with the role of RMask Loss. For training, Mask-DETR adopts the same data augmentation and optimization setup as Poly-DETR ( i.e ., random horizontal flipping and large-scale jittering, AdamW with a learning rate of 1e −4), and is trained with the same hybrid supervision strategy [35] for ac-celeration, rather than the denoising strategy [33]. Here, we plan to release the code for Mask-DETR together with the camera-ready version to ensure the re-producibility of our results. 

## E Do Box Branches Work in the Decoder? 

Poly-DETR is built upon Detection Transformers by extending the box regres-sion of their decoders to predict polar parameters. A natural question is whether keeping the original box branch and adding a parallel polygon branch can be beneficial. The answer is yes: the box branch remains effective and can serve as a practical trick to substantially reduce training time. To make it compatible with polygon geometry, the box parameterization is slightly modified from (x, y, w, h )

to (x, y, l, t, r, b ), where (x, y ) denotes the coordinates of starting points instead of box centers and (l, t, r, b ) are distances from these starting points to their four box sides, i.e ., a special polar parameters with four rays. The gain mainly comes from the matching stage. Polygon-level matching typically relies on rasterization to estimate shape overlap, which becomes ex-pensive when computed for all Query-GT pairs (this overhead is confined to training but not required at inference). With the box branch enabled, assign-ment can be performed using box-based costs, largely avoiding the costly polygon matching for all pairs, while the polygon branch is still supervised by loss func-tions. Tab. 8 summarizes the outcome: under the same 12-epoch schedule, GPU training time is reduced from 150h to 83h, with only a minor drop in accuracy (37.8 →37.7 mAP). G.2 Failure Cases 

We visualize three typical failure cases to illustrate the challenges inherent in polar-based methods. As shown in Fig. 9, Case 1 includes two fork instances with highly variable contours, where the concave and convex areas are difficult to capture. With a limited number of rays, the network tends to overlook the concave regions in favor of maximizing the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) with the instance contour, which results in ignoring boundary details. In Case 2, the umbrella instance has both regular and protruding regions. Since the polar rays are uniformly distributed, only a limited number of rays can capture the details of the protruding region, leading to inaccuracies in Polar Representation. Case 3 highlights a car instance fragmented due to occlusion. Polar Representation typically assumes the instance as a whole around the starting point, making it difficult to handle fragmented regions and leading to incomplete predictions.
