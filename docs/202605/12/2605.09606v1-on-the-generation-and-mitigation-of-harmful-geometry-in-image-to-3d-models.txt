## Abstract 

Recent advances in image-to-3D models have significantly improved the fidelity and accessibility of 3D content cre-ation. Such a powerful reconstruction capability that enables creative design can also be misused by the adversaries to gen-erate harmful geometries, which can be further fabricated via 3D printers and pose real-world risks. However, such risks are largely underexplored: it remains unclear how well cur-rent image-to-3D models can produce these harmful geome-tries, and whether existing safeguards can reliably prevent such generation. To fill this gap, we conduct a systematic measurement study of harmful geometry generation and mitigation. We first describe this risk through three kinds of unsafe cate-gories: direct-use physical hazards, risky templates or com-ponents, and deceptive replicas. Each category is instan-tiated with representative objects. We evaluate both open-source and commercial image-to-3D models under original, degraded, viewpoint-shifted, and semantically camouflaged inputs. We consider different evaluation metrics, including geometric validity, multi-view VLM-based semantic scor-ing, targeted human validation, and controlled physical fab-rication. The results reveal a concerning reality that current image-to-3D models can effectively reconstruct the harmful geometries, while fewer than 0.3% of such geometries trigger commercial moderation flags. Notably, harmful 3D struc-tures can still be clearly generated even when the input im-ages are taken from non-standard viewpoints, corrupted with noise, or intentionally obscured. As a first step toward mitigation, we evaluate three rep-resentative safeguard families, including input moderation, model-level benign alignment, and output-level filtering. We find that existing safeguards have distinct weaknesses: off-the-shelf input moderators achieve only low recall, while the others improve recall but incur over 30% false posi-tives on ambiguous benign/harmful pairs. We further de-velop a stacked defense that can reduce harmful retention to <1%, but still at 11% overall false-positive cost. Taken together, our findings demonstrate the risk in the current system and encourage better geometry-aware safeguards for

## 1 Introduction 

Recent advances in image-to-3D models [17, 19, 43, 44] have rapidly improved the fidelity and usability of 3D content gen-eration. Given a single image (or multiple views), modern systems can now produce textured 3D assets that are increas-ingly usable in rendering, animation, and downstream fab-rication workflows such as 3D printing. These capabilities boost these models from research prototypes to widely ac-cessible tools, such as Vast AI 1 and Hunyuan3D 2.Since image-to-3D outputs are fabricable geometry that consumer 3D printers can physically realize on demand (see Figure 1), unmoderated risky outputs can pose direct physi-cal threats, which is distinct from unsafe 2D images whose risks remain perceptual [32, 33]. However, this risk remains largely underexplored. We therefore ask: how well cur-rent image-to-3D models can generate harmful geometries (Evaluation) , and whether existing safeguards reliably pre-vent such generation (Mitigation) ?

Building on this threat model, we perform risk evaluation and mitigation as follows. 

Risk Evaluation. We frame risk evaluation as a measure-ment pipeline. To construct the dataset, we instantiate un-safe categories with representative object families, i.e., ki-netic weapons, security-sensitive structural components, and visually realistic weapon-like replicas. These families are grounded in legal and regulatory frameworks across major jurisdictions (e.g., the United States, the European Union, and China). For data collection, we define explicit inclusion and ex-clusion criteria that restrict the scope to externally visible, image-recoverable, and policy-relevant geometry-mediated risks. These criteria allow geometry-retention metrics to capture risk-relevant features within this scope, since the selected objects’ risk-relevant functional cues (e.g., blade edges, barrel hollows) are inherently expressed in external geometry. For each risk category, we follow a funnel-based collection process: starting from high-quality prototype im-ages and expanding them with structured perturbations, in-cluding viewpoint shifts, image degradation, and semantic camouflage. This design allows us to measure whether risk-relevant geometry is preserved when inputs become visually non-canonical. For behavioral measurement, we use two complementary sets of signals (see Section 6 for more details): (i) geometric validity, which captures whether the generated mesh is struc-turally usable, and (ii) semantic geometry-retention signals, including harmful-cue preservation, functional-cue preserva-tion, and visual fidelity assessed through multi-view VLM-based scoring. To reduce reliance on any single proxy, we cross-check these automated signals with targeted human validation (240 samples, 5 annotators), finding that VLM scores are broadly consistent with human judgments and pre-serve the relative ranking of outputs. We further verify scorer sensitivity using controlled visible-fault edits, where both VLM and human scores decrease as risk-relevant cues are weakened. In total, we evaluate 6 image-to-3D models (open-source and commercial) on 114 seed images expanded to 1,057 per-turbed variants, and conduct controlled physical fabrication on a curated subset. On our 1–5 ordinal rubric for harmful-cue preservation, current systems score 3.12–3.66 on proto-type inputs, indicating that risk-relevant geometry and pro-portions are consistently recovered with only fine-grained in-terfaces degraded. They often remain at similarly elevated levels even under non-canonical inputs, yet only 3 requests (0.28%) in our commercial evaluation trigger moderation flags. We further conduct physical fabrication experiments on a highest-scored subset spanning three risk categories, which are drawn from the VAST-3D outputs. We evaluate properties such as dimensional consistency and structural in-tegrity to assess functionality. 

Risk Mitigation. To understand whether existing safeguards can reliably prevent such generation, we consider three rep-resentative safeguard families: input moderation, model-level benign alignment, and output-level geometric filtering. Our evaluation shows that each stage is individually insuffi-cient. For input moderation, off-the-shelf visual moderators miss functionally hazardous but visually benign inputs (0– 27.3% recall), exposing a fundamental semantic–geometric mismatch in 2D moderation. Although fine-tuning an im-age foundation model raises F1 to 0.887, it still incurs 7.4% false positives on hard negatives, suggesting that ambigu-ous benign/harmful pairs cannot be reliably separated from 2D appearance alone. For model-level benign alignment, suppression improves in some settings but does not trans-fer reliably across 3D representations and introduces mea-surable benign-utility trade-offs, indicating that alignment is entangled with model-specific geometric priors. For output-level geometric filtering, moderate-to-strong F1 of 0.7–0.8 is achievable, but hard negatives remain challenging. Astacked combination of these safeguards reduces measured harmful-geometry retention to <1% on our benchmark, at an 11% overall false-positive cost. Overall, these results suggest that effective mitigation requires defenses that are geometry-aware, representation-aware, and integrated across the pipeline, rather than stronger classifiers at any single stage. Notably, our study focuses on safety measurement and safeguards benchmarking, and no printable assets will be re-leased. Our contributions can be summarized as follows: • We present a systematic measurement of safety risks in modern image-to-3D models, focusing on risk-relevant ob-ject categories grounded in existing legal frameworks. • We evaluate model behavior across diverse input condi-tions and show that current models often preserve harmful cues even under perturbed inputs. Even commercial API services largely overlook this risk, with near-zero modera-tion observed in our evaluation. • We evaluate three representative safeguard families and find that no single defense is sufficient; even stacked de-fenses incur a non-trivial usability cost. 

## 2 Related Work 

3D Representation. To enable the learning and rep-resentation of 3D geometries, a variety of methods have been proposed, each tailored to different application con-texts. Unstructured 3D representations include mesh, voxel, point clouds, Signed Distance Fields (SDF), Neural Radi-ance Fields (NeRF) [23], and Gaussian Splatting [18], each of which plays a distinct role in 3D modeling and reconstruc-tion tasks. Importantly, these representations are geometry-grounded, which means they can be interconverted through algorithms. Recently, the focus of 3D geometry representation has shifted toward developing compact latent formulations. These approaches compress geometric information into highly lightweight forms, which enables efficient integration with diffusion models [8, 14]. Amid extensive exploration 2and methodological competition, two dominant paradigms have emerged in the field of 3D generation: (1)VecSet [3,46], which encodes 3D shapes as sets of 1D latent vectors; (2) Sparse voxel [34, 44], which embed 3D assets into features anchored to sparsely distributed voxels. 

3D Generation. In the domain of image-to-3D genera-tion, early approaches explored a range of architectures, in-cluding reconstruction-based methods [15, 37] and autore-gressive models [35, 42]. However, these methods often struggle to reconstruct high-fidelity geometry in regions un-observed in the input image, and potential inconsistencies across multiple views may further degrade generation qual-ity. More recently, the field has shifted toward developing 3D-native diffusion models, which typically consist of two core components: a geometry encoder and a flow-matching transformer. While conditioning the flow-matching trans-former [8] on input images has become standard practice, the choice of geometry encoder, as discussed above, remains a source of performance variation. Building upon the Vec-Set encoder, several works, including Michelangelo [49], CLAY [47], Hunyuan3D-2 [17], and TripoSG [19], have demonstrated impressive results in high-quality 3D synthe-sis. Concurrently, another promising direction leverages the sparse voxel encoder, with representative models such as Trellis series [43,44] and Spar3D [20] achieving competitive performance. The high fidelity of these models is now en-abling real-world deployment in industrial applications, such as Vast AI and Hunyuan3D. Despite these advances in generation quality, security im-plications, particularly in image-to-3D generation, have re-mained largely unexamined. Notably, such models can di-rectly convert 2D images into printable 3D objects, raising novel safety concerns. To date, the only related study [38] investigates risks in text-to-3D models by adapting harmful prompts from text-to-image systems [32]. In this work, we identify and expose a distinct and critical risk tied to physi-cal harm, which arises specifically in the context of image-conditioned 3D generation. 

3D Classification. To capture the geometric characteris-tics of 3D objects, current approaches primarily operate on either point cloud or mesh representations. For point-cloud-based methods, models such as PointNet [31] and its successors [21] directly process unordered point sets using permutation-invariant neural networks to produce classifica-tion outputs. For mesh-based methods, the 3D shape is rep-resented as a graph where vertices and edges encode surface connectivity. Graph neural networks, such as MeshCNN [13] and follow-up architectures [29], leverage this structure to learn both local geometric features and global topological patterns. In this work, we adapt and evaluate both paradigms for the task of harmful geometry moderation, training classi-fiers to distinguish between benign and regulated or danger-ous 3D objects. 

## 3 Threat Model 

Since online regulated 3D assets and consumer-grade fabri-cation have long raised physical safety concerns [1,6,27,45], we study whether current image-to-3D models can recover misuse-relevant geometry from images, and whether exist-ing safeguards can reliably prevent such recovery. Our goal is to establish the existence of this risk in current systems, rather than to claim that every generated output is immedi-ately functional as an end-use artifact. 

Adversary’s Capability. We consider an adversary who does not possess professional CAD skills and does not manu-ally reconstruct complex 3D geometry from scratch. Instead, the adversary uses an image-to-3D model as the main source of geometric inference from one or a few reference images. We assume access only to commodity post-processing and fabrication tools commonly available to ordinary users, such as object rescaling from a known reference, automatic mesh repair by slicing software, and consumer-grade 3D print-ing with standard materials (e.g., PLA). We do not assume specialized manufacturing expertise, high-end equipment, or substantial manual redesign after generation. 

Adversary’s Goal. The adversary’s goal is not necessarily to obtain a fully functional end-use object directly from the model, but to recover geometry that is sufficiently accurate to enable downstream physical misuse. We therefore distin-guish three unsafe outcomes :• Direct-use physical hazards: geometries whose harmful-ness is primarily encoded in visible shape and structural form, such as impact-bearing surfaces or sharp tips. These objects are security-relevant as printed : even when fab-ricated using consumer-grade materials, they may still present a direct risk of bodily harm. • Risky templates or components: geometries that may not be immediately operational in a consumer-grade print, but preserve sufficient spatial references for downstream du-plication, fitting, or refinement. These objects are security-relevant because they lower the barrier to misuse by recov-ering interface-critical geometry, even when full function-ality requires stronger materials or downstream re-creation in a different material. • Deceptive replicas: geometries whose main risk lies in vi-sual realism rather than complete mechanical functionality. These outputs can resemble regulated or threatening ob-jects closely enough to support intimidation or deception, even when hidden internal mechanisms are absent. 

Relationship to Existing 3D Platforms. CAD/STL repos-itories (e.g., Thingiverse, Cults, MakerWorld) already host weapon-related geometries, but their coverage is coarse. We manually searched each of our 83 seed objects across the three platforms. At the category level (i.e., any broadly similar item exists), 85.54% find a match. At the instance level (i.e., a geometrically precise match to the specific seed object), the rate drops to 42.17%. Public repositories con-centrate on standardized shapes; highly customized variants, such as exact key bittings, niche firearm accessories, or ATM skimmers, are largely absent. Some platforms further restrict availability: Cults, for instance, blocks search queries for specific firearm brands such as Glock and Sig Sauer, though 3other brands remain accessible. Image-to-3D bypasses both limitations by generating the exact geometry depicted in an arbitrary reference photograph, without depending on com-munity uploads or platform coverage. Experimental settings and discussions are in Section A. 

## 4 Evaluation Scope 

We select representative object families through a two-step process. First, we restrict scope to objects whose security rel-evance is primarily carried by external geometry and that are plausibly recoverable and fabricable under consumer-grade workflows. Second, we reviewed recurring legal treatment across major jurisdictions to identify object families that are consistently subject to restriction rather than merely contro-versial. This process yields three families below; it is in-tended as principled scoping rather than an exhaustive legal survey. 

Kinetic Weapons. This family instantiates direct-use physi-cal hazards : external topology (e.g., sharp or impact-bearing geometry) that remains harmful even when printed with consumer-grade materials. Knuckle dusters, for instance, face near-universal regulation. They are classified as con-trolled implements in China [36], banned weapons in Ger-many [10], and prohibited in multiple U.S. states [11]. We collect two representative cases: knuckle dusters and tacti-cal spikes, and assess whether generated geometry preserves sharp, reinforced, or impact-bearing structural features. In our evaluation, we assess whether the generated geometry exhibits sharp, reinforced, or otherwise unsafe structural fea-tures characteristic of kinetic weapons. 

Structural Components. This family instantiates risky tem-plates or components : fit-critical or interface-critical geome-try that enables downstream duplication or refinement. Mul-tiple jurisdictions treat regulated structural parts equivalently to complete weapons—e.g., EU Directive 2021/555 classi-fies firearm receivers under the same regime as complete firearms [9], and 18 U.S.C. §1029 criminalizes hardware de-signed to facilitate access-device fraud [41]. We collect three sub-classes: firearm structural parts (e.g., receivers), intru-sion tools (e.g., bump keys), and fraud-related components (e.g., skimmer faceplates). We assess whether generated geometry preserves interface-critical dimensions and spatial references, such as receiver cavity profiles, key bitting pat-terns, and card-slot clearances, that could serve as functional templates for downstream replication. 

Visual Threats. This family instantiates deceptive replicas :visually realistic geometry that supports intimidation or de-ception. The UK Violent Crime Reduction Act prohibits manufacture of Realistic Imitation Firearms [28]; U.S. fed-eral law requires imitation firearms to carry a blaze orange tip [7]; China bans replicas matching the color and contour of military firearms [24]. We collect three cases: real firearms, toy guns, and sci-fi guns—the latter two to test whether models suppress toy-like appearance and default to generat-ing visually realistic non-compliant replicas. ## 5 Data Collection Strategy 

To ensure that our dataset is both representative and di-verse, we adopt a funnel-based collection strategy. We be-gin by curating a seed set of 114 high-quality prototype im-ages for each risk category. Each seed is then expanded into 15 distinct variants using a suite of augmentation tech-niques—including 456 viewpoint shifting variants, 405 im-age quality degradation variants(e.g., blur, compression arti-facts, or low resolution), and 82 semantic camouflaging vari-ants (e.g., stylization, sketch-to-photo translation, or color modification). This process yields a final dataset of 1,057 examples, improving coverage across a wide range of real-world input conditions that image-to-3D models may en-counter (see Figure 2, upper). 

Prototype Selection. This benchmark targets harmful geom-etry satisfying the following conditions, where the property is externally visible , recoverable from images , and plausibly usable under image-to-3D workflows . The detailed sampling criteria table is shown in Section B. Each prototype is carefully chosen to reflect canonical or commonly encountered visual patterns of the target harmful object, such as brass knuckles, firearm frames, or bump keys. Specifically, they are sourced from three distinct image types in a 4:3:3 ratio: • Real-world Images : Collected from Google Images and Reddit, these often feature cluttered scenes, poor lighting, occlusions, and arbitrary camera angles. They are intended to evaluate the model’s robustness under noisy conditions. • E-commerce/studio Images : Captured against clean white backgrounds with standardized viewpoints and optimal lighting, these represent the ideal input scenario and reflect the upper performance bound of the models. • Illustrative Images : Sourced from 3D printing communi-ties (e.g., Thingiverse concept sketches) or created from scratch, these include abstract drawings, CAD renderings, or stylized illustrations. They test the model’s ability to generalize from non-photorealistic inputs. Notably, each collected prototype is verified by three inde-pendent annotators to ensure it aligns with the legal defini-tions in Table 3. 4View-shifting Augmentation. To simulate partial observa-tion scenarios where only a limited perspective of the object is available, we leverage the state-of-the-art image genera-tion model Nano Banana 3 to synthesize four viewpoints for each prototype: • Top-viewpoint : A direct top-down (flat-lay) view, with the camera oriented vertically above the object. • Side-viewpoint : A side profile (edge) view, obtained by ro-tating the object 90 degrees to reveal only its thickness or silhouette, intentionally occluding internal features such as holes or cavities. • Iso-viewpoint : A 45-degree isometric view that simultane-ously exposes the top surface and one side, providing clear depth cues and geometric context. • Bottom-viewpoint : A bottom-up view, capturing the object from underneath, which is an uncommon but plausible an-gle. For each prototype, we craft four variants. This augmen-tation strategy explicitly tests the model’s ability to reason about and reconstruct full 3D geometry from sparse or in-complete visual cues, thereby evaluating its capacity for 3D imagination under partial observability. 

Degradation Augmentation. This augmentation evaluates the model’s ability to generate coherent and complete 3D ge-ometry from low-quality or incomplete visual inputs. Specif-ically, we assess whether the model can reconstruct harmful objects in real-world adversarial conditions, where critical visual details are degraded or obscured. We apply three types of realistic degradations to each prototype image: • Gaussian Blur : Simulates out-of-focus snapshots, mimick-ing casual or surreptitious photography. • Random Noise : Emulates low-light conditions (e.g., grainy surveillance footage), where signal-to-noise ratio is poor. • Random Masking : Represents deliberate obfuscation, where key portions of the object are explicitly hidden. For each prototype, we craft nine variants, using the degra-dation. Our evaluation focuses on whether the model’s gen-erative priors enable the recovery of regulated or dangerous structures. The detailed settings are shown in Section B. 

Semantic Camouflaging. This augmentation evaluates the model’s behavior when presented with dual-use disguises, which reflect a critical real-world threat that adversaries may deliberately obfuscate the true nature of dangerous items by embedding them within a benign surface. ## 6 Virtual Evaluation 

Our evaluation is designed to measure harmful geometry re-tention in generated 3D assets. We aim to measure whether image-to-3D models preserve visible geometric cues that are relevant to downstream misuse, and how this preservation changes across models and input perturbations. 

## 6.1 Evaluation Metrics 

Regarding evaluation metrics, we use a multi-layer mea-surement protocol. First, we measure low-level genera-tion quality and mesh validity. Second, following previous work [43, 44], we use an anchored ordinal rubric to assess visible semantic and geometric cues from multi-view ren-derings. Third, we validate the semantic rubric through hu-man annotation, geometry-level fault sensitivity, and selected physical fabrication studies.

## 6.1.2 Semantic Geometry Retention 

The quantitative metrics above capture low-level visual con-sistency and mesh validity, but they do not directly character-ize higher-level properties such as category fidelity, misuse-relevant shape cues, or apparent harmful-cue preservation. We therefore use two state-of-the-art VLMs (Gemini-3-Flash and Qwen-3-VL) as a scalable semantic scoring proxy. They provide an ordinal signal for understanding whether gener-ated geometries preserve harmful and functional cues. 

Rubric Description. For each generated object, we con-struct a 2 × 3 diagnostic collage consisting of: (1) the refer-ence image, (2) the best-matching RGB render, (3) the best-matching normal map 5, and (4–6) normal maps from three additional rotated views ( +90 ◦, +180 ◦, +270 ◦). An example is shown in Figure 2. This multi-view collage exposes ap-pearance similarity and view-consistent geometric structure. The VLM assigns a 5-level ordinal rubric on three dimen-sions (The detailed prompt and scoring rubric are provided in Section C.1): • Visual Fidelity : The degree to which the generated asset preserves the visible characteristics of the reference im-age. A higher score indicates stronger visual and shape alignment. For augmented inputs, we instead report Ref-erence Alignment , which evaluates the generated geome-try against the original clean prototype rather than the de-graded or altered input. • Functional-cue Preservation : The degree to which the generated geometry preserves visible cues related to han-dling, interfacing, fitting, or downstream refinement. Ex-amples include contact surfaces, grip regions, holes, slots, tips, or interface boundaries. • Harmful-cue Preservation : The degree to which the gener-ated geometry preserves visible cues associated with the

For harmful-cue preserva-tion, we report both the mean score across the two VLMs and a conservative aggregation using the maximum score across them. ## 6.1.3 Validation of Semantic Proxy 

As a scalable semantic proxy, we calibrate the VLM score with a targeted human validation study on 240 gener-ated samples from two representative models (Trellis-1 and VAST-3D), each annotated by five raters on Functional-cue Preservation and Harmful-cue Preservation using a 5-point scale. 

Human Calibration. The human study evaluates whether the ordinal rubric produces rankings broadly consistent with human assessment of visible geometry cues. First, we show that the inter-annotator agreement is substantial overall. For harmful-cue preservation on the Trellis-1 subset, the intra-class correlation reaches ICC(2,k)=0.67, indicating good re-liability [4]. On VAST-3D, ICC values are more moderate (0.31-0.45), which we attribute in part to reduced variance among higher-quality outputs (e.g., functional-cue preserva-tion Std = 0.64). As shown in Table 4, VLM-based evaluators show moder-ate positive alignment with human judgment and generally preserve the relative ranking of output quality. Spearman correlations are generally above 0.5 for Qwen and above 0.4 for Gemini. These results support the use of VLM scores as a scalable first-pass evaluation signal. Details are shown in Section C.2.

## 6.4 Robustness to View-rotation 

In this section, we assess how the potential hazard of gener-ated 3D models varies with the viewpoint of the input image. This setting reflects a realistic scenario where an adversary deliberately provides an image from a seemingly benign an-gle, while relying on the model’s generative priors to recon-struct the unseen but dangerous parts. The evaluation results are shown in Figure 6 (see Section C.6 for quantitative re-sults). Across different input viewpoints, we observe notable variations in both reconstruction quality and hazard poten-tial. The iso and top views consistently yield the highest fi-delity and alignment scores, indicating that models can faith-fully reconstruct the intended geometry when presented with informative perspectives. In contrast, the bottom and side views are significantly less effective, with alignment scores falling below 4, meaning many outputs under these views ex-hibit significant deviation in style or are completely different objects. Crucially, despite the noticeable drop in alignment score (from an average of 4.4 for iso views to 3.9 for bot-tom views), the harmful-cue preservation remains relatively high (decreasing only modestly from 3.65 to 3.35 on aver-age). This indicates that even when the overall reconstruction is imperfect, the model often retains or hallucinates danger-ous structural features. This precisely aligns with the adver-sary’s goal of leveraging the model’s internal priors to recon-struct unseen but harmful components. However, the side view leads to significant reductions in both alignment and harmful-cue preservation, likely because it reveals minimal discriminative detail (e.g., only thickness or edge profiles). Among the evaluated models, Trellis-2 and VAST-3D demonstrate the strongest view-rotation robustness, main-taining hazardous outputs even under the most ambiguous viewpoints. This resilience suggests that their richer geomet-8ric priors enable consistent generation of high-risk structures across diverse input conditions, raising further safety con-cerns. 

## 6.5 Robustness to Semantic Camouflaging 

In this section, we assess how the potential harmfulness of generated 3D models varies under semantic camouflaging. This reflects a realistic setting where an adversary deliber-ately obfuscates the dangerous nature of the input image to bypass the safety content filter. For this experiment, we ex-clude the Toy & Sci-Fi Guns category, as these objects are non-functional and do not pose physical risks, rendering the attack conceptually redundant. Results are visualized in Fig-ure 8 (see Section C.5 for quantitative results). While visual alignment with benign prompts remains high (> 4.0), the functional-cue and harmful-cue preservation ex-hibit distinct behaviors across model architectures: Surpris-ingly, earlier open-source models (e.g., Trellis-1, Hunyuan-3D-2) demonstrate strong robustness against semantic cam-ouflaging. Statistical tests show no significant difference between the original and semantically camouflaged outputs (p > 0.05). This suggests that these models rely heavily on coarse geometric priors rather than surface texture. Con-versely, advanced commercial models and Trellis-2 show sta-tistically significant sensitivity to the attacks. While this indi-cates that high-fidelity models pay more attention to surface semantics, the harmful-cue preservation remains consistently elevated, indicating that risky geometry often persists even when semantic camouflage changes the surface appearance. The attack successfully reduces the harmful-cue preservation slightly, but fails to reliably suppress security-relevant geom-etry. These findings underscore a critical gap: pixel-level dis-guises do not erase geometric threats. Current image-to-3D models successfully retain or reconstruct risk-relevant topol-ogy, including coarse external or interface geometry that re-mains security-relevant even when internal mechanisms are not fully recoverable. 

## 7 Case Study: Physical Fabrication 

In this section, we use physical fabrication as a bounded case study to ground our virtual geometry-retention metrics in ob-servable physical properties. We select 10 objects from the highest-scoring VAST-3D outputs, stratified across all three risk categories, ensuring coverage of distinct mechanism-specific properties (fit, dimensional consistency, structural integrity, and visual realism). 

Fabrication Setup. To match the adversary capability de-fined in Section 3, we restrict fabrication to commodity hard-ware and materials accessible without specialized expertise or procurement. Specifically, we use the Bambu Lab X1C with standard PLA filament—a setup representative of the consumer-grade 3D printing workflow available for under $1,000 in total equipment cost, requiring no prior manufac-turing experience beyond default slicer settings (Bambu Stu-dio). Since current image-to-3D models output unit meshes (i.e., the geometry has relative proportions but lacks abso-lute physical scale), we use a known dimension of the tar-get object (e.g., the standard width of a keyway) to estab-lish a scaling factor. Then, in the slicing software, the mesh is uniformly scaled until the reference feature matches the real-world metric. This step requires no CAD modeling ex-pertise, only a caliper or a ruler, yet it effectively recovers the functional dimensions of the generated artifact. In our experiments, we applied this method to all printed objects, ensuring they matched the target physical specifications. 

Functionality Evaluation. The printed objects exhibit high geometric fidelity (see Section D). We further evaluate real-world usability through controlled physical tests (see Fig-ure 7). The printed knuckles and spikes fit adult hands com-fortably. When struck against corrugated cardboard, they produce clean punctures and structural deformation. While PLA is lighter than metal, the sharpened geometry demon-strates a clear capacity for bodily harm as an improvised weapon. For the printed keys, the PLA TSA key inserts fully into a standard TSA residential lock cylinder, validating the ac-curacy of the generated profile. However, we observed that standard PLA lacks the shear strength to manipulate pins un-der spring tension (the bit twisted upon torque application). Consequently, we characterize this threat not as a direct tool, but as a functional template. Using the printed PLA key as a physical guide, we successfully commissioned a local lock-smith to cut a metal duplicate. The metal copy opened the target lock on the first attempt. This case study demonstrates that, for at least one restricted-key example, the generated model can serve as a physical template for restricted keys. Direct functional testing of the auto sear and card skimmer was ethically and legally restricted. For the card skimmer, the printed housing accepts a standard-thickness card through its slot and provides sufficient internal clearance to seat a com-pact magnetic read-head module, confirming that the gen-erated geometry serves as a viable enclosure for skimming hardware. For the auto sear, dimensional measurements con-firm that critical interfaces match operational specifications within functional tolerance (Table 6). While PLA lacks the shear strength for sustained cycling, the printed geometry can serve as a dimensionally accurate template for metal duplica-tion, bypassing the need for CAD expertise. In dim lighting or peripheral vision, the printed handguns are nearly indis-tinguishable from real firearms and lack any mandated safety marking, posing deception risks during security screenings.

This effect is especially pronounced for visual threats, where attention drifts away from weapon-identifying com-ponents. Together, these results indicate that attention misal-location rather than simple capacity limitations is a primary factor underlying the vulnerability.

This suggests that current geo-metric representations, whether learned or engineered, strug-gle to differentiate objects that are both visually and struc-turally similar to harmful positives (e.g., a toy gun vs. a real pistol). These findings indicate that purely geometric analysis is insufficient for reliable safety filtering in ambiguous cases. Future work should integrate semantic context into detection frameworks to resolve these challenging cases. 

## 8.4 Combined Defense Analysis 

To evaluate the cumulative effectiveness, we integrate input filtering, benign alignment, and output moderation (specifi-cally, TripoSG-VAE) into a sequential pipeline. We evaluate this pipeline on a composite dataset containing prototypes, semantic camouflaged examples, and adversarial examples, as well as the benign set used in the alignment test. For voxel-based models (e.g., Trellis) where alignment is less ef-fective, we bypass Stage 2 to preserve generation quality, re-lying solely on input and output filtering (represented by the "W/O Alignment" trajectory). ## 9 Discussion 

Lessons from Defense. Figure 13 indicates that, within our evaluated benchmark, safeguard composition benefits from conditional design rather than naive layer accumula-tion. In alignment-enabled settings , the substantial reduction of residual hazards renders may be redundant in alignment-enabled settings under our benchmark; given the associated false positives, final-stage moderation should be optional rather than default. In constrained settings (e.g., closed mod-els), conversely, the absence of alignment leaves risks that coarse input filters cannot intercept. This necessitates fine-grained output moderation to systematically target high-risk structures while preserving utility. Limitations. First, re-garding the dataset, while we followed a rigorous funnel-based collection strategy across three major risk categories, our taxonomy is not exhaustive. Second, our Fabrication tests were conducted on a single type of printer with standard PLA material. While sufficient to demonstrate functionality for the tested items, more demanding malicious applications might require engineering-grade materials (e.g., Nylon-CF, Metal), which introduce different fabrication constraints that we did not model. 

## 10 Conclusion 

Our findings indicate that existing safety mechanisms are in-sufficient when applied in isolation, while layered, geometry-aware defenses substantially reduce the likelihood of produc-ing usable harmful artifacts. We highlight an urgent need to treat image-to-3D systems as security-critical components rather than purely creative tools, and to incorporate physical-world risk considerations into their deployment.

## Ethical Considerations 

Legal Oversight. Prior to fabrication experiments, we con-sulted with legal counsel to confirm that our activities are consistent with applicable research exemptions. Specifically, the objects fabricated in this study were produced solely for controlled measurement purposes (e.g., dimensional verifica-tion and material stress testing) under supervised laboratory conditions, and were destroyed upon completion of the ex-periments. 

Scope of Physical Testing. For the TSA key experiment, we note that the key profile used is based on publicly available leaked designs, not proprietary or access-controlled specifi-cations. The locksmith duplication was conducted to verify the template-transfer threat model under controlled condi-tions; no unauthorized access to any lock or secured prop-erty was attempted. For legally restricted components (auto 15 sear, card skimmer), only controlled measurements are per-formed to test the functionality; no deployment testing was conducted. 

Data and Artifact Release. No directly printable hazardous STL files or manufacturable assets will be publicly released. Only rendered visualizations (e.g., 2D projections or shaded previews) and evaluation code will be made available. 

Responsible Disclosure. We reported our findings to the de-velopers of evaluated commercial services (VAST and Ten-cent) and relevant open-source maintainers on 2026.1.21, providing a summary of the identified risks and offering tech-nical assistance for developing stronger safety guardrails. As of the time of submission, we are awaiting their response.

## A Relationship to Existing 3D Platforms 

To contextualize the threat of image-to-3D generation, we conducted a comprehensive empirical comparison against traditional 3D model repositories. We selected three major platforms—Cults3D, MakerWorld, and Thingiverse—and searched for 3D models corresponding to our 83 seed ob-jects. 

Moderation and Paywall Friction. Traditional 3D plat-forms are governed by strict Terms of Service (ToS) and active community moderation. For example, we observed that Cults3D actively restricts search queries associated with prominent firearm brands; searching for keywords such as 

Sig Sauer , Ruger , Beretta , or Glock triggers direct platform restrictions, preventing users from accessing matching mod-els. Furthermore, when high-quality or specific weapon com-ponents do exist, they are often placed behind a paywall by creators or face imminent takedown due to platform policy violations. In stark contrast, commercial image-to-3D APIs lack these domain-specific safeguards, successfully generating geom-etry for 99.72% of our requests without imposing addi-tional financial barriers (beyond standard API costs) or post-generation takedowns. 

The Specificity Gap: Category vs. Instance Matching. 

Our search results revealed a steep drop-off between finding a conceptually similar item and finding an exact geometric replica. • High Category Match (85.54%): For the vast majority of items, platforms host at least one generic representation. A user searching for "brass knuckles" or "keys" will find hundreds of results. • Low Instance Match (42.17%): Finding a functional sub-stitute for a specific physical target is significantly harder. The successful instance matches primarily consist of stan-dardized items with universally recognized shapes (e.g., tactical spikes, standard brass knuckles) or highly iconic, mass-market firearms (e.g., Glock and AR-series), which have a dedicated hobbyist modeling community. Why Instance Matches Fail in Public Repositories: For the remaining 57.83% of objects, public platforms fall short due to the highly idiosyncratic nature of the items: • Keys: While platforms host numerous 3D key models (including well-known public leaks like the TSA master keys), they do not host keys with the exact bitting (teeth details) required to open a specific user’s lock. Generat-ing the precise bitting requires custom modeling, which image-to-3D can extrapolate directly from a photo. • Specific Firearm Accessories: Generic rails or silencers ex-ist, but accessories tailored to fit obscure or specific firearm models are rarely modeled by the community. • Niche and Malicious Objects: Devices explicitly designed for criminal activity, such as ATM skimmers, are highly niche. They lack a mainstream modeling community and are aggressively purged by platform moderators, making them virtually impossible to find as downloadable STLs. Ultimately, image-to-3D eliminates the attacker’s depen-dency on the open-source community’s willingness to model and host specific items, enabling the instantaneous cloning of customized, niche, or restricted objects.

Setup. We use 240 generated samples, each annotated by five raters on functional-cue preservation and harmful-cue preservation . For each sample, we compare the mean human score against the corresponding VLM score from Gemini and Qwen. 

Human Calibration. Table 4 summarizes the alignment be-tween VLM-based evaluators and human judgments. Both Qwen and Gemini show significant positive Spearman corre-lations with human ratings across models and metrics, with Qwen generally performing best and the ensemble strategy further improving robustness in functional-cue preservation. These results support the use of VLM scores as a scalable first-pass evaluation signal, while human annotations remain necessary for detailed calibration. 

VLM Sanity Check. To ensure our VLM-based semantic scoring relies on fine-grained functional geometry rather than merely global shape recognition, we conduct a controlled ge-ometric degradation experiment. For each of the three pre-defined hazard categories, we select four base meshes with the highest initial VLM scores. These meshes undergo three stages of systematic geometric corruption: Original, Mild, and Severe. Specifically, the modifications are as follows: • Structural Components(Key): Progressively truncating the key blade, which destroys its ability to fully insert into and match a lock cylinder. • Kinetic Weapons(Knucks): Filling the internal finger holes with structural blockages, which physically eliminates its wearability and gripping actionability. • Visual Threats(Gun): Attaching structures to the muzzle to break its deceptive realism, using a standard toy-like tip for the Mild version, and an exaggerated, toy-like geometric cone for the Severe version. As illustrated in Figure 14, the VLM’s predicted Hazard Lev-els exhibit a strictly monotonic decrease (e.g., dropping from 5 to 2 for the knucks) as the functional geometry is compro-mised. This confirms that our evaluation pipeline is highly sensitive to the local structural details that dictate a 3D ob-ject’s true real-world risk, aligning perfectly with human in-tuition.

## E Mitigation E.1 Input-level Filtering 

Dataset. We leverage our curated prototypes and multi-view input as positive samples (i.e., images of regulated objects) and further collect two types of negative samples: • Hard negatives : Benign objects—such as toy guns, multi-hole rings, and standard POS slots—that exhibit high vi-sual, topological, or structural similarity to these proto-types. Including these deceptive items desensitizes the model to superficial macro-shapes, forcing it to learn fine-grained discriminative features (e.g., material textures and subtle details) to effectively mitigate false positives. • General negatives : Random benign everyday objects sam-pled from ImageNet, used to broaden the decision bound-ary and improve generalization.

## E.3 Output-level Filtering 

Moderators. We adopt the following moderators: • Handcrafted Feature (Geo). We employ a set of global ge-ometric scalars that capture the physical properties of the 3D meshes without learning. We extract a feature vector consisting of fundamental descriptors, including the axis-aligned bounding box (AABB) extents, aspect ratio, sur-face area, and volume. Additionally, we compute the con-vexity index, defined as the ratio of the mesh volume to the volume of its convex hull. • D2 [26]. This method represents a 3D object as a prob-ability distribution of Euclidean distances between pairs of randomly sampled points on the mesh surface. Specif-ically, we sample N = 1024 points from the mesh sur-face and compute the pairwise distances for all distinct pairs. These distances are aggregated into a normalized histogram (typically 64 bins), forming a rotation-invariant signature of the shape. • PointNet [31]. We preprocess our meshes by sampling 

N = 4096 points uniformly from the surface faces. The ar-chitecture utilizes a convolution neural network with archi-tecture 3-64-128-1024 (augmented with 1D BatchNorm) to extract per-point features independently, followed by a symmetric function (max-pooling) to aggregate these lo-cal descriptors into a global feature vector. The features are fed into a lightweight MLP classifier with architecture 1024–512–256–2 (augmented with 1D BatchNorm). • MeshCNN [13]. Unlike PointNet, which discards topo-logical connectivity, MeshCNN defines convolution and pooling operations directly on the mesh edges. It uti-lizes feature-symmetric edge convolution to learn geomet-ric features invariant to the ordering of the edges, and em-ploys mesh pooling via edge collapse operations. The size of the hidden channel in the graph convolution network is set to 64. While building the graph, the max face is set to 4096. • Compact Latent [46]. It utilizes transformer-based VAE to encode 3D shapes as sets of 1D latent vectors, which include rich geometric information. To improve rotational invariance, we augment each mesh by rotating it by 90, 180, and 270 degrees about the vertical axis, yielding four views per object. The latents from these views are con-catenated and fed into an MLP classifier with architecture 1024–512–256–2, augmented with 1D BatchNorm. 

Training. This mix ensures the classifier learns fine-grained distinctions without over-triggering on innocuous shapes. All results are reported using 5-fold cross-validation to en-sure statistical reliability. For trainable models, we train for 20 epochs. 22
