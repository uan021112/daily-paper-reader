# Abstract 

3D semantic scene understanding has broad applications in digital twins, autonomous driving, smart agriculture, and embodied perception. However, dense point-wise annotation for point clouds is extremely expensive, making fully supervised 3D semantic learning di ffi cult to scale. Recent annotation-free methods can discover semantic regions without manual 3D labels, but they often su ff er from weak object-level consistency, ine ffi cient global grouping, and category-agnostic segmented regions. We propose an annotation-free 3D scene semantic understanding method based on multi-granularity distillation and graph-di ff usion-based segmentation. The proposed method first leverages structured visual knowledge guidance and superpoint graph di ff usion to perform e ffi cient global semantic propagation, alleviating the problem of inconsistent region-level semantics. It then conducts semantic inference through segmentation-cluster association, assigning interpretable category names to segmented 3D regions and improving the overall e ff ectiveness of annotation-free 3D semantic understanding. Extensive experiments on real-world datasets demonstrate the e ff ectiveness of the proposed framework. Compared with the advanced existing annotation-free baselines, our method improves oAcc, mAcc, and mIoU by 5.9%, 8.1%, and 2.4% at most, respectively. These results highlight the promise of the proposed framework for scalable annotation-free 3D scene understanding, especially in real-world scenarios requiring both object segmentation and semantic recognition. 

# 1 Introduction 

3D semantic scene understanding is a core problem in com-puter vision and graphics, with broad relevance to scene parsing, robotics, embodied perception, digital twins, and immersive con-tent creation. Point clouds acquired by LiDAR, RGB-D sensors, or multi-view reconstruction provide rich geometric observa-tions for this problem, and modern point-based networks such as PointNet and PointNet ++ have established e ff ective paradigms for learning on irregular 3D data [ 1 , 2]. Despite this progress, semantic learning in 3D remains fundamentally di ffi cult because point clouds are sparse, unordered, and often lack the appear-ance cues that make semantic reasoning easier in images. More critically, dense point-wise annotation is extremely expensive, making it unrealistic to rely on manual labels and di ffi cult to scale fully supervised 3D semantic learning. [3, 4]. To reduce the dependence on manual labels, previous re-search has explored annotation-free or label-free representation learning through clustering and pseudo-label discovery. In 2D, methods such as IIC and PiCIE show that meaningful semantic groupings can emerge without human annotations [ 5, 6 ]. In 3D, recent methods including GrowSP, PointDC, and LogoSP demonstrate that point clouds can be partitioned into semanti-cally meaningful regions using superpoints, cross-modal cues, or global grouping strategies [ 3, 4, 7]. Meanwhile, foundation models such as DINOv2, SAM, and SAM3 have made it in-creasingly attractive to inject strong 2D semantic priors into 3D pipelines, while works such as OpenScene and SAM3D further illustrate the promise of projecting multi-view visual semantics into 3D representations [8, 9, 10, 11, 12]. However, existing approaches still face three major lim-itations. First, point-level knowledge transfer alone does not explicitly enforce object-level consistency, which may lead to fragmented or inconsistent semantic regions. Second, global grouping in 3D often relies on costly or brittle graph process-ing, limiting its e ffi ciency and robustness for large-scale scenes. Third, the final 3D clusters typically remain category-agnostic, even when the geometric partition itself is meaningful. To ad-dress these challenges, we propose an annotation-free frame-work with three key components. Our contribution summary is as follows: 

• We introduce a multi-granularity distillation scheme that combines teacher features with segmentation-derived 3D mask grouping, allowing the 3D backbone to be constrained simultaneously at the point level, the mask-level prototype level, and the inter-prototype level. 

• We propose an e ffi cient superpoint graph processing method based on graph di ff usion, which performs global semantic propagation over superpoints and yields higher-quality region representations for cluster-ing and pseudo-label generation. 

• We further introduce an inference-time semantic asso-ciation strategy that matches predicted 3D clusters with segmentation-derived visual semantics projected into

# 2 Related Work 

Annotation-required 3D semantic segmentation. Early 3D semantic segmentation methods were mainly developed un-der fully supervised settings with densely annotated point clouds. Representative architectures include point-based and convolutional models such as PointNet and PointNet ++ [1 , 2], PointCNN [ 13 ], SPLATNet [ 14 ], TangentConv [ 15 ], and sub-manifold sparse convolutional networks [ 16 ]. Subsequent meth-ods improved scalability and contextual modeling through super-point grouping, sparse convolutions, and learned neighborhood aggregation, including SPG [ 17 ], Minkowski networks [ 18 ], Monte Carlo Convolution [ 19 ], DGCNN [ 20 ], KPConv [ 21 ], and RandLA-Net [ 22 ]. More recent transformer- and superpoint-based models, such as Point Transformer [23], Stratified Trans-former [ 24 ], and Superpoint Transformer [ 25 ], further enhance long-range geometric reasoning, while online parsing frame-works such as Fusion-aware Point Convolution [ 26 ] and Super-voxel Convolution [ 27 ] improve e ffi ciency and temporal consis-tency. Despite their strong performance, these methods rely on dense semantic labels and therefore remain di ffi cult to scale to annotation-scarce scenarios. 

Annotation-free point cloud learning without visual priors. 

To reduce annotation cost, many studies have explored label-free learning from geometry alone. In 2D, clustering-based methods such as IIC and PiCIE [ 5 , 6] showed that semantic structures can emerge without human labels, motivating related studies in 3D. Early 3D methods mainly focused on self-supervised rep-resentation learning, including PointContrast [ 28 ], OcCo [ 29 ], discrete generative pretraining [ 30 ], DepthContrast [ 31 ], Point-BERT [ 32 ], and MaskPoint [ 33 ]. Although these methods learn transferable 3D representations through contrastive, genera-tive, or masked modeling, they do not directly address scene-level semantic grouping. More task-oriented annotation-free segmentation methods have therefore been proposed, such as GrowSP [ 3], which performs semantic discovery through super-point growth and iterative clustering, and U3DS3 [ 34 ], which studies geometry-driven scene partitioning. Recent methods fur-ther improve category discovery through prototype or clustering-based reasoning, including P-SLCR [ 35 ], FreePoint [ 36 ], and SuperCluster [ 37 ]. However, geometry-only grouping remains limited when visually distinct categories share similar shapes or when semantically related regions exhibit large geometric variation. 

Annotation-free point cloud learning with visual priors. Acomplementary line of work injects semantic knowledge from pretrained 2D models into 3D learning. Image2Point [ 38 ], CLIP2Scene [ 39 ], and PointDC [ 4 ] transfer image priors by distilling or aligning 2D visual features with 3D represen-tations, while stronger visual teachers such as DINOv2 [ 8]further improve the quality of transferable embeddings. Lo-goSP [ 7] also introduces visual knowledge into annotation-free 3D segmentation by combining local superpoint organi-zation with global grouping cues. Another related direction exploits open-vocabulary or mask-based priors for 3D scene understanding. OpenScene [ 11 ] projects 2D open-vocabulary features into 3D, while OpenMask3D [40], Weakly Supervised 3D Open-vocabulary Segmentation [ 41 ], Open3DIS [ 42 ], and MaskClustering [ 43 ] combine 2D masks and 3D grouping for open-vocabulary or proposal-based scene understanding. SAM3D [ 12 ] and SAM-guided Graph Cut [ 44 ] further show that masks from strong 2D models can provide useful structural priors for 3D partitioning. These studies confirm the value of 2D foundation models for 3D perception, but they mainly focus on point-level feature transfer, open-vocabulary querying, or direct 2D-mask aggregation into 3D proposals. By contrast, our method builds a unified annotation-free framework that inte-grates mask-structured multi-granularity distillation, e ffi cient superpoint-level graph di ff usion, and inference-time semantic naming through segmentation-cluster association. 

# 3 Method 

3.1 Overview 

Our framework contains two training stages followed by a semantic inference step, and the entire pipeline is annotation-free in the sense that it does not rely on manual 3D point annotations. An overview of the pipeline is shown in Fig. 1. 

Stage I: Multi-granularity distillation. We project multi-view DINOv2[ 8] features into 3D to construct teacher features for visible points, and project SAM3[ 10 ] masks into 3D to obtain instance-aware mask groups. A sparse 3D backbone fθ is then trained with a multi-granularity objective that jointly enforces point-level semantic alignment, mask-level prototype consis-tency, and prototype-level discrimination. 

Stage II: Graph-di ff usion-based segmentation. The backbone learned in Stage I is used to initialize the segmentation model, providing semantically enriched point features for superpoint construction and clustering. These features are aggregated to superpoints, organized as a graph, and refined by graph di ff usion to produce globally smoothed region representations, which are further used for clustering and pseudo-label learning. 

Semantic inference. The segmentation model obtained from the first two stages can partition a point cloud into consistent clusters using point-cloud geometry and learned 3D features alone, but the resulting clusters do not inherently carry explicit semantic names. To recover semantic identities at inference time, we combine the predicted 3D clusters with SAM3-derived 3D mask labels projected from multi-view images, and assign each cluster a semantic category through point-level overlap aggregation and voting.

# 4 Experiments 

4.1 Implementation 4.1.1 Datasets and evaluation metrics 

We conduct experiments on two large-scale LiDAR semantic segmentation datasets: nuScenes [47 ] and Se-manticKITTI [ 48 ]. nuScenes contains 1,000 scenes with 32-beam LiDAR point clouds and 16 semantic classes. Se-manticKITTI provides 43,000 point clouds from 64-beam Li-DAR with 19 evaluation classes and 21 /11 train /test splits. Both datasets are evaluated using three standard metrics: overall ac-curacy (oAcc), mean accuracy (mAcc), and mean Intersection over Union (mIoU). 

4.1.2 Experimental settings 

Our framework is implemented in PyTorch and trained on a single NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU (24 GB). We use MinkowskiEngine [ 18 ] for sparse 3D convolution. 4.1.3 Baseline 

We compare our method with both annotation-required and annotation-free baselines. For annotation-required methods, we report representative supervised 3D segmentation models, in-cluding MinkUNet [ 18 ], Cylinder3D [ 51 ], and SPVNAS [ 50 ]on nuScenes, as well as PointNet [ 1], PointNet ++ [2 ], and SparseConv [ 16 ] on SemanticKITTI. These methods serve as su-pervised upper-bound references. For annotation-free baselines, we include generic clustering methods, including K-means [ 49 ], IIC [ 5], and PiCIE [ 6]. We further compare with geometry-driven or point-cloud-only annotation-free methods, includ-ing GrowSP [ 3], U3DS3 [ 34 ], and P-SLCR [ 35 ], as well as visual-prior-based methods, including PointDC [ 4], PointDC-DINOv2 [ 7 ], and LogoSP [ 7]. Among them, PointDC, PointDC-DINOv2, and LogoSP leverage image-derived cues, making them the most relevant comparisons to our setting. 

4.2 Quantitative Results of Comparative Experiments 

We evaluate our method against state-of-the-art approaches on nuScenes and SemanticKITTI validation datasets. Tables 1-2 present the overall performance comparison among annotation-free methods, while Table 3 reports several annotation-required methods as supervised references. On nuScenes, we achieve 60.7% oAcc, 31.2% mAcc, and 21.2% mIoU under the annotation-free setting, surpassing the second-best annotation-free method by notable margins. On SemanticKITTI, our method reaches 56.2% oAcc, 29.2% mAcc, and 17.7% mIoU under the annotation-free setting, establishing new state-of-the-art performance. The quantitative results demonstrate that our method consistently outperforms existing annotation-free meth-ods across both datasets.

4.4 Ablation Studies 

To validate the e ff ectiveness of the proposed design, we conduct ablation studies on nuScenes from two aspects: compo-nent ablation and loss-weight ablation. 

4.4.1 Component ablation 

Table 4 evaluates the e ff ects of the distillation strategy and the superpoint feature processing method. Rand denotes random initialization, P-distill denotes point-level distillation, and M-distill denotes the proposed multi-granularity distillation. For feature processing, PCA serves as the baseline without additional graph-based propagation, while GFT and Di ff usion introduce superpoint-level feature processing. The results show that both multi-granularity distillation and Graph Di ff usion are important to the final performance. With Di ff usion, M-distill improves oAcc, mAcc, and mIoU from 48.6%, 13.1%, and 8.7% under Rand to 60.7%, 31.2%, and 21.2%, respectively. Compared with P-distill under the same Di ff usion setting, M-distill further improves the three metrics by 3.8, 1.7, and 1.5 percentage points, indicating that mask-level and prototype-level constraints provide additional benefits beyond point-wise feature alignment. In addition, under M-distill, Di ff usion outperforms PCA and GFT across all three metrics, demonstrating its advantage in propagating semantic information over superpoint graphs. 

4.4.2 Loss-weight ablation 

Table 5 further studies the loss weights in the multi-granularity distillation stage, where λpoint is fixed to 1. Using only the point-level loss achieves 56.9% oAcc, 29.5% mAcc, and 19.7% mIoU. Adding the prototype alignment term im-proves oAcc and mIoU to 58.5% and 20.3%, showing that region-level prototype supervision helps enhance semantic con-sistency. When the prototype-level contrastive term is further introduced, the performance generally improves, with the default setting λproto = 1 and λnce = 0.3 achieving 60.7% oAcc, 31.2% mAcc, and 21.2% mIoU. A larger contrastive weight does not always bring further gains; for example, increasing λnce to 0.9 reduces mIoU to 18.4%. These results suggest that a moderate contrastive weight best balances intra-region consistency and inter-region discriminability. 

# 5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we presented an annotation-free framework for 3D semantic scene understanding that unifies semantic distil-lation, superpoint-based graph di ff usion, and semantic inference within a single pipeline. The proposed method is built on three Preprint – D istill , D iffuse , and Semanticize (DDS): A nnotation -F ree 3D S cene Understanding Based on Multi -G ranularity 

Distillation and Graph -D iffusion -B ased Segmentation 8key components: a multi-granularity distillation scheme for transferring structured 2D semantic priors into 3D features, an effi cient graph-di ff usion module for global superpoint-level se-mantic propagation, and a SAM3-cluster association strategy for assigning semantic identities to category-agnostic 3D regions at inference time. Extensive experiments on nuScenes and Se-manticKITTI show that our framework consistently outperforms existing annotation-free baselines and achieves state-of-the-art performance under the annotation-free setting. In the future, we plan to extend the framework to more general open-vocabulary and cross-domain 3D scene understanding settings. Another promising direction is to develop a tighter joint optimization of training-time grouping and inference-time semantic association, so that region discovery and semantic naming can be learned in a more unified manner.
