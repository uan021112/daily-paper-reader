Abstract. The real-world adoption of portrait relighting is hindered by dataset domain gaps, camera sensitivity, and computational costs. We address these challenges with Hybrid Domain Knowledge Fusion, a paradigm that fuses the specialized strengths of synthetic, One-Light-at-A-Time (OLAT), and real-world datasets into a compact model. Our approach features specialized prior models hardened by domain-aware adaptation, followed by augmented knowledge distillation into a lightweight student model with multi-domain expertise. Our method demonstrates a 

6× to 240 × inference speedup while maintaining state-of-the-art (SOTA) visual quality in the experiments. Additionally, we construct a massive, high-fidelity synthetic dataset with diverse ground-truth intrinsics to support our training pipeline. 

Keywords: Portrait Relighting · Knowledge Distillation · Domain Adaptation 

# 1 Introduction 

Video portrait relighting, which aims to re-illuminate subjects to match target High Dynamic Range Images (HDRI), has become an active area in academia and industry, driven by the escalating demand spanning from daily life like im-mersive telepresence and live streaming, to professional media production like cinematic visual effects. For real-world deployment, a relighting algorithm is required to be versatile and faithful to handle unconstrained subjects and en-vironmental lighting; robust to distinct cameras; and lightweight for real-time execution with temporal stability. The learning-based approaches have set new standards for visual quality. Re-garding the rendering process, the research landscape diverges into physically-based rendering and pure neural rendering. The former integrate explicit re-flectance models (e.g., Phong or Cook-Torrance BRDFs) in their architectures, while the latter is model-free, utilizing pure networks to learn relighting physics. Diffusion-based generative models in the pure neural category have rapidly risen to prominence [7, 22] due to their superb ability of synthesizing photorealistic

Because diffusion models are stochastic denoisers in essence, they are prone to corrupt fine-grained facial features and skin textures, leading to un-intended identity shifts and hallucinated semantics [19]. Furthermore, diffusion models struggle to model accurate physical light transport [18] and suffer from temporal flickering [10, 14] and high latency, precluding real-time adoption. Physically-based neural rendering methods [15, 24, 35] on the other hand ensure rigorous light transport process and excel at identity preservation. Yet, these methods are brittle in the wild due to data domain gap. The absence of paired in-the-wild captures with ground-truth intrinsic components forces the algorithms to rely on data from controlled environments (e.g., light stage or synthetic data). Consequently, these models exhibit poor extrapolation to diverse environmental lighting and real-world camera configurations, deteriorating when confronted with the sensor noise, lens glare, and motion blur inherent to real environments. To overcome these challenges, we introduce Hybrid Domain Knowledge Fusion . Rather than attempting to let a single model extrapolate from ho-mogeneous or mainly homogeneous data, we decouple the learning process into two steps. First, we develop specialized prior models that master specific domain knowledge of synthetic data, One-Light-at-A-Time (OLAT) data, and unlabelled real data. To dissolve the domain gaps, we introduce targeted augmentations to prior models against camera artifacts, novel albedo consistency constraints against ambient lighting, and a 2D Layer Normalization (LN) that stabilizes cross-domain statistics. Second, we fuse this multi-domain expertise into a single, lightweight student network via augmented knowledge distillation. This distilla-tion framework goes beyond model compression. It leverages massive real data to generate domain-routed rich pseudo ground truth regarding the characteristics of each prior, which effectively absorbs domain knowledge. Instead of relying on the raw outputs of the prior models, it applies asymmetric augmentation only to the input of the student model but not the prior model, for the purpose of going even beyond the robustness of prior models. In summary, our primary contributions are: 

– We propose Hybrid Domain Knowledge Fusion, a novel training paradigm that bridges the distinct domain gap through prior model training with do-main adaptation and augmented knowledge distillation. This results in a realistic, robust and compact model that is ready for real-world adoption. 

– We present the fastest video portrait relighting model, achieving real-time performance on consumer-grade edge hardware (e.g., NVIDIA RTX 2060) without compromising visual quality. 

– We construct a massive, high-fidelity synthetic portrait relighting dataset powered by NVIDIA Omniverse and Digital Human Technology, with rich demographic diversity and explicit intrinsic components, supporting our physically-based rendering pipeline. 

# 2 Related Work 

2.1 Deep Learning for Portrait Relighting 

Learning-based relighting can be classified into pure neural rendering and physically-based rendering. Pure neural approaches, particularly diffusion models, excel at synthesizing complex illumination and light scattering [7, 12, 22, 37]. However, their sequential denoising mechanism poses challenges for real-world applications due to heavy computation and temporal inconsistency [10, 14]. Furthermore, their stochastic nature frequently hallucinates semantic features [19], causing unintended identity shifts that preclude their use in fidelity-sensitive scenarios. In comparison, physically-based rendering approaches explicitly model light transport by decomposing portraits into intrinsic components (e.g., albedo, nor-mal) and applying established reflectance models [15, 24, 35]. This paradigm guarantees photometric linearity and excels at identity preservation. However, achieving high-fidelity intrinsic decomposition requires supervision from light 4 Q. Huang et al. 

stage captures or synthetic data. While accurate within these controlled distri-butions, these models suffer from a domain gap when extrapolated to highly diverse, unconstrained real-world captures. To meet the strict latency requirements of real-time applications, recent ar-chitectures employ simplified diffuse shading [20], low-order Spherical Harmon-ics [6], or lightweight encoder-decoder networks that heavily compress physical priors [28, 36]. While elegantly optimized for rapid inference, simplifying these rendering equations fundamentally compromises high-frequency lighting effects like specularities. Furthermore, the resulting acceleration is often still insufficient to guarantee stable, real-time performance on entry-level consumer hardware. 

2.2 Domain Adaptation and Knowledge Distillation 

To overcome computational bottlenecks without sacrificing visual fidelity, re-cent works have adapted Knowledge Distillation (KD) [13] to neural render-ing [3, 9] and lighting prior extraction [4]. However, applying generalized KD to physically-based portrait relighting presents a unique structural challenge: the massive domain gap between pristine synthetic training data and degraded real-world inference captures. Standard KD objectives become poorly conditioned when forcing a student network to map noisy, out-of-distribution inputs directly to representations learned exclusively from clean data, resulting in degraded relighting accuracy. To address this, we propose the first multi-teacher knowledge distillation framework uniquely designed to fuse heterogeneous data domains for physically-based relighting. By routing distillation through multiple domain-specific teach-ers and applying asymmetric linear-radiance augmentations, our approach trans-fers the precise physical accuracy of synthetic experts while explicitly hardening the student network against unconstrained real-world optical degradations. 

# 3 Method 

We introduce Hybrid Domain Knowledge Fusion, a paradigm that bridges the domain gap between isolated training datasets and unconstrained real-world de-ployment. The pipeline consists of two phases: training three specialized, cross-domain-aware prior models ( Pphys , Pref l , Preal ), and distilling their fused ex-pertise into a compact, real-time student model S.

Noise Augmentation. We adapt the noise-free synthetic data to mimic inputs from everyday cameras by introducing realistic noise NGAN . We create a noise dataset and train a generative network to produce noise for our augmentation. The dataset was built on a set of sequences of static scenes, acquired with differ-ent Logitech cameras, spatially registered to guarantee pixel accuracy, and tem-porally filtered to isolate the temporal noise component in sets of 8 × 8 patches. We use these additive noise patches to train a GAN [11] using UNet [27] to generate more noise samples to augment the training data. We apply identical noise profiles to both the input and the target ground truth during training. This forces the prior models to remain robust to noise rather than attempting to perform denoising.

Domain-Aware Layer Normalization. To mitigate the severe statistical variance encountered when fusing synthetic, OLAT, and real-world datasets, we adopt 2D layer normalization [21] across the network. Normalizing indepen-dently across the spatial and channel dimensions for each instance stabilizes cross-domain feature distributions and prevents statistical mismatch during in-ference. 

3.3 Augmented Knowledge Distillation 

The multi-domain expertise of the prior models is distilled into a single, highly efficient student network S.

Architectural Compression. We systematically compress S by reducing the channel capacity of the normal, albedo, shading, and rendering modules by a factor of four. Because supervised student training resolves the severe cross-domain batch variance handled during prior training, we adopt BN that favors hardware acceleration, except for albedo modules. These modifications drasti-cally reduce inference latency. 

Domain-Routed Pseudo-Ground Truth Supervision. To fuse the spe-cialized capabilities of the prior models, subsets of the training data are explic-itly routed to the prior model best suited for that specific domain. The selected teacher generates high-fidelity pseudo-ground truths of normals, albedos, diffuse and specular weights, light maps, and the final rendered output. 

Asymmetric Augmentation. To let the student model surpass the in-the-wild robustness of its teachers, the augmentation is performed asymmetrically during distillation. Specifically, the prior models receive the original (unaug-mented) input to generate clean pseudo-labels, while the student model is aug-mented using the degradations from Sec. 3.2 plus contrast, brightness, and am-bient color augmentation. This approach effectively allows our model to learn representations that are robust to artifacts. 

3.4 Network Architecture and Training Details Prior Models. Our prior models adopt the Phong-based network architec-ture [35], which decomposes the foreground into surface normal and diffuse albedo. The predicted normal is used to generate a diffuse light map and multi-ple specular light maps from pre-convolved environment maps [23]. A rendering network predicts per-pixel weights to fuse these light maps and estimates an ad-ditive residual for the final relit image. The original framework has three training 8 Q. Huang et al. 

stages: fully supervised pretraining on a virtual light stage dataset; unsupervised syn2real adaptation to refine the albedo with residual layers using real images and self-supervised lighting consistency losses; and temporal refinement through residual layers using optical flow. These networks are implemented using a UNet architecture. We replace the standard BN with 2D LN across all networks to en-sure statistical stability across domains. We also halve the channel capacity and residual block counts of [35] and employ a multi-resolution strategy: the normal network operates at 512 × 512 while all other modules process at 768 × 768 . We empirically observe that these modifications provide a 4× efficiency boost while maintaining comparable visual fidelity. All prior models are trained using the Adam [16] optimizer with 0.0001 learn-ing rate with 8 batch on 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs. Pphys is trained from scratch on our synthetic dataset for 100k iterations, optimized via L1, perceptual, and GAN losses applied to the predicted surface normals, diffuse albedo, and final RGB output, following the configurations in [24]. Distilled Student Model. The compressed student network S is trained for 125k iterations at 768 ×768 resolution. Optimization utilizes the Adam optimizer with a batch size 2 per GPU across 16 NVIDIA A100 GPUs and increased learning rate of 0.0002. # 4 Datasets 

To support the Hybrid Domain Knowledge Fusion paradigm, we construct a massive training database comprising over 640k synthetic renders, 200k OLAT renders, and 650k unlabelled real-world image and video sequences. 

4.1 Synthetic Dataset 

One of our contributions is a high-fidelity, large-scale dataset leveraging NVIDIA Omniverse and Digital Human Technology. To prevent dataset bias and ensure broad generalization across populations, we construct 800 avatars, 300 from Dig-ital Human Generator API and 500 from RenderPeople 1, with unique identities, grooming, clothing and accessories. We further introduce skin tone and texture perturbations to the digital human avatars. Subjects are illuminated under over 2100 HDRIs with randomized rotations to simulate diverse illumination. This includes 1608 real-world environments ac-quired from various databases, 471 synthetically generated HDRs with enhanced directional lighting, and 44 HDRs covering varied scenery and lighting scenarios. The data generation pipeline renders subjects using diversified camera loca-tion and poses, and exports perfectly pixel-aligned 3840 × 2160 RGB render-ings, alpha matting, diffuse albedos, and surface normals for over 340k samples through Omniverse Replicator extension as ground truth. Unlike prior work that relies on slower path tracers [35], our pipeline utilizes the NVIDIA RTX Real-Time engine. By explicitly enabling Subsurface Scattering and Deep Learning Anti-Aliasing, we simulate the complex light transport and scattering properties of human skin while maintaining extreme computational efficiency. To expand diversity, we supplement these renderings with an additional 300k samples rendered from TripleGanger scans at 512 × 512 .

4.2 OLAT Dataset 

We utilize raw light stage [8] captures comprising 320 diverse subjects, each recorded with 2 facial expressions using 7 frontal cameras and 164 LEDs. Train-ing pairs are synthesized via linear superposition of the OLAT captures [8] weighted by randomly rotated HDR environment maps. Explicit intrinsic supervision is extracted directly from the captures: surface normals are derived via photometric stereo [32], and diffuse albedos are approx-imated using images captured under flat omnidirectional lighting [24]. We adopt NVIDIA Maxine SDK 2 to extract the foreground image (same for real dataset). We hold out 32 identities to form our quantitative evaluation test set.

4.3 Real Dataset 

To capture the long-tail distribution of unconstrained identities, lighting, and imaging devices, we curate 22k real images from online videos and proprietary collections, spanning low-end webcams to professional cameras. We subsample 650k clips from two benchmarks, TalkingHead-1kH [30] and HD-VILA [34], to form our video dataset. They provide continuous motion, natural pose variations, and dynamic lighting changes for the student model to achieve robust video relighting. The evaluation set utilizes held-out frames from online videos and internal captures. 

# 5 Experiments and Results 

We evaluate our method along three axes: visual quality, computational effi-ciency, and design choices. For visual quality, we conduct quantitative and quali-tative assessments on held-out OLAT data (Sec. 4.2) with pixel-accurate ground truth and on unconstrained in-the-wild portraits under diverse HDR lighting. For efficiency, we benchmark inference latency against competing methods un-der matched hardware conditions. Finally, we perform ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each key component. 

Evaluation Metrics. Following established protocols [22, 24, 35], we re-port Mean Squared Error (MSE), Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR), Struc-tural Similarity (SSIM) [31], and Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS) [38]. All metrics are computed on the foreground region using pre-computed segmentation masks. 

Baselines. We compare against six recent portrait relighting methods: LU-MOS [35], DiffusionRenderer [17], UniRelight [12], IC-Light [37], SwitchLight v3 [15], and 3D-Aware Relighting [6]. For SwitchLight we use their released por-tal 3; all other methods use publicly available codebases. 3D-Aware Relighting operates on tightly cropped faces and does not relight the full upper body; it is therefore included in qualitative comparisons but excluded from the quantitative benchmark.

# 6 Conclusion 

We presented Hybrid Domain Knowledge Fusion, a training paradigm that ad-dresses three core barriers to real-world portrait relighting: the domain gap be-tween controlled training data and unconstrained deployment, sensitivity to di-verse imaging devices, and prohibitive computational cost. Our approach devel-ops three specialized prior models—physics, reflectance, and realism—each mas-tering a complementary data domain through targeted augmentations, albedo consistency constraints, and domain-aware normalization. These priors are then fused into a single compact student network via augmented knowledge distil-lation, which combines domain-routed pseudo-ground truth supervision with asymmetric augmentation to produce a model that is simultaneously accurate, robust, and fast. Experiments demonstrate that our method achieves state-of-the-art relight-ing quality on OLAT benchmarks while delivering 6× to 240 × speedups over existing approaches, reaching 84 fps on an entry-level RTX 2060 and exceeding 500 fps on an RTX 4090 at 512 × 512 resolution. We additionally construct a large-scale, high-fidelity synthetic dataset with pixel-aligned ground-truth in-trinsic components used in our training pipeline.

Across these settings, our method outperforms all baselines in two deployment-critical dimensions: identity preservation and illumination fidelity . Diffusion-based methods (DiffusionRenderer, UniRelight, IC-Light) frequently introduce specular artifacts, global color shifts, or flattened shading that alter facial ap-pearance or fail to match the directionality of the target environment; 3D-Aware Relighting exhibits identity drift (e.g., changes in skin tone, facial structure) as shown in Figs. 11 and 12; SwitchLight v3 yields plausible global illumination but over-smooths skin and hair texture and can shift clothing color (Figs. 10–11), producing a synthetic look. In contrast, our outputs preserve fine-grained identity cues—skin tone, facial geometry, and high-frequency texture—while faithfully adopting the target lighting. This behavior is consistent with our design: albedo consistency constraints and domain-aware training decouple intrinsic reflectance from illumination, and the distilled student retains this fidelity at real-time rates as reported in the main paper.

# C Additional Details on the Synthetic Dataset 

Our dataset is rendered utilizing the NVIDIA Omniverse Replicator extension. To ensure the model learns a highly generalized representation, the dataset com-prises an expansive manifold of identities. As visualized in Figure 13, our data generation strategy explicitly randomizes multiple core attributes in the real-world relighting task to maximize structural and photometric diversity: 

– Identity and Skin Tone: The dataset spans a comprehensive spectrum of ages, facial structures, and skin reflectance properties. To mitigate demographic bias, procedural shader-level perturbations are applied during generation, randomly sampling melanin density and subsurface redness parameters. 

– Grooming and Accessories: Subjects are rendered with a wide variety of hair and beard styles. We also introduce accessories that are common in daily life, such as varying styles of headsets and eyeglasses. 

– Attire and Textiles: We simulate clothing with diverse patterns and graphics by procedurally mapping textures onto the UV coordinates of the garment meshes. Specifically, we sample from a collection of 2,000 images across 8 categories sourced from OpenClipArt 5. These patterns act as alpha masks to modulate the diffuse albedo with randomized color palettes. 

– Poses and Illumination: Subjects are rendered across varying body poses from frontal to profiles to ensure rotation-invariant intrinsic estimation.
