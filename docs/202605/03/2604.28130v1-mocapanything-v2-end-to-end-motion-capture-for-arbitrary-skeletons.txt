1 Introduction 

Recovering 3D character animation from monocular video, com-monly referred to as motion capture, is a long-standing problem at the intersection of computer vision and computer graphics. While recent deep learning approaches have achieved strong performance for human motion capture [Goel et al . 2023; Kanazawa et al . 2018a; Kocabas et al . 2020; Pavllo et al . 2019], extending this capability to the arbitrary-skeleton setting remains fundamentally challenging. In this setting, the goal is to recover motion directly in the parameter space of a target rigged asset given only a monocular video and a reference skeleton [Gong et al . 2025]. The difficulty arises from the large variation in skeleton topology, joint count, and especially local coordinate conventions across different assets. A central challenge lies in recovering joint rotations. Unlike joint positions, rotations are defined with respect to a skeleton-specific coordinate system determined by the rest pose and local axis con-ventions. As a result, the same joint positions can correspond to different rotations under different skeletons, making direct video-to-rotation prediction prone to overfitting and poor generalization. To mitigate this issue, existing methods adopt a factorized design. A learned Video-to-Pose (V →P) network first predicts 3D joint positions, and an analytical inverse-kinematics (IK) solver then converts these positions into joint rotations. This design leverages the fact that joint positions are largely shared across skeletons performing the same motion, thereby improving generalization at the pose level. However, this factorization introduces a fundamental limitation. Since joint positions do not fully determine rotations, the pose-to-rotation mapping is inherently under-constrained, and analytical solvers cannot resolve degrees of freedom such as bone-axis twist, nor can they adapt to the noise distribution of predicted poses. Moreover, the non-differentiable IK stage prevents the two components from being jointly optimized, forcing the pose predictor to ignore the downstream rotation objective. In this work, we present the first fully end-to-end framework for arbitrary-skeleton motion capture from monocular video, in which both the Video-to-Pose and Pose-to-Rotation (P →R) stages are learnable and jointly optimized. The key challenge lies in the ill-posed nature of the P →R mapping: the same 3D joint positions can correspond to different rotation values under different rest poses and local coordinate frames, and therefore pose alone is insufficient to determine rotations, as it does not specify how the target skeleton defines its rotation coordinate system. To address this issue, we extend the reference signal. While prior work relies on static geometry (skeleton, mesh, and rendered views) [Gong et al . 2025], we additionally introduce a single ref-erence pose–rotation pair from the target asset, which is naturally available for any rigged skeleton. Together with the rest pose, this reference acts as an explicit coordinate-system anchor, turning the ill-posed P →R mapping into a well-constrained conditional predic-tion problem that can be modeled by a neural decoder. This design enables the P →R stage to be learned, and more impor-tantly, unlocks end-to-end training of the entire pipeline. Gradients from the rotation objective can now flow back through the pose intermediate and into the visual encoder, so that the pose repre-sentation is no longer optimized solely for positional accuracy, but is adaptively reshaped into a representation that better supports rotation recovery. In addition, we remove the mesh intermediate used in prior work [Gong et al . 2025]. While mesh representations can be ben-eficial when accurate, predicted mesh sequences often introduce noise and lead to error accumulation. By directly predicting joint positions from video, our approach improves robustness and signif-icantly reduces computational cost, achieving approximately 20 ×

faster inference. To support both stages, we introduce a skeleton-aware atten-tion mechanism, Global-Local Graph-guided Multi-Head Attention 

(GL-GMHA), which alternates between local kinematic-chain rea-soning and global cross-branch coordination. This design enables effective modeling of both structural constraints and global motion dependencies across diverse skeletons. We evaluate our method on the Truebones Zoo dataset, which covers seen, rare, and unseen skeletons, as well as the Objaverse benchmark. Our approach significantly improves rotation accuracy across all settings, especially on unseen skeletons, while also pro-viding substantial gains in efficiency. Our main contributions are as follows: 

• End-to-end learnable Video-to-Pose-to-Rotation frame-work. We present the first fully end-to-end trainable pipeline for arbitrary-skeleton motion capture, enabling joint opti-mization of pose and rotation. 

• Reference-conditioned rotation modeling. We intro-duce a reference pose–rotation pair that, together with the rest pose, defines the rotation coordinate system and re-solves the ambiguity of pose-to-rotation mapping. 

• Efficient direct video-to-pose prediction. We eliminate the mesh intermediate, improving robustness and achieving approximately 20 × faster inference. 

• Skeleton-aware attention mechanism. We propose GL-GMHA, which integrates local kinematic reasoning with global skeleton coordination in a unified attention frame-work. 

2 Related Work 

Recovering animation-ready motion from monocular video requires bridging the gap between observable visual cues (e.g., joint positions) and skeleton-specific motion parameters (e.g., rotations). Existing approaches address different aspects of this problem, but none fully MoCapAnything V2: End-to-End Motion Capture for Arbitrary Skeletons •

resolve the ambiguity of mapping pose to rotation under varying skeleton definitions. We review three related directions. 

2.1 Pose Estimation 

Pose estimation methods aim to localize anatomical keypoints from images or videos. Classic approaches include heatmap-based ar-chitectures such as HRNet [Sun et al . 2019] and regression-based methods, while recent transformer-based frameworks such as ViT-Pose [Xu et al . 2022b] and DETR-style models [Carion et al . 2020; Shi et al . 2022; Xiao et al . 2022] perform end-to-end keypoint pre-diction without hand-crafted grouping. To improve generalization beyond fixed categories, category-agnostic pose estimation (CAPE) methods [Hirschorn and Avidan 2023; Rusanovsky et al . 2024; Shi et al . 2023; Xu et al . 2022a] (e.g., POMNet, CapeFormer, Pose Any-thing, CapeX) predict keypoints for unseen objects by matching support and query representations. These approaches significantly improve cross-category generalization and are directly relevant to our setting, which also involves diverse skeleton structures. However, all these methods focus on keypoint localization and operate either in 2D or in a fixed keypoint space. They do not model 3D motion trajectories, temporal dynamics, or skeleton-specific parameterizations, and thus cannot directly produce animation-ready joint rotations. In particular, they do not address how the same joint configuration should be interpreted under different skeleton coordinate systems. 

2.2 Motion Capture 

Monocular motion capture has been extensively studied in the con-text of parametric human models such as SMPL [Loper et al . 2015] and SMPL-X [Pavlakos et al . 2019]. These methods regress pose and shape parameters directly from images or videos, using ar-chitectures ranging from optimization-based fitting (e.g., SMPLify-X) to feed-forward networks such as VIBE [Kocabas et al . 2020] and recent transformer-based models. While effective, these ap-proaches operate in a fixed parameter space and are tightly coupled to predefined skeletons, limiting their applicability to arbitrary ani-matable assets. Beyond humans, model-free reconstruction meth-ods (e.g., CMR [Kanazawa et al . 2018b], LASSIE [Yao et al . 2022], MagicPony [Wu et al . 2023], 3D-Fauna [Li et al . 2024]) recover de-formable surfaces without relying on explicit templates, and video-based extensions (e.g., BANMo [Yang et al . 2022], LASR [Yang et al .2021]) model temporal consistency. Model-based approaches such as SMAL [Zuffi et al . 2017] extend parametric modeling to specific ani-mal categories. However, these methods either lack explicit skeletal parameterization or remain tied to species-specific templates, and therefore do not generalize to arbitrary skeletons required for anima-tion. Recent work on arbitrary-skeleton motion capture addresses this limitation by conditioning on a reference asset. MoCapAny-thing [Gong et al . 2025] adopts a factorized design, where a learned Video-to-Pose network predicts joint positions and an analytical inverse kinematics (IK) solver recovers joint rotations. This decom-position improves cross-skeleton generalization by leveraging pose as a shared intermediate representation. Nevertheless, this design introduces a fundamental limitation. The mapping from joint positions to rotations is inherently under-constrained: the same pose can correspond to different rotations under different rest poses and local coordinate systems. Analytical IK solvers resolve this ambiguity using hand-crafted constraints, but cannot capture motion priors, resolve twist ambiguity, or adapt to noisy pose predictions. Moreover, the non-differentiable IK stage prevents end-to-end optimization, limiting the ability of the system to align intermediate pose representations with the final rotation objective. In contrast, we explicitly address the ambiguity of pose-to-rotation mapping by introducing a reference pose–rotation pair that defines the underlying coordinate system. This enables us to cast pose-to-rotation as a learnable conditional problem and train the entire Video-to-Pose-to-Rotation pipeline end-to-end.

Given an input video V = {𝐼 1, . . . , 𝐼 𝑇 } and a target skeleton S, our goal is to recover a sequence of 3D joint motion parameters that are consistent with the observed video and expressed in the parameter space of S. The target skeleton is defined by a joint hierarchy (parent indices 𝝅 ), bone offsets o ∈ R𝐽 ×3 (the rest pose), and per-joint semantic labels. We assume S is a tree -structured rigged skeleton with a single root; the number of joints 𝐽 and the topology are otherwise arbitrary. In practice, we set the maximum joint count to 150 to accommodate the largest skeleton in our training data (143 joints); this bound is purely a padding/memory choice and can be adjusted for other datasets. 3.2 Overview 

Unlike prior arbitrary-skeleton methods [Gong et al . 2025], which adopt a factorized design, a learned Video-to-Pose stage followed by a non-differentiable analytical IK stage for Pose-to-Rotation (See Fig. 2), our framework casts both stages as learnable neural modules and trains them jointly end-to-end (see Fig. 3). This unified design is what makes joint optimization of the full V →P→R pipeline possible; its technical enabler is a reference pose–rotation pair that resolves the ill-posedness of a learnable P →R mapping (§3.5). 3.3 Global-Local Graph-Guided Multi-Head Attention 

We adopt a topology-aware attention mechanism to encode skele-tal structure. Building upon Graph-guided Multi-Head Attention (GMHA) [Gat et al . 2025], we incorporate graph-derived joint re-lations, including distance and kinematic connectivity, to guide attention across arbitrary skeletons. To better capture articulated motion, we extend GMHA into a 

Global-Local variant (GL-GMHA) by alternating between two com-plementary attention patterns. Local layers restrict attention along kinematic chains to model intra-limb dependencies, while global layers allow full connectivity to capture cross-limb coordination and whole-body dynamics. We employ GL-GMHA as a shared structural backbone across both the Video-to-Pose and Pose-to-Rotation modules, ensuring consistent topology-aware reasoning throughout the pipeline. This design enables joint modeling of local constraints and global coherence without additional parameters, and naturally generalizes to diverse skeleton topologies. 

3.4 Video-to-Pose Module 

This module predicts joint positions from the input video, without relying on explicit geometric intermediates such as mesh or surface normals. While our experiments (§4.3) confirm that ground-truth mesh can improve pose estimation when available, predicted mesh introduces error propagation that degrades overall stability. We therefore adopt a purely vision-driven design. 

Reference query encoder. To ground the joint layout in the target skeleton, we first encode a reference frame , defined as a single frame for which the joint positions and corresponding image features are both known. The reference joint positions pref ∈ R𝐽 ×3 are mapped through a frequency-based positional embedding [Mildenhall et al .2020] and projected to a query dimension 𝑑 . Per-joint semantic embeddings, obtained by encoding joint names with the T5 [Raffel et al . 2020] text encoder, are added to provide category-agnostic joint identity; these embeddings serve as semantic positional identifiers and enable the model to generalize across skeletons with arbitrary joint counts and naming conventions. The output is a set of reference joint queries Qref ∈ R𝐽 ×𝑑 that encode both the skeletal structure and the visual appearance of the reference frame.

3.5 Reference-Conditioned Pose-to-Rotation Module 

3.5.1 The Ill-Posedness of Pose-to-Rotation Mapping. Recovering joint rotations from positions alone is fundamentally ill-posed. Ro-tations are always expressed relative to a skeleton’s rest pose and a choice of local coordinate frames. Geometrically, the rest pose fixes the origin of each joint’s local frame, but not its axes : the same positional trajectory can be expressed by many different rotation sequences under different axis conventions, even before account-ing for bone-axis twist (the rotation about a bone’s longitudinal axis), which joint positions do not constrain at all. Conditioning on rest pose alone, which is the static-geometry signal used in prior arbitrary-skeleton work [Gong et al . 2025], therefore supplies only the coordinate origin, and the learning target R = 𝑓 (P, o) remains multi-valued; directly learning it leads to unstable convergence and temporally inconsistent predictions. To make P →R learnable, we need an explicit axis anchor in addition to the rest-pose origin. In the next subsection we show that a single reference pose–rotation pair from the target asset provides exactly that. 

3.5.2 Reference-Conditioned Modeling. We extend the reference signal of prior arbitrary-skeleton work [Gong et al . 2025], which encodes only the target asset’s static geometry, with a single pose– rotation pair sampled from the same asset. Intuitively, rest pose supplies the coordinate origin while the reference pose–rotation pair supplies the coordinate axes : together they fully specify the local frame convention, converting the multi-valued P →R mapping into a well-constrained conditional prediction task. Concretely, we con-dition the rotation prediction on three sources of skeleton-specific information: 

(i) Rest-pose encoding. A Rest Pose Encoder takes the bone offsets 

o ∈ R𝐽 ×3 and per-joint semantic embeddings as input, and processes them through a stack of GL-GMHA layers to produce a rest-pose embedding Erest ∈ R𝐽 ×𝑑 that captures the static skeletal geometry and topology. • Gong and Wen, et al. 

(ii) Reference pose–rotation pair. A single reference frame, com-prising a known joint position configuration pref and its correspond-ing ground-truth rotation rref , serves as the coordinate-system an-chor. A Reference Encoder jointly embeds this pair by projecting the positions and 6D rotations into a shared feature space, adding the joint semantic embedding, and processing through GL-GMHA lay-ers conditioned on Erest via FiLM modulation [Perez et al . 2018]. This produces a reference feature Cref ∈ R𝐽 ×𝑑 that encodes how a spe-cific pose maps to rotations under the target skeleton’s coordinate conventions. Intuitively, the reference pair tells the model: “for this skeleton’s local coordinate definition, this particular joint configuration cor-responds to these rotations.” This single anchor suffices to resolve the coordinate-system ambiguity that makes the pose-to-rotation mapping ill-posed. We use a single reference pair by default; the effect of additional reference pairs is analyzed in our ablation study (§4.5). 

(iii) Pose encoding. The predicted (or ground-truth) joint position sequence P ∈ R𝑇 ×𝐽 ×3 is encoded by a Pose Encoder , which projects positions to the query dimension, adds semantic embeddings, and applies alternating GL-GMHA (for spatial reasoning) and per-joint windowed temporal attention with RoPE (for temporal modeling), optionally modulated by Erest through FiLM. The output is a pose feature sequence Q ∈ R𝑇 ×𝐽 ×𝑑 .

3.5.3 Rotation Decoder. The rotation decoder predicts per-frame, per-joint 6D rotations from the pose features, conditioned on the reference and rest-pose embeddings. It consists of 𝐿 decoder blocks, each containing: (1) FiLM conditioning on Erest , providing skeleton-specific modulation; (2) Per-joint temporal self-attention (windowed with RoPE) for temporal coherence; (3) GL-GMHA spatial attention (alternating local/global mask-ing) for cross-joint reasoning; (4) Per-joint reference cross-attention , where each joint attends to the reference feature Cref to retrieve rotation anchoring information from the coordinate-system anchor; (5) Feed-forward network with residual connection. The reference cross-attention is applied in the first 𝐿 cross ≤ 𝐿 decoder layers; the remaining layers rely on the already-integrated reference information. The final layer projects to the 6D rotation output via a two-layer MLP head. Joints flagged as rotation-static (determined independently from the position-static flag; see §3.4) are overwritten with the reference rotation, mirroring the static-joint handling in the pose stage. By conditioning on the rest pose, the reference pair, and the input pose jointly, the decoder converts the ill-posed rotation recovery problem into a well-constrained conditional prediction task. The model learns statistical motion priors from data, resolving under-constrained degrees of freedom (e.g., twist) that are fundamentally inaccessible to analytical IK. 

3.6 End-to-End Training Strategy 

Joint optimization. With P →R cast as a learnable neural mod-ule (§3.5), end-to-end joint training of the entire V →P→R pipeline becomes possible for the first time in arbitrary-skeleton motion capture. We train the Video-to-Pose and Pose-to-Rotation modules jointly: gradients from the rotation loss flow back through the pre-dicted pose and into the visual encoder, so the intermediate pose representation is reshaped not by a positional-accuracy objective alone, but by what best serves the final rotation objective. This is the key departure from factorized pipelines, whose non-differentiable IK handoff between V →P and P →R precludes any such co-adaptation; we quantify the effect in §4.4. 

Loss function. The total loss combines four terms spanning both the pose and rotation stages: 

L = 𝜆 pos Lpos + 𝜆 rot Lrot + 𝜆 rot_v Lrot_v + 𝜆 root Lroot , (2) where Lpos is the per-joint position error between predicted and ground-truth joint positions. For the rotation stage, Lrot measures the geodesic angular error between predicted and ground-truth ro-tations averaged over all joints, and Lrot_v penalizes the angular velocity difference to promote temporal consistency in the rota-tion sequence. Lroot additionally re-weights the root joint’s rotation error, which we found accelerates convergence of the global orien-tation. 4.4 Training Strategy Analysis: Validating the End-to-End Claim 

This experiment directly tests the central claim of our framework: making P →R learnable is not sufficient on its own; its benefit arises from enabling end-to-end gradient coupling with V →P. We there-fore compare four training regimes: a gradient-detached variant (where gradients from P →R do not flow back to V →P), end-to-end training with ground-truth poses only, end-to-end training with pre-dicted poses only, and our default mixed-pose end-to-end training. Results are shown in Table 3. The results yield three findings. First, enabling gradient flow is critical. Compared to the gradient-detached variant, joint training reduces the Zoo-Unseen angle error from 7.82 ◦ to 6.54 ◦, showing that the benefit of a learnable P →R module comes from end-to-end adaptation rather than standalone modeling. Second, GT-only training suffers from a distribution gap and fails on Zoo-Unseen (13 .28 ◦ vs. 6.54 ◦), while Pred-only training is unstable due to noisy early predictions. Finally, the mixed-pose strategy achieves the best balance. Varying the warm-up length ( 𝐸 𝑤 ) shows consistent perfor-mance across 𝐸 𝑤 ∈ [ 20 , 50 ], indicating that the method is robust to the mixing schedule. We use 𝐸 𝑤 =30 by default.

4.7 GL-GMHA Attention Analysis 

Table 6 evaluates the effectiveness of the Global-Local extension to graph-guided attention. The ablation confirms the value of the alternating design. Remov-ing graph biases entirely (“Full Attn”) yields the worst results on most metrics, as the model loses the structural inductive bias that encodes joint relationships. The original uniform GMHA [Gat et al .2025], which attends globally in every layer, is a strong baseline but does not explicitly model kinematic-chain locality. The “All-local” variant, which restricts attention to ancestor paths in every layer, can capture intra-branch dependencies but loses cross-branch coordination, with its angle error on Zoo-Rare jumping to 16 .91 ◦

and on Zoo-Unseen to 10 .37 ◦, well above Ours. Our alternating GL-GMHA achieves consistently lower angle error than uniform GMHA across all four splits, by interleaving the two attention pat-terns at every pair of layers, validating that kinematic-chain locality and skeleton-global coordination are complementary rather than redundant.

4.9 Cross-Attention Depth 

We study the effect of reference cross-attention depth ( 𝐿 cross ) in the rotation decoder, while keeping the overall model depth fixed to 8 layers (Table 8). Without cross-attention ( 𝐿 cross =0), the model fails on Zoo-Unseen (23.49 ◦), showing that reference condition-ing is essential. Applying it in all layers ( 𝐿 cross =8) brings no further gain and slightly degrades performance, indicating diminishing returns when over-conditioning the decoder. 

4.10 Efficiency Analysis 

Our efficiency gain comes from eliminating both the mesh prediction stage and the analytical IK solver. For a 120-frame input sequence, V1 requires approximately 40 s for feature extraction, 15 min for mesh reconstruction, 20 s for pose estimation, and an additional 

∼5 min for IK optimization, resulting in a total runtime of over 20 minutes. In contrast, our method retains the same feature extraction cost (40 s), but directly predicts pose and rotation in a single forward pass within ∼10 s, reducing the overall runtime to under 1 minute. This corresponds to a ∼20 × speedup in practice. The improvement stems from two design choices: removing the mesh intermediate eliminates a heavy and error-prone reconstruction stage, while re-placing analytical IK with a learned rotation decoder avoids iterative per-frame optimization and enables efficient batched computation. Importantly, this efficiency gain does not come at the cost of accu-racy. Averaged over Zoo-Seen/Rare/Unseen (Table 2), our method achieves an angle error of 10 .6◦, compared to 18 .9◦ for V1 with ground-truth mesh and 20 .1◦ with predicted mesh, demonstrating a favorable trade-off between speed and accuracy.

5 Limitations 

Our method has three main limitations. First, the Pose-to-Rotation decoder relies on motion priors learned from the training distribu-tion; for motions that lie well outside this distribution (for example, forcing a quadruped skeleton into a bipedal “arms-raised” pose it would never perform naturally), the decoder may produce implausi-ble rotations, even when the upstream pose prediction remains rea-sonable. Expanding the diversity of (skeleton, motion) combinations in the training data is the most direct path to addressing this. Second, the framework assumes a single foreground subject with minimal camera motion and does not explicitly handle heavy occlusion, since such cases are under-represented in our training data. Incorporating occlusion-aware data augmentation or a segmentation-conditioned visual backbone is a promising direction. Third, per-species cover-age in the training set is uneven (Truebones Zoo contains roughly 

1,000 sequences across only a few dozen species), so rotation quality on rarely seen species is bounded by data scarcity rather than by the modeling framework itself. 

6 Conclusion 

We have presented the first fully end-to-end framework for arbitrary-skeleton motion capture from monocular video, in which both Video-to-Pose and Pose-to-Rotation are learnable neural modules and are jointly trained. The enabler is a simple extension of the reference signal used by prior work: adding a single pose–rotation pair from the target asset, which anchors the coordinate axes of each joint’s local frame and thereby converts the ill-posed P →R mapping into a well-constrained conditional prediction task. Once P →R is learnable, joint training lets the intermediate pose representation reshape itself to serve the final rotation objective. This is something factorized pipelines, with their non-differentiable IK handoff, cannot achieve. On Truebones Zoo and Objaverse, our approach reduces the av-erage rotation angle error from ∼17 ◦ (V1’s factorized learned-V →P+ analytical-IK pipeline) to ∼10 ◦, with the largest improvements on unseen skeletons whose axis conventions rest-pose structure alone cannot disambiguate. Removing the mesh intermediate used by prior work as a video-to-joint bridge further yields ∼20 × faster inference, since predicted-mesh errors compound through the pipeline rather than providing useful information. We hope that this shift, from factorized learn-then-solve pipelines to a unified end-to-end learnable V →P→R, provides a useful foun-dation for future work in universal character animation and cross-skeleton motion understanding.
