Abstract. World models have made significant progress in modeling dynamic environments; however, most embodied world models are still restricted to 2D representations, lacking the comprehensive multi-view information essential for embodied spatial reasoning. Bridging this gap is non-trivial, primarily due to challenges from severe scarcity of paired multi-view data, the difficulty of maintaining spatiotemporal consistency in generated 3D geometries, and the tendency to hallucinate manipula-tion details. To address these challenges, we propose Embody4D, a dedi-cated video-to-video world model for embodied scenarios, capable of syn-thesizing arbitrary novel views from a monocular video. First, to tackle data scarcity, we introduce a 3D-aware compositional synthesis pipeline to curate a heterogeneous dataset compositing cross-embodiment robotic arms with diverse backgrounds, guaranteeing broad generalization. Sec-ond, to enforce geometric stability, we devise an adaptive noise injection strategy; by leveraging confidence disparities across image regions, this method selectively regularizes the diffusion process to ensure strict spa-tiotemporal consistency. Finally, to guarantee manipulation fidelity, we incorporate an interaction-aware attention mechanism that explicitly at-tends to the robotic interaction regions. Extensive experiments demon-strate that Embody4D achieves state-of-the-art performance, serving as a robust world model that synthesizes high-fidelity, view-consistent videos to empower downstream robotic planning and learning. 

Keywords: World Model · 4D Generation · Embodied AI

• We develop a compositional synthesis pipeline that combines cross-morphology manipulators with real-world backgrounds to create a diverse dataset, addressing data scarcity. 

• We design a confidence-aware adaptive noise injection strategy that modulates noise intensity based on regional confidence levels, ensuring rigorous spatiotem-poral consistency. 

• We introduce an interaction-aware attention mechanism using motion inductive biases to prioritize dynamic regions, preserving the physical fidelity of complex manipulations.

## 2 Related Works 

2.1 4D Generation 

Video-to-video 4D generation aims to synthesize temporally and spatially con-sistent dynamic 3D scenes from monocular videos, providing comprehensive en-vironmental representations for embodied intelligence and content creation [39]. Nevertheless, the advancement of this domain is significantly bottlenecked by the severe scarcity of training data. [30, 53]. Early approaches [56] like Dimen-sionX [44] employs LoRA fine-tuning to extend single-view videos into multi-view sequences but lacks the capability for precise viewpoint control. To mitigate data and camera control limitations, recent “warp-then-inpaint” paradigms [36], rep-resented by Trajectory Crafter [58], construct pseudo-4D data with camera in-formation from monocular videos to serve as generation conditions. Conversely, ReCamMaster [4] tackles these issues by synthesizing 4D data via game engines and optimizing the camera information injection mechanism. Inspired by these advancements, to address the scarcity of 4D data, we leverage the MuJoCo simu-lator to construct a dataset exhibiting high 4D consistency across diverse scenes and robotic arm morphologies. 2.2 Embodied World Models 

World modeling stands as a cornerstone of robotics, endowing systems with the capacity to comprehend, encode, and forecast the evolution of dynamic scenes [11]. As the core of interactive environment construction, 3D and 4D generation techniques are critical for bridging the gap between perception and decision-making, synthesizing high-fidelity dynamic scenes for embodied planning [28]. Most existing embodied world models are confined to single-view video pre-diction [63]. While recent studies acknowledge the necessity of multi-view per-ception and support generating synchronized head and wrist views [26, 33, 57], they typically rely on fixed multi-view reference frames during inference, thus falling short of flexible, arbitrary viewpoint generation. Furthermore, represen-tative works in 4D embodied world modeling, such as TesserAct [61], primarily focus on augmenting single-view generation with depth and normal maps for point cloud reconstruction. It fails to fully exploit the potential of 4D models for novel view completion and is severely restricted by limited robotic arm di-versity. In this work, we present a novel embodied 4D generation model with exceptional generalization across diverse robotic arm morphologies. It is capa-ble of directly synthesizing embodied videos from arbitrary viewpoints given a monocular video, providing robust support for downstream planning tasks. 

## 3 Preliminaries: Flow Matching Framework 

Flow Matching [32] provides a simulation-free approach for training continu-ous normalizing flows to model high-dimensional data distributions [14]. Un-like diffusion-based methods that implicitly define the generative process via 5

stochastic noise removal, Flow Matching explicitly regresses a vector field that transports a simple prior distribution p0 (e.g., Gaussian noise) to the complex data distribution p1 (e.g., video latents) via an Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) [43]. Specifically, the Rectified Flow formulation [35,49], which constructs the transport path as a straight line. By minimizing the curvature of the genera-tive trajectory, this formulation allows for stable and efficient coupling between noise and data. Formally, let x1 denote the target video latent representation and x0 ∼N (0, I) be the initial noise drawn from a standard Gaussian distribution. The forward process is defined as a linear interpolation between noise and data: 

xt = tx1 + (1 − t)x0, t ∈ [0 , 1] , (1) where t represents the continuous timestep. This linear formulation implies a constant ground-truth velocity field vt over time:

## 4 Methodology 

4.1 Compositional 4D Embodied Data Synthesis 

Current 4D generation is bottlenecked by data scarcity [39], particularly the absence of global multi-view observations in the embodied domain. Existing datasets are largely confined to static head and dynamic wrist perspectives [7, 45], which are inadequate for robust generation. Moreover, the homogeneity of robotic arms in these datasets severely limits cross-embodiment generalization. To overcome this limitation and enable generalized 4D generation for multi-ple robotic manipulators, we present a simple yet effective strategy for embodied 4D data composition. Specifically, as shown in Fig. 2 (left), we first selected 30 embodied models from the MuJoCo Menagerie [59], covering humanoid robots, single-arm, dual-arm manipulators, and small grippers. For foreground gener-ation, we controlled the models within the MuJoCo environment to execute random movements within a defined range, starting from their default poses. The background data was constructed from the DL3DV dataset [31] using a 6 F. Author et al. 

random sampling strategy: image pairs were initially filtered from fixed-interval candidates by minimizing camera translation, and subsequently refined using GPT-4o [1] to verify center visibility in subsequent frames, ensuring high tem-poral consistency. To ensure high-fidelity 4D consistency, we configure dual vir-tual cameras in MuJoCo that rigorously align with the extrinsic parameters of background image pairs sourced from DL3DV, capturing the full motion of the robotic arm to obtain view-consistent foreground-background data. For the pri-mary reference view, the robotic arm can be composited into the background with arbitrary rotation and scaling. 4.2 Confidence-aware Adaptive Noise Injection Strategy 

Most existing 4D generation methods overlook the inherent spatial variations in reliability when applying priors warped from reconstructed dynamic point clouds (from source to target views) [36,58,62]. In low-confidence regions, such as occlu-sions and boundaries, over-constraining the model with inaccurate warp priors 7

encodes flawed spatial mappings, creating persistent artifacts that compromise the restorative capacity of the generation process. Conversely, in high-confidence visible regions, the uniform sampling process of standard diffusion models intro-duces unnecessary stochastic fluctuations, which degrades sharp textures and rigid structures, thereby undermining consistency. To address this, we introduce a confidence-aware adaptive noise injection optimization mechanism that performs spatial scheduling based on the relia-bility of different warped regions. Specifically, it applies lower noise levels to high-confidence areas to ensure texture locking, while injecting higher noise into low-confidence regions to grant greater generative freedom. Through this confidence-driven differentiated processing, we successfully balance the struc-tural authenticity of the robotic arm with the overall continuity of the visual generation. Firstly, we introduce a lightweight confidence estimation module designed to capture an accurate confidence distribution. The module operates on two inputs obtained via point cloud reprojection: the warped latent features Zwarp and the geometric mask Mgeo . To mitigate the boundary information loss caused by naive resizing, as well as the domain gap introduced when encoding binary masks using an RGB-trained VAE, we employ a network f to synthesize a continuous, multi-channel confidence map C:

C = f (Zwarp , Mgeo ). (8) Given the confidence map C, we apply spatially adaptive initial noise with varying intensities across regions of different confidence levels. Specifically, we define the location-dependent spatial noise schedule as: 

Σt = [ σlow + C ⊙ (σhigh − σlow )] · t,  (9) where σlow and σhigh control the flow behavior: σlow allows maximum stochastic-ity in low-confidence (unknown) regions to encourage generative diversity, while 

σhigh enforces minimum noise in high-confidence (known) regions to preserve structural fidelity. Building upon the foundational Flow Matching formulation defined in Sec. 3, we construct the flow state xt as a convex combination of the clean target x1

4.3 Embodied Interaction-Aware Attention 

For the task of embodied 4D generation, the dynamic evolution of the robotic arm and interacting objects constitutes the core of visual semantics. To address the geometric distortions prone to occurring in high dynamic regions during novel view synthesis, we propose a Embodied Interaction-Aware Atten-tion tailored for embodied settings. Moving beyond traditional approaches that process global features indiscriminately, this module introduces segmentation models as an explicit motion inductive bias to enforce the attention mecha-nism to redistribute attention specifically toward the high-dynamic interaction regions [5]. This strategy effectively decouples the interaction foreground from background noise and anchors the synthesis in motion priors, thereby preserv-ing the spatiotemporal integrity of interaction entities across drastic viewpoint transformations. To compensate for the absence of object manipulation in our synthetic data, we incorporate a monocular robotic manipulation dataset and construct pseudo-4D pairs via a warp-then-inpaint pipeline. We adopt an adaptive strategy for foreground mask extraction. For simulation or stable sequences, we utilize Mem-Flow [12] to estimate optical flow. Conversely, for real-world footage where camera jitter introduces noise, we leverage SAM3 [8] for robust video object 9

segmentation. Both approaches ensure the generation of temporally consistent masks that encompass the robotic arm and manipulated objects. These fore-ground masks trace the spatiotemporal trajectory of both the robotic arm and the manipulated objects, preserving their geometric consistency. This explicitly indicates which regions of the reference video the model should focus on during generation. For our synthetic data, the arm foreground is directly accessible from the rendered scenes, providing clear supervision on the motion-relevant regions. Upon extracting the interaction region masks, we enhance the perception of critical robotic manipulation components by reshaping the distribution of attention maps. This explicitly steers the model’s focus toward these regions, ensuring geometric consistency under dynamic interaction states. ## 5 Experiments 

5.1 Experimental Setups Dataset. To train this model, we initially utilize a curated dataset of 23K 4D samples synthesized via foreground-background composition, enabling the model 10 F. Author et al. 

to learn diverse robotic arm morphologies and enhancing its 4D consistency. Sub-sequently, we leverage 24K monocular embodied data uniformly sampled from five datasets (AGIBOT [7], Rh20t [15], Robset [6], Bc-z [23], and Interndata-A1 [45]) to learn real-world robotic arm interactive operations. We evaluate our method on a test set of 120 monocular videos, consisting of 70 samples synthesized via our composition-based approach to include di-verse robotic arms, and 50 real-world samples curated from various open-source datasets. Each video contains 49 frames with a resolution of 384 × 672. 

Implementation. We fine-tune Embody4d based on the pretrained Wan2.1-T2V-1.3B [49] architecture. During training, the frame resolution is fixed at 384 ×672, and the video length is set to 49 frames. Both training stages use a batch size of 4 and are conducted on 8 A100 GPUs. We apply vggt [50] as the reconstruction foundation model for estimating camera parameters and depth maps. Please refer to the supplementary material for more details. 

Baselines and Metrics. For a fair comparison, we evaluate our approach against several state-of-the-art baselines, including camera-controlled video gen-eration models and point cloud-rendered multi-view generation models, i.e., Tra-jectoryCrafter [58], ReCamMaster [4], Ex-4D [19], and Reangle-A-Video [25]. For video generation evaluation, we strictly follow the VBench [21] protocol, reporting scores across multiple dimensions: Subject Consistency, Background Consistency, Temporal Flickering, Motion Smoothness, and Imaging Quality. In addition, we evaluated the Visual Quality metric on the Q-Align [54] benchmark. Furthermore, to assess the practical utility of our generated data, we conduct real-robot experiments. Specifically, we use videos generated by different mod-els to fine-tune the current state-of-the-art π0.5 VLA policy [16, 22]. The final grasping success rate on the real robot is used as the primary metric to quan-tify generation quality through downstream task performance. For real-world experiments, we compare our method with two strong generative baselines, Tra-jectoryCrafter and ReCamMaster, which achieve relatively high visual quality among existing methods.

## 6 Conclusion 

In this work, we have presented Embody4D, a novel video-to-video 4D world model designed for Embodied AI that enables the synthesis of arbitrary novel views from monocular videos. To tackle severe data scarcity, We introduce a compositional synthesis pipeline to composite diverse robotic manipulators with real-world backgrounds, fostering broad generalization. Furthermore, we devise a confidence-aware adaptive noise injection strategy that modulates noise in-tensity based on regional confidence levels, thereby ensuring rigorous geometric and temporal consistency. Moreover, we incorporate an interaction-aware atten-tion mechanism guided by motion priors, which effectively preserves the physical 15 

integrity of complex robotic interactions. Finally, extensive experiments demon-strate that Embody4D achieves state-of-the-art performance, while real-world evaluations confirm its efficacy in empowering downstream robotic planning and policy learning.

B.1 Dataset Curation 

When rendering the robotic arm foreground in the MuJoCo [46] simulation envi-ronment, we ensure that the simulated motion closely mimics real world dynam-21 Source View Target View Source View Target View Source View Target View

ics by strictly constraining the movement magnitude. Specifically, each move-ment step size is set to one hundredth of the total range of motion for each joint. To maintain spatial consistency, the camera placement within the simulation environment must strictly adhere to the extrinsic parameters of the target back-grounds intended for compositing. This process necessitates a precise coordinate transformation to map the OpenGL coordinate system used in the DL3DV [31] dataset into the XML format required by MuJoCo, which is defined by specific values for distance as well as azimuth and elevation angles. Our empirical evaluation demonstrated that employing 49 frame sequences with independent camera and background poses leads to suboptimal perfor-mance. The compounding motion of both the foreground robotic arm and the 22 F. Author et al. 

background camera generates highly complex data that hinders the ability of the model to achieve convergence. To mitigate this issue, each data sample consists of two separate videos captured from two distinct and fixed camera poses. To prevent excessive parallax which might cause the robotic arm to drift out of the frame, we utilize the GPT-4o [1] model to evaluate and select background pairs that maintain a moderate level of parallax. As illustrated in Fig. 10, our training pairs are annotated by concatenating the identifier of the robotic arm with a detailed background description to form the final text prompt. This composition based approach yields more precise labels than the direct annotation of the final composited video sequence. For this purpose, we employ the CogVLM2 Llama3 Caption model [17] as our primary labeling tool to ensure high quality descriptive metadata. 

B.2 Acquisition of Interaction Priors 

When utilizing the interaction-aware attention mechanism, it is first necessary to obtain a motion prior bias. For data captured in real world scenarios, cam-era movement often introduces a certain degree of jitter, which in turn leads to noise in the optical flow estimation [12]. In cases where the noise level is exces-sively high, we directly employ the Segment Anything Model 3 [8] to perform segmentation. The input prompt for this process is defined as “robot arm and objects”. As illustrated in Fig. 11, we provide several examples of the resulting optical flow estimations alongside the segmentation masks generated by the Seg-ment Anything Model 3, all masks will be normalized to the range of 0 to 1 for calculations. 

B.3 Training Implementation Details 

We implement our Embody4D using the Wan2.1-T2V-1.3B diffusion transformer as the generative backbone. All parameters are fully fine-tuned in an end-to-end manner. During training, the frame resolution is fixed at 384 × 672 , with a sequence length of 49 frames. We adopt a two-stage training strategy. In the first stage, the model is trained on our self-constructed combinatorial real-world 4D dataset to enhance tempo-ral consistency. In the second stage, we freeze the interaction-aware blocks and utilize pseudo-4D data, generated by projecting interaction data onto arbitrary perspectives and back-projecting them to the initial view. This augmentation enables the model to generalize effectively to complex real-world interactions. Finally, all model parameters are fully fine-tuned in an end-to-end manner. – Stage 1: Conducted for 33k iterations with a learning rate of 1 × 10 −5.

## D Limitations and Future Works 

While our approach demonstrates significant potential, several challenges re-main. First, the inherent constraints of the generative model lead to degraded performance in scenes involving substantial viewpoint variations. Second, the current 4D generative paradigm relies heavily on temporal continuity; when sim-ulating a fixed-view camera for downstream tasks, the model must synthesize the target viewpoint directly rather than transitioning smoothly from the initial frame. This spatiotemporal discontinuity often compromises generation qual-ity. Finally, inference efficiency remains a bottleneck, as generating a sequence of 49 frames currently requires approximately two minutes, failing to meet the requirements for real-time deployment. Future research should prioritize optimizing performance under extreme view-point shifts and and fixed viewpoints while improving computational efficiency. Regarding model training, beyond the multi-view fine-tuning employed here, future work could incorporate large-scale multi-view data during pre-training.
