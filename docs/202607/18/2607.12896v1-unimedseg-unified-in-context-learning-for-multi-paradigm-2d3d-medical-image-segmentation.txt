# UniMedSeg: Unified In-Context Learning for Multi-Paradigm 2D/3D Medical Image Segmentation 

Yunzhou Li *, Jiesi Hu *, Yanwu Yang, Hanyang Peng, Chenfei Ye, Jianfeng Cao, Yixuan Yuan and Ting Ma †,

Senior Member, IEEE 

Abstract — Medical image segmentation foundation mod-els are expected to generalize across diverse clinical sce-narios, yet existing universal methods remain fragmented by prompt paradigms and spatial dimensions. Visual in-context learning, interactive segmentation, and language-guided segmentation are typically handled by paradigm-specific models, while 2D and 3D images are also modeled separately. Such isolation prevents heterogeneous anno-tations and data from being jointly absorbed by a sin-gle scalable model and limits cross-paradigm knowledge transfer. To address this bottleneck, we propose UniMed-Seg, a Transformer-centric universal segmentation frame-work that maps visual examples, geometric interactions, language instructions, and 2D/3D images into a shared sequence space, enabling heterogeneous medical super-vision to be jointly learned through a unified in-context interface without prompt- or dimension-specific branches. To overcome the long-sequence memory bottleneck caused by visual contexts, we introduce Decoupled Split Attention, which reduces attention complexity to linear while preserv-ing hardware-friendly computation and focused context-target interaction. Extensively trained and evaluated on a large corpus curated from 27 public datasets, UniMed-Seg achieves state-of-the-art performance across visual in-context, interactive, and language-guided segmentation without task-specific fine-tuning, demonstrating strong generalization on diverse held-out tasks. I. I NTRODUCTION 

Medical image segmentation is fundamental to computer-aided diagnosis and digital healthcare, supporting clinical decision-making through precise delineation of anatomical and pathological structures [1], [2]. Given complex clinical variations and cross-center domain shifts, universal foundation models are increasingly important for robust generalization and zero-shot or few-shot adaptation across diverse medical scenarios [1], [3]. Foundation models are expected to benefit from large-scale and diverse training data, which is essential for improving generalization and task versatility across clinical scenarios. However, medical image segmentation data are heterogeneous not only in imaging modality and anatomy, but also in supervision format and spatial dimension. Existing universal segmentation methods are typically developed around specific paradigms, such as visual in-context learning (ICL) with image-label pairs [3], [4], interactive segmentation with ge-ometric prompts [1], [5], and language-guided segmentation with textual instructions [2], [6]. In addition, 2D slices and 3D volumes are often handled by dimension-specific architectures. Although Medical SAM3 adopts a 2D backbone to process 3D images as sequences of 2D slices [7], it does not natively model the complete 3D spatial structure, making it difficult to learn from volumetric data. These paradigm- and dimension-specific designs restrict each model to limited input interfaces and application scenarios, fragmenting heterogeneous supervi-sion across isolated models. Consequently, diverse annotated data cannot be jointly absorbed by a single scalable foundation model, limiting both its scaling potential and its ability to generalize across tasks, supervision formats, and spatial di-mensions. To overcome this bottleneck, a universal medical segmen-tation framework should absorb heterogeneous supervision through a unified in-context learning interface. This echoes the broader trend in general AI, where Transformer-based archi-tectures and sequence interfaces serve as scalable backbones arXiv:2607.12896v1 [cs.CV] 14 Jul 2026 2 IEEE TRANSACTIONS ON MEDICAL IMAGING 

for diverse tasks and multimodal inputs. Under this view, visual examples, geometric interactions, language instructions, and 2D/3D images can be transformed into contextual se-quences, allowing heterogeneous contexts to be jointly trained and processed across paradigms and spatial dimensions [8]. However, flattening high-resolution medical images, especially 3D volumes, together with multiple image-based contexts into a single sequence leads to quadratic computational complex-ity and substantial memory consumption, which need to be addressed. Built upon this formulation, we introduce UniMedSeg ,a Transformer-centric foundation model for unified medical image segmentation across paradigms and spatial dimensions. UniMedSeg discards prompt- and dimension-specific local branches, and instead maps visual images, dense masks, geometric guidance, and language tokens into a standardized sequence interface processed by a shared Transformer back-bone. This enables joint representation learning from heteroge-neous 2D/3D data and diverse supervision formats, supporting scalable training on 27 public datasets and 20,000 synthetic 3D volumes. To handle the rapidly growing visual-token length caused by multiple image-based contexts, especially 3D volumes, we propose Decoupled Split Attention, which reduces long-context attention complexity to linear while pre-serving hardware-friendly dense computation. Together with tailored type and position embeddings, this design promotes focused context-target interaction, enabling effective context-based prediction and fine-tuning-free generalization to unseen tasks. Our contributions are summarized as follows:  

> •

We present UniMedSeg, a Transformer-centric foundation model that unifies heterogeneous 2D/3D images, dense masks, geometric prompts, and language instructions as contextual inputs within a shared sequence interface. This design enables diverse medical supervision signals to be jointly absorbed for scalable representation learning, without relying on paradigm-specific model branches.  

> •

We propose Decoupled Split Attention for scalable long-context learning. It reduces the attention complexity of long visual-context sequences to linear, preserves hardware-friendly dense computation, and promotes fo-cused context-target interaction together with tailored type and position embeddings.  

> •

Extensive evaluations on a large corpus curated from 27 public datasets and 20,000 synthetic 3D volumes demon-strate that UniMedSeg achieves state-of-the-art perfor-mance across multiple segmentation paradigms. Without task-specific fine-tuning, it exhibits strong generaliza-tion under cross-center domain shifts, unseen anatomical structures, and cross-species targets. 

II. R ELATED WORKS 

A. Universal Medical Image Segmentation 

Traditional medical image segmentation has been long dominated by U-Net topologies [9], [10]. However, these architectures rely on static input channel configurations and localized receptive fields [9], [10], rendering them fundamen-tally incapable of natively ingesting multi-modal prompts with highly variable dimensions (e.g., 1D text embeddings vs. 3D spatial masks). While the field is shifting toward universal foundation models [1], [8] driven by scalable, Transformer-centric backbones (e.g., EVA [11], Primus [12]), early medical Transformers still implicitly treat images and prompts as dis-tinct tensor entities [13]. A critical scaling bottleneck remains: the extreme heterogeneity of medical annotations [14]. Clinical data is fragmented across dimensions (2D/3D) and supervision formats (dense masks, geometric prompts, text). Conventional architectures fail to absorb these disparate signals without relying on format-specific, hard-coded local branches [15]. Moreover, whenever a new prompt format or imaging modality is introduced, these static architectures require localized struc-tural modifications and isolated retraining. This rigid paradigm not only wastes computational resources but also prevents the network from mining deep, cross-modal synergies, severely restricting the scaling potential of medical foundation models on diverse clinical datasets. 

B. Unified Multi-Paradigm Segmentation 

Existing universal models typically resolve multimodal in-puts through isolated, structurally asymmetrical fusion mech-anisms. The first paradigm, ICL [3], [16]–[20], adapts to novel tasks via support image-label pairs. Furthermore, many previous ICL models are built upon convolution-centric back-bones, inherently limiting their capacity for scaling up to massive datasets. Moreover, these models either concatenate support masks along the channel dimension or utilize special-ized cross-attention bridges, restricting them to dense visual guidance while rejecting high-level semantic reasoning. Cru-cially, extending this naive concatenation to 3D volumetric data leads to an intractable quadratic explosion in memory and computational complexity, making multi-example long-context learning nearly impossible for high-resolution anatom-ical structures. The second paradigm, interactive segmentation (e.g., Med-SAM [1], SAM-Med3D [21], nnInteractive [22], Multi-verSeg [23]), relies on dedicated, structurally distinct prompt encoders to process geometric primitives [5], [24]. This hard-coded decoupling forces geometric features to interact with visual features only at specific network bottlenecks, preventing early-stage deep feature alignment. For instance, mapping a sparse 3D bounding box or point prompt to complex volu-metric boundaries requires profound spatial reasoning, which is severely bottlenecked if the prompt and image features are processed in silos. The third paradigm, language-guided segmentation [2], [25]–[28], typically employs asymmetrical cross-attention (e.g., text as queries, images as keys), intrinsically treating text as an auxiliary condition rather than a peer contextual repre-sentation. This asymmetrical treatment makes it particularly challenging to establish robust feature alignments between global semantic descriptions and fine-grained, localized 3D anatomical structures. To fully unlock scaling potential, a radical architectural departure is required: discarding asymmetrical branch configurations in favor of a unified sequence space. By treating all heterogeneous signals, including vision, ge-ometry, and natural language, as homogeneous tokens within a single, permutation-invariant sequence, the model natively ensures equal-footing cross-paradigm knowledge transfer. III. M ETHOD 

A. A Transformer-Centric Architecture 

While traditional U-Net architectures excel at task-specific segmentation, their rigid topologies and inductive biases limit their adaptability to heterogeneous inputs and spatial di-mensions. To address this, UniMedSeg discards the U-Net backbone in favor of a highly scalable, Transformer-centric architecture inspired by Primus [12] . As illustrated in Fig. 1, spatial inputs (target images, visual examples, and prompts) are processed by 3D/2D encoders and augmented with learnable type embeddings, whereas lan-guage instructions are encoded via BioMedBERT and an MLP adapter. These tokens form a unified global sequence. Follow-ing distinct Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE) strategies, the sequence is processed by a Transformer encoder stacking N

SplitEvaBlocks based on EVA [11] to stabilize training and ensure cross-modal alignment. Finally, target tokens are sliced, reshaped, and passed through a lightweight 2D/3D decoder to reconstruct the final prediction [12].

C. Unified 2D/3D Representation 

To unify 2D and 3D processing without training separate models, UniMedSeg employs a dynamic routing mechanism at the patch embedding stage. Given a 2D input X ∈ RC×H×W

or a 3D input X ∈ RC×Z×H×W , dimension-specific, single-layer, non-overlapping 2D and 3D convolutions ( P2D and 

P3D ) tokenize X into a flattened sequence z ∈ RN ×Demb ,where N and Demb denote the sequence length and embedding dimension, respectively. Once tokenized, both modalities share an identical Transformer trunk to maximize weight utilization and learn dimension-agnostic, generalized anatomical features. Symmetrically, lightweight dimension-specific Convolutional Transpose decoders (e.g., three transposed layers for a patch size of 8) explicitly reconstruct the spatial features back to their native resolutions. 

D. Multimodal Alignment: Type Embedding and RoPE Identity 

To integrate heterogeneous tokens within a shared sequence, UniMedSeg provides explicit structural priors to the model, reducing the difficulty of learning complex token associations from scratch. Specifically, we define token identity and spatial correspondence through Type Embedding and RoPE, respec-tively. 

Type Embedding. We introduce three learnable type embed-dings e0, e1, e2 ∈ RDemb , which are added to context images, context labels or spatial guidance maps, and target images, respectively. Language tokens are pre-extracted semantic fea-tures from a pretrained encoder. Therefore, we do not assign additional type embeddings to them.

IV. E XPERIMENTS 

A. Datasets 

Training and Validation Datasets. Among them, 21 datasets [14], [31]–[45], comprising 11,397 3D scans, were used for training and validation with a random 9:1 split. This corpus covers diverse modalities, including T1, T2, FLAIR, MRI/MRA, PET, and CT, as well as major anatomical regions such as the brain, abdomen, prostate, and heart. To further en-hance anatomical diversity, we incorporated 20,000 synthetic 3D volumes generated following [46]. 

Held-out Testing Datasets. The remaining 6 independent datasets, totaling 1,361 3D scans, were used as held-out test sets to assess generalization across cross-center domain shifts (brain, abdomen, and prostate cohorts from unseen scanners [43], [47]–[49]), unseen anatomical targets (nasal cavity [50]), and unseen species (rat lungs [51]). 

Data Preprocessing and Augmentation. Following Neu-roverse3D [19], all datasets were standardized to a fixed volumetric dimension of 128 ×128 ×128 . Intensity values were normalized following percentile clipping, supplemented by diverse spatial and intensity perturbations for online augmenta-tion. For 2D training, both target and context slices were not sourced from independent 2D images but were dynamically extracted from 3D samples along the same anatomical axis to ensure planar consistency. Specifically, slice sampling fol-lowed a multinomial distribution weighted by the foreground area, ensuring the prioritization of slices with prominent anatomical features. Finally, a rolling mechanism was applied so that sampled slices iteratively served as the target while the remaining slices automatically formed a coherent context sequence.

B. Implementation Details 

Model Training. All experiments were implemented in Py-Torch and run on NVIDIA V100 and A800 GPUs. We optimized UniMedSeg using Adam with separate parame-ter groups for visual and language components. The visual modules, including patch embeddings, type embeddings, the Transformer encoder, and decoders, were trained with a base learning rate of lr base = 0 .001 , while the text adapter used a learning rate of 100 × lr base to accelerate visual-semantic alignment. To expose the model to diverse segmentation paradigms, each training instance was sampled from four con-ditioning modes with equal probability: visual examples only, interactive prompts only, language instructions only, or hybrid conditioning, where the hybrid mode uniformly combines two or three paradigms. Language instructions were used only for brain datasets. For non-brain datasets, language-related modes were removed while preserving the relative sampling ratios among the remaining modes. We further sampled 3D and 2D training instances with probabilities of 0.65 and 0.35, respectively. 

Model Evaluation. We use the Dice coefficient to evaluate segmentation performance, reporting average scores over 8 runs with randomly sampled contexts for robust estimation.

C. Comparison Methods 

We benchmarked UniMedSeg against state-of-the-art mod-els across three segmentation paradigms: 

In-Context Learning (ICL). We evaluated 3D (4 contexts) and 2D (16 contexts) ICL using the official weights of SegGPT [16], Tyche [52], Neuralizer [17], UniverSeg [3], Neuroverse3D [19], and Medverse [20]. For 3D volumes, 2D ICL models adopted the slice-level processing protocol from [20]. 

Interactive Segmentation. We compared 3D segmentation using a single point or bounding box per volume against SAM-Med3D [21], Medical SAM 2 [53], nnInteractive [22], and WS-ICL [54]. In contrast, the 2D-native MedSAM [1] was evaluated using dense, slice-by-slice bounding boxes. 

Language-Guided Segmentation. We evaluated text-driven 3D segmentation on brain datasets. VoxTell [26] was evaluated using original weights since target classes were included in its training data. Meanwhile, SegVol [6], BiomedParseV2 [28], Text3DSAM [27], and CAT [25] were fine-tuned on our training set for fair comparison on unseen classes.

Ablation Analysis of Key Components. As summarized in Table VI, we validated the backbone and text adapter of UniMedSeg. Replacing SplitEvaBlock with a standard ViT or removing the MLP text adapter causes significant performance degradation across all paradigms, confirming their critical roles in capturing 3D spatial dependencies and ensuring robust visual-semantic alignment. 

Architectural Ablation on Attention and Spatial Align-ment. To validate the architectural necessity of our unified sequence interface, we conducted two critical ablations fo-cusing on the attention mechanism and spatial positional embedding strategies (Table VII). First, to assess the impact of context isolation, we replaced the Decoupled Split Attention with a standard global Full Attention mechanism, allowing unconstrained cross-attention among all tokens. Reverting to Full Attention results in consistent performance degradation AUTHOR et al. : TITLE 9

across all paradigms. This indicates that unconstrained cross-attention introduces severe context crosstalk, where the model is distracted by irrelevant background features from non-target visual examples. Our Decoupled Split Attention effectively filters out this noise, enforcing robust, pair-level semantic matching. Second, to evaluate the role of multimodal spatial alignment, we removed the independent 2D/3D RoPE iden-tity anchoring, substituting it with a continuous 1D global RoPE across the flattened sequence. This modification leads to a catastrophic performance drop, particularly in 3D ICL. Without a shared spatial coordinate system to explicitly link prompt locations to the target anatomy, the Transformer loses vital structural priors. Flattening complex volumetric geome-tries into a 1D sequence forces the model to infer spatial correspondences from scratch, proving highly ineffective for dense medical image segmentation. Furthermore, we evaluated the efficiency of Decoupled Split Attention against standard Full and Masked Attention on A800 GPU (Table VIII). While Full Attention permits unrestricted global interactions, Masked Attention uses boolean masks to restrict cross-context depen-dencies to simulate our isolation strategy. Despite its functional equivalence, both baselines retain dense O(N 2) complexity, rapidly triggering OOM or severe latency explosion as context size increases. In contrast, our Split Attention maintains stable linear scaling.

V. C ONCLUSION 

In this work, we presented UniMedSeg, a Transformer-centric foundation model designed to address the scaling bottleneck caused by isolated prompt interfaces, and separate 2D/3D model designs in universal medical image segmenta-tion. By formulating visual examples, geometric prompts, lan-guage instructions, and 2D/3D images as contextual inputs in a shared sequence space, UniMedSeg enables diverse medical data to be jointly absorbed within a single in-context learning framework. This unified interface facilitates cross-paradigm and cross-dimensional knowledge sharing while reducing the need for paradigm- or dimension-specific architectural branches. To support scalable long-context learning, we further introduced Decoupled Split Attention, which reduces attention complexity while preserving hardware-friendly computation and effective context-target interaction. Trained on large-scale heterogeneous data, UniMedSeg consistently achieves strong performance across visual in-context, interactive, and language-guided segmentation tasks without task-specific fine-tuning. Extensive held-out evaluations further demonstrate its robust generalization under cross-center domain shifts, unseen anatomical structures, and cross-species targets, highlighting the effectiveness of unified sequence-based in-context learning for scalable medical segmentation foundation models.
