## 1 Introduction 

3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) has evolved from per-scene optimization to feed-forward networks that regress Gaussian parameters from offline image sequences. Moving beyond this offline paradigm, a more challenging yet practical setting is online feed-forward 3DGS, where the system incrementally constructs a 3DGS representation from streaming unposed images without access to future frames while maintaining efficiency and novel view synthesis (NVS) quality. Such ca-pability is crucial for latency-sensitive applications, including video stabiliza-tion [13, 30], AR/VR interaction [7, 25], and autonomous navigation [8, 28]. To build high-quality online 3DGS under streaming inputs, optimization-based frameworks [10,13,18] perform per-frame iterative refinement to maintain multi-view consistency and rendering fidelity. However, these approaches require iterative updates, which inevitably incur substantial computational overhead, leading to prohibitive latency for applications that require immediate feedback. To improve efficiency, feed-forward approaches offer a natural alternative. Recent methods enable real-time streaming geometry recovery [9,34] or focus on offline feed-forward 3DGS reconstruction [6, 16]. However, these approaches do not directly extend to streaming NVS. Unlike depth or point cloud estimation, 3DGS-based NVS is highly sensitive to cross-view misalignment; small pose or scale errors can accumulate over time and progressively degrade rendering qual-ity. Consequently, achieving both high-quality rendering and low-latency 3DGS reconstruction from streaming unposed inputs remains unresolved. To address this problem, we propose FreeStreamGS , an online feed-forward 3DGS framework that achieves high-quality NVS directly from unposed stream-ing inputs (Fig. 1). We observe that lifting offline feed-forward 3DGS to online fundamentally weakens the implicit regularization of multi-view geometric con-sistency, as future observations are unavailable during inference. As a result, both geometric reconstruction and camera estimation become progressively un-stable, degrading NVS quality. Specifically, (1) frame-wise intrinsic prediction drifts, introducing global scale inconsistency and cross-frame misalignment of reconstructed Gaussians (left side of Fig. 2); and (2) per-pixel 3DGS constrains Gaussian updates along viewing rays, making the reconstruction sensitive to coupled pose–depth errors that accumulate and distort Gaussian placement over time (right side of Fig. 2). To address these challenges, FreeStreamGS incorporates two synergistic mech-anisms designed to restore geometric stability in causal streaming. First, we propose Decoupled Intrinsic Recovery Head (DIR-Head) . With the observation that most streaming applications use fixed camera intrinsics and by anchoring intrinsics across time, DIR-Head enables multiple frames to share a unified es-timate, mitigating cumulative scale drift and enforcing global scale consistency across per-frame 3DGS predictions. Second, to address inaccuracies in per-pixel 3DGS caused by pose–depth drift, we further introduce Dynamic Point Refine-ment Offsets (DPR-Offsets) , which refine Gaussian positions by compensating for coupled pose–depth errors and relaxing rigid pixel-to-3DGS unprojection constraints. In summary, our main contributions include: 1. FreeStreamGS , an online feed-forward framework for high-quality 3DGS re-construction from unposed streaming inputs in a causal manner; 2. DIR-Head , motivated by the observation that frame-wise intrinsic estima-tion often suffers from drastic drifts and recursive bias; it stabilizes intrinsic estimation to mitigate global scale inconsistency. 3. DPR-Offsets , a geometric compensator that provides the necessary spatial relaxation to break free from rigid viewing-direction constraints, thereby holistically rectifying the coupled drift of camera poses and depth to ensure precise alignment of 3D Gaussian primitives. Extensive experiments demonstrate FreeStreamGS achieves competitive perfor-mance among online methods while remaining comparable to offline feed-forward approaches, offering a practical balance between latency and rendering fidelity. 4 R. ## 2 Related Work 

Feed-forward-based Offline 3DGS leverages neural networks to directly regress 3D Gaussian primitives, bypassing the time-consuming per-scene opti-mization of the original 3DGS. While early efforts focused on generalizable recon-struction from sparse views, recent advancements have shifted this paradigm to-ward large-scale universal modeling for unposed image sequences. Approaches [1, 2, 23, 26, 32] such as PixelSplat [1] and MVSplat [2] regress Gaussians directly by leveraging epipolar geometry or cost volumes with given poses. Subsequently, methods [4, 29] like NoPoSplat [29] extend this paradigm to the unposed set-ting, employing canonical coordinate anchoring to eliminate the dependency on pre-computed poses. More recently, the field has converged towards unified trans-former architectures capable of high-fidelity synthesis. Several works [6,12,14,16] leverage powerful backbones (e.g., VGGT [22]) to jointly predict camera param-eters and 3D scenes in a single pass. For instance, AnySplat [6] utilizes a differ-entiable voxelization strategy to merge redundant primitives from unconstrained views, and WorldMirror [16] introduces multi-modal prompting to unify depth and 3DGS generation. However, despite their impressive visual quality, these methods fundamentally rely on the non-causal processing of the entire image se-quence to extract global context, rendering them ill-suited for online streaming scenarios. 

Optimization-based Online 3DGS achieve high-fidelity streaming recon-struction by jointly performing pose tracking and iterative optimization of 3D Gaussian primitives. Despite strong visual quality, representative works [5, 10, 18,27], such as OnTheFly-NVS [18], rely on per-frame optimization which incurs substantial inference latency, limiting their applicability in low-latency scenarios requiring immediate feedback. In contrast, FreeStreamGS bypasses this heavy computational overhead by performing reconstruction in a single feed-forward pass, achieving low-latency processing without the need for iterative refinement. 

Feed-forward-based Online 3DGS avoid iterative optimization by perform-ing incremental reconstruction in a single forward pass. Representative meth-ods [3, 11, 24] such as StreamGS [11] and SaLon3R [3] establish correspon-dences using adjacent frame pairs to reduce redundancy. However, this pairwise paradigm provides only a limited temporal receptive field, preventing effective long-term context aggregation and leading to geometric drift over time. To ad-dress this, we leverage full-history causal attention to expand the temporal re-ceptive field and introduce a decoupled intrinsic head with dynamic offsets to holistically rectify geometric instabilities, ensuring high rendering quality.

## 4 Proposed Method: FreeStreamGS 

FreeStreamGS is an online feed-forward framework for high-quality 3DGS recon-struction from unposed image streams (Fig. 3). Our pipeline is organized into four sequential stages: (1) Decoupled Extrinsic and Intrinsic Recovery (Sec. 4.1) aggregates temporal context and leverages the Decoupled Intrinsic Recovery Head (DIR-Head) for stable intrinsic estimation; (2) Gaussian Primitive Re-covery (Sec. 4.2) generates explicit 3D Gaussian primitives refined by Dynamic Point Refinement Offsets (DPR-Offsets) under geometric supervision; (3) On-line Recursive Gaussian Fusion (Sec. 4.3) incrementally consolidates streaming primitives into a consistent global representation; and (4) a tailored Training Objective (Sec. 4.4) designed a causal-aware loss suite to enforce multi-view con-sistency and ensure high-quality rendering. 

4.1 Decoupled Extrinsic and Intrinsic Recovery 

As shown in Fig. 3, our framework begins by processing the unposed streaming input through a full-history causal feature extractor based on StreamVGGT [34]. This module leverages full-history causal attention and efficient KV caching to establish long-term temporal consensus, yielding a feature map Ft for each frame. From these features, we proceed to recover camera parameters via two decoupled heads designed to ensure long-term geometric stability. Specifically, first, a causal extrinsic head Φext queries Ft and interacts with the cached historical state Ht−1

to predict the relative camera extrinsic matrix ˆEt = [ ˆRt|ˆtt], facilitating smooth trajectory recovery relative to the initial observation. Second, while camera extrinsics are inherently relative and temporal, intrin-sics are physically static properties of the lens. To address the drift inherent in frame-wise intrinsic estimation, we propose the Decoupled Intrinsic Recovery Head (DIR-Head) . We enforce a physically grounded constraint by assuming a square pixel aspect ratio and a globally consistent focal length for the entire se-quence ( fx = fy = f ). As illustrated in Fig. 3, the DIR-Head is implemented via a lightweight MLP Φint that operates exclusively on the first frame feature F0 to anchor the global coordinate system. To ensure this module remains resolution-agnostic, the MLP is designed to predict a normalized focal length. This value is then scaled by the image width W to obtain the absolute focal length ˆf in pixel units: 

ˆf = Φint (F0) · W.  (3) By predicting a normalized ratio rather than absolute pixel values, DIR-Head achieves better generalization across different image scales and stabilizes the numerical range during training. The final intrinsic matrix ˆK is constructed as:

4.2 Gaussian Primitive Recovery 

With the causal features Ft extracted and camera parameters Pt recovered, our framework transforms these latent representations into explicit 3D Gaussian primitives, as illustrated in Fig. 3. Specifically, we employ a DPT-like [20] con-volutional Gaussian decoder D to map the feature map Ft into a set of per-pixel Gaussian attribute maps A = {Dt, qt, st, α t, ct}, as defined in Sec. 3. We observe that the standard per-pixel 3DGS representation constrains Gaus-sian position updates primarily along the viewing direction through depth lifting. In a causal setting, this constraint causes small depth and pose errors to accumu-late and distort Gaussian placement, thereby significantly degrading rendering quality. To resolve this, we propose Dynamic Point Refinement Offsets (DPR-Offsets) . This module enables the decoder D to additionally predict a per-pixel 3D residual map ∆μ t ∈ RH×W ×3, which provides the necessary spatial relax-ation to rectify joint pose-depth drift. 4.4 Training Objective 

To ensure high-fidelity scene reconstruction and accurate parameter recovery, our framework is optimized end-to-end using a causal-aware multi-task objective, as denoted by Fig. 3. The final training objective Ltotal is a weighted sum of the intrinsic distillation loss Lint (Sec. 4.1), the scale-invariant geometric loss Ldepth 

(Sec. 4.2), and a specialized generalization-weighted photometric loss Lphoto :

Ltotal = Lphoto + λdepth Ldepth + λint Lint . (10) For the photometric component, we compute a reconstruction loss term Lrec 

that combines MSE, SSIM, and LPIPS metrics: Lrec (ˆI, I) = LMSE + λ1LSSIM +

λ2LLPIPS , where ˆI and I denote the rendered image and the corresponding ground truth. Crucially, we observe that standard feed-forward models tend to overfit to the input views, behaving as latent auto-encoders that fail to cap-ture the true underlying 3D structure. Implementation Details. We implement FreeStreamGS in PyTorch and train it end-to-end for 5 epochs on four NVIDIA RTX 5880 Ada GPUs (48GB), requir-ing approximately one day. We use AdamW [17] with a global batch size of 4 (one per GPU). The loss weights are set as λ1 = 0 .05 (SSIM), λ2 = 0 .05 (LPIPS), 

λnovel = 1 .5, λdepth = 1 .0, and λint = 10 .0. For network initialization, the full-history causal feature extractor and causal extrinsic head are initialized from StreamVGGT [34] and kept frozen during training. This design choice is moti-vated by two considerations. First, as we train in an unposed manner without ground-truth camera labels, we leverage these pre-trained priors to avoid con-vergence instability or degenerate solutions, following the training philosophy of AnySplat [6]. Second, our DPR-Offsets are explicitly designed to compensate for residual pose drift at the Gaussian primitive level, rendering additional ex-trinsic optimization unnecessary in practice. Ablation Study. As shown in Table 4, we conduct an ablation study on the DL3DV-140 [15], RE10K [33], and NYUv2 [19] benchmarks to evaluate the con-tribution of three key components: (1) w/o Novel-View-Weighted Supervision (NV-Sup) , (2) w/o DIR-Head , and (3) w/o DPR-Offsets . Across all datasets, re-moving any component leads to performance degradation, while the full model consistently achieves the strongest results. 

1) Impact of DIR-Head. Removing DIR-Head results in the largest perfor-mance drop, indicating the impact of recursive drift in frame-wise intrinsic esti-mation. Inaccurate intrinsics lead to inconsistent projection scales, which in turn cause misalignment of reconstructed geometry across frames. This observation suggests that anchoring the global coordinate system via the first-frame feature 

F0 is important for mitigating intrinsic instability and maintaining consistent geometric alignment throughout the sequence. We also find teacher-forcing essential for training DIR-Head from scratch. Without the teacher prior, early erratic focal length predictions would lead to chaotic unprojections, preventing the downstream decoder from learning consis-tent scene geometry. This confirms that teacher-forcing is a crucial facilitator for initial convergence in an unposed online setting. 

2) Impact of DPR-Offsets. Conventional per-pixel 3DGS recovery constrains Gaussian updates along camera rays, making the reconstruction sensitive to cou-pled pose–depth errors. Application: Online Full-frame Video Stabilization. To demonstrate the practical utility of FreeStreamGS on real-world captured videos, we apply our framework to online full-frame video stabilization, an area that remains an open challenge. Since FreeStreamGS produces feed-forward 3DGS from raw, unposed streaming inputs, it can effectively stabilize unconstrained hand-held sequences in a causal manner. Specifically, we smooth the estimated camera extrinsic tra-jectory using a Savitzky–Golay filter [21] and perform NVS along the smoothed trajectory based on the reconstructed 3DGS. During rendering, a sliding win-dow ( N = 20 ) aggregates Gaussians from neighboring frames to reduce potential pixel misalignment. As illustrated by the flattened temporal profiles (X–T slices) in Fig. 6, high-frequency camera shakes are effectively suppressed over 150-frame sequences. ## 6 Conclusion 

In this work, we presented FreeStreamGS , an online feed-forward framework for high-fidelity 3DGS reconstruction from unposed streaming inputs. By introduc-FreeStreamGS 15 

ing the DIR-Head and DPR-Offsets , we effectively resolve recursive intrinsic drift and rectify Gaussian placement distortions stemming from rigid viewing-direction constraints, thereby restoring the geometric consistency typically weak-ened in causal settings. Extensive experiments demonstrate that FreeStreamGS achieves rendering quality and stability competitive with state-of-the-art offline feed-forward methods. 

Limitations. Despite these advancements, handling highly dynamic scenes with fast-moving objects remains challenging for ours. In addition, our method is not well suited for ultra large-scale or long-range scenes, as the high storage overhead of 3DGS limits scalability. 16 R. Chen et al.

## D Failure Cases and Analysis 

As illustrated in Fig. D.4, our method exhibits suboptimal performance in spe-cific challenging scenarios. For regions with transparency (a), the inherent am-biguity in depth estimation—which often perceives depths behind the glass sur-face—coupled with complex light transport like reflections leads to blurred ge-ometric artifacts. For scenarios containing thin structures or dense natural ele-ments like grass (b), the limited resolution of depth estimation fails to capture fine-grained geometry. Moreover, the lack of a more sophisticated fusion mech-anism to effectively consolidate redundant Gaussians in these high-frequency areas results in reduced structural fidelity and over-smoothing. Beyond these visual artifacts, our framework has limitations in dynamic and large-scale scenes. First, it lacks explicit temporal modeling for dynamic objects, as the system is primarily designed under a static scene assumption.
