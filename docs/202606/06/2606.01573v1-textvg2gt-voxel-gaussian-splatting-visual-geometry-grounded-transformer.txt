## Abstract 

Gaussian splatting has shown strong potential for 3D re-construction and novel view synthesis. However, most ex-isting methods require accurate camera parameters and per-scene optimization, while feed-forward methods with pixel-aligned Gaussian primitives often suffer from arti-facts and non-uniform primitives. In this paper, we pro-pose VG 2GT, a Voxel-Gaussian Splatting Visual Geometry-Grounded Transformer. VG 2GT leverages a frozen pre-trained visual foundation model (VFM), incorporates a multi-scale differentiable voxel module to enhance geo-metric understanding, and directly splits and regresses Gaussian primitive parameters from voxel features. Dur-ing training, depth maps are supervised through stochas-tic solid volume rendering, enabling geometrically accu-rate Gaussian scene reconstruction while keeping the vi-sual foundation model fully frozen. This design enables VG 2GT to be seamlessly plugged into any patch-feature-based VFM, while substantially reducing the required train-ing cost. VG 2GT outperforms current state-of-the-art meth-ods on widely used DTU, Replica, TAT, and ScanNet datasets. 

## 1. Introduction 

Recent visual foundation models [19, 37, 38] have demonstrated strong capabilities in reconstructing 3D scenes from multi-view 2D images. By consolidating com-plex multi-stage 3D reconstruction pipelines into a unified end-to-end large-scale visual Transformer, these methods substantially improve reconstruction efficiency while mit-igating the error accumulation in conventional pipelines. Most visual foundation models represent reconstructed scenes as depth maps or point clouds, exhibiting strong ge-

> arXiv:2606.01573v1 [cs.CV] 1 Jun 2026

ometric priors. However, point-cloud-based representations often fall short in preserving camera-level image details and view-dependent appearance. In contrast, radiance-field-based representations, such as NeRF [24] and 3D Gaussian Splatting [15], have achieved remarkable performance in novel view synthesis (NVS) and 3D reconstruction. These methods typically rely on known camera intrinsics and extrinsics, and therefore often incorporate structure-from-motion (SfM) as a preprocessing stage to estimate cam-era parameters [31], followed by per-scene optimization to obtain the final reconstruction. Such a pipeline inevitably propagates errors from SfM into the reconstruction process. Moreover, the computational burden of per-scene optimiza-tion significantly limits the efficiency and practicality of radiance-field-based reconstruction methods. Feed-forward 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) recon-struction methods [3, 39, 46] have recently emerged as a promising direction for efficient high-fidelity scene recon-struction. By bypassing expensive per-scene optimization, these methods directly regress Gaussian scene representa-tions from multi-view images and camera parameters. Nev-ertheless, they still require camera poses to construct multi-view cost volumes for depth estimation, which limits their applicability in real-world scenarios and makes them sus-ceptible to cumulative errors introduced by SfM. Driven by the rapid progress of visual foundation mod-els, pose-free feed-forward 3DGS methods have also been explored [12, 19, 49]. However, existing approaches com-monly adopt pixel-aligned Gaussian primitives, enforcing a one-to-one correspondence between 2D pixels and 3D points. In practice, a 3D point is often observed by multiple pixels across different views, and effectively aggregating these multi-view observations is crucial for forming a com-pact, coherent, and geometrically consistent scene represen-tation. The pixel-aligned formulation inevitably leads to un-even Gaussian distributions and cross-view inconsistencies [18], thereby limiting reconstruction quality and multi-view consistency. In this paper, we propose VG 2GT, a feed-forward non-pixel-aligned 3DGS reconstruction framework that directly regresses geometrically accurate Gaussian scenes and cam-era parameters from an arbitrary number of pose-free im-ages. To alleviate the uneven distribution of Gaussian primitives, we introduce a multi-scale differentiable voxel module that aggregates point-cloud features into structured voxel features. At the coarse scale, we employ PointTrans-former [45] together with trilinear interpolation to compute complementary geometric features, which are injected into patch tokens to enhance global and consistent scene under-standing. At the fine scale, we use a DPT head to extract global point-cloud features and voxelize them into a struc-tured representation. The resulting voxel features are fur-ther refined by PointMLP and decoded by a self-splitting Gaussian head to predict the parameters of Gaussian primi-tives. During training, instead of relying on standard 3DGS rasterization [15], we adopt stochastic solid volume render-ing, where Gaussian primitives are modeled as continuous stochastic solids and rendered along camera rays [25]. This rendering formulation improves the quality of synthesized Gaussian depth maps and further enhances the overall ge-ometric reconstruction accuracy. Moreover, unlike existing feed-forward 3DGS methods that typically require training the entire model, our framework freezes all original param-eters of the visual foundation model (backbone, depth head, camera head). Only the multi-scale voxel module and Gaus-sian primitive decoding head are optimized. This design substantially reduces training cost and enables our method to be seamlessly plugged into various patch-feature-based visual foundation models. 1. We propose VG 2GT, a feed-forward, non-pixel-aligned 3DGS reconstruction framework that enables geometri-cally accurate Gaussian scene reconstruction from vari-ous number of unposed and uncalibrated images. 2. We introduce a multi-scale voxel-based feature enhance-ment strategy, where Gaussian primitives are split and regressed from fine voxel features. Together with train-ing supervision based on stochastic solid volumetric ren-dering, this design effectively alleviates overlapping arti-facts and geometric reconstruction errors commonly ob-served in pixel-aligned feed-forward 3DGS methods. 3. Extensive experiments on multiple datasets demonstrate that VG 2GT achieves state-of-the-art geometric recon-struction accuracy and novel view synthesis quality com-pared with existing feed-forward 3DGS methods. 4. VG 2GT substantially reduces the training cost, enabling high-quality 3DGS reconstruction on frozen visual foun-dation models using only academic-scale computational resources, while remaining adaptable to a wide range of visual foundation models. 

## 2. Related Works 

2.1. Traditional Pipelines 

Traditional pipelines for geometric reconstruction and novel view synthesis (NVS) from RGB images are typically divided into three stages: camera pose estimation, dense re-construction, and per-scene optimization for NVS. Given hand-crafted features [20] or learning-based descriptors [6], together with feature matching methods [30], camera poses and sparse 3D points can be estimated. Based on the esti-mated camera poses and dense RGB image features, dense depth estimation can then be performed [32, 48]. Among these techniques, plane sweeping has been widely adopted and further developed due to its favorable trade-off between computational cost and reconstruction performance [7]. Af-ter dense point-cloud reconstruction, traditional pipelines usually enhance the scene representation and optimize the full scene to further improve reconstruction accuracy and enable NVS, with neural radiance fields [24] and 3D Gaus-sian Splatting [15] being representative recent examples. Although each component of traditional pipelines has achieved substantial progress, such a decoupled design in-evitably leads to error accumulation. In particular, er-rors introduced during camera pose estimation can signif-icantly affect subsequent per-scene optimization for NVS [52]. Moreover, the separated design of traditional pipelines also influences the overall computational efficiency. 

2.2. Optimization-based Novel View Synthesis 

Common representations for 3D scenes include triangle meshes, point clouds, voxel grids, multi-plane images, and neural implicit functions. In recent years, radiance field have shown strong potential for NVS and 3D reconstruc-tion. Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) and its follow-up methods [2, 24] map 3D positions and viewing directions to colors and densities using a multi-layer perceptron (MLP), achieving high-quality NVS. To address the computational inefficiency and limited transferability of neural implicit representations, 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) [15] repre-sents a scene as a set of explicit Gaussian primitives and enables real-time NVS through differentiable rasterization. Subsequent works further improve rendering quality [21], geometric accuracy [10, 11, 50], and training efficiency [29]. Most NeRF and 3DGS methods require accurate cam-era parameters, which are primarily estimated by classi-cal SfM methods [31] or improved variants [27, 36], in-evitably introducing additional errors. Therefore, several recent works attempt to jointly optimize camera parame-ters and scene representations. However, these methods ei-ther require sequential RGB-D frames as input and rely on known camera intrinsics [9, 13], or are restricted to limited scene settings [23, 43]. In addition, they all require per-scene training. In contrast, our method directly regresses 3DGS scenes through a feed-forward network, eliminat-ing the requirement for camera parameters and per-scene training, thereby significantly improving reconstruction ef-ficiency while avoiding accumulated errors. 

2.3. Visual Foundation Models 

With the rapid development of parallel computing hard-ware and neural networks, visual foundation models have demonstrated great potential in 3D vision tasks. DUSt3R [38] performs end-to-end reconstruction from two uncon-strained RGB images to point maps through self-attention and cross-attention. VGGT [37] further introduces an al-ternating attention mechanism to enable joint understand-ing of multi-view RGB images. Subsequent works have investigated improvements in encoder design [14], training strategies [19], and permutation equivariance of input views [40]. These end-to-end visual foundation models demon-strate that different 3D vision tasks, such as camera param-eter estimation, depth estimation, and point-map prediction, can be jointly learned with a shared large-scale Transformer backbone. Existing visual foundation models commonly adopt ViT, such as DINO[26], as the image encoder and use alternating attention mechanisms as the network backbone. For decod-ing, sparse outputs such as camera parameters are typically predicted by MLP heads, while dense outputs such as depth maps and point maps are decoded by DPT [28]. Through DPT, patch tokens, which are usually downsampled by a factor of 14, can be progressively upsampled and aligned with the input image pixels, enabling full-resolution depth and point-cloud estimation. Several works focus on Gaussian scene regression [19, 47]. However, most of them decode Gaussian scenes using DPT heads, resulting in pixel-aligned Gaussian primitives. This formulation inevitably causes ununiform Gaussian dis-tributions and multi-view inconsistencies [18]. AnySplat [12] performs non-pixel-aligned Gaussian scene reasoning through differentiable voxelization, but it still first regresses per-scene pixel-aligned Gaussian primitives and subse-quently aggregates them into a Gaussian scene through vox-elization.

We propose VG 2GT, a feed-forward framework for con-structing 3DGS representations from unconstrained RGB images. As a backend enhancement to VGGT [37], our method strengthens global scene consistency through multi-scale voxel construction, substantially improving both novel view synthesis (NVS) quality and geometric reconstruction accuracy, as shown in Figure 2.

3.1.2. Bottlenecks 

VGGT obtains one token for each image patch, typically corresponding to a 14 × 14 pixel region. As a result, the im-age features are usually downsampled by a factor of 14. To recover dense depth maps, VGGT adopts DPT [28] to pro-gressively upsample image tokens into pixel-aligned fea-tures. To further obtain feed-forward 3DGS scenes, rep-resentative methods such as Depth Anything 3 (DA3) [19] apply a DPT head based on the backbone to produce pixel-aligned features, followed by a lightweight MLP that re-gresses the Gaussian attributes for each pixel. However, pixel-aligned features lack global consistency in feed-forward networks and introduce excessive overlap, since the architecture enforces a one-to-one correspondence between each pixel and one Gaussian primitive. 3.4. Training 

We choose DA3-GIANT [19] as the VFM for training. Throughout the training process, the DA3 backbone and all its original decoders are kept frozen. In addition, we discard the original Gaussian head of DA3 and train the proposed differentiable multi-scale voxel module instead. This design allows our method to be plugged into arbitrary feed-forward patch-feature-based VFMs while preserving the strong representation capability of the pretrained back-bone during training. 

Datasets . Following prior works [17, 37], we train our model on a mixture of three datasets, including synthetic dataset Infinigen, video-captured dataset KITTI [8, 34], and RGB-D captured dataset ScanNet [4]. These datasets cover both indoor and outdoor scenes under synthetic and real-captured conditions. Such diverse training data improves the generalization and adaptability of our model, enhancing transferability to unseen scenes.

Implementation Details . We train the model by op-timizing the proposed training objective with the standard AdamW optimizer for a total of 420K iterations. The initial learning rate is set to 3 × 10 −6 and is gradually decayed to 1 × 10 −8 using a cosine annealing schedule. Following prior work [12], we set the default voxel size to 0.002 me-ters, split each voxel feature into 2 Gaussian primitives, and use an image resolution of 224 × 448 . At each iteration, we randomly sample 3-18 images. Training is conducted on 2 NVIDIA RTX PRO 6000 GPUs and takes 49.5 hours, corresponding to 99 GPU hours.

Datasets. We evaluate all methods on three datasets: Replica [33], which contains synthetic indoor scenes; DTU [1], the standard benchmark for geometry reconstruction from real captured RGBs; and Tanks and Temples (TAT) [16], which consists of real captured outdoor scenes. This evaluation protocol covers diverse scene types and enables a comprehensive assessment of robustness across differ-ent environments. During evaluation, all input images are center-cropped and uniformly resized to 518 × 518 .

Baselines. We compare against state-of-the-art (SOTA) pose-free feed-forward 3DGS methods, including FreeSplatter [47], AnySplat [12], YoNoSplat [49], and DA3 [19]. These methods predict pixel-aligned Gaussian scenes, while AnySplat further introduces a voxel module to re-voxelize and aggregate the predicted Gaussians. For a fair comparison, we use the same default voxel size as our method ( 0.002 m ). We also include posed feed-forward 3DGS baselines, including MVSplat [3], VolSplat [39], and DepthSplat [46]. In addition, we compare with the standard 3DGS initialization strategy [15], where VGGT [37] is used to generate colored point clouds for initializing the Gaussian scene. 

4.2. Evaluation of Reconstruction Geometry 

We evaluate the geometric reconstruction performance of our method on the DTU, Replica, and TAT datasets. To solve the scale ambiguity inherent to pose-free reconstruc-tion methods, following prior works[12, 35], we first glob-ally align the predicted depth map of each scene to the ground-truth depth map. RMSE, ABS, δ1.25 and δ1.05 are then computed on the aligned depth maps. For the DTU and TAT datasets, the ground-truth depth maps are obtained by back-projecting scanned point clouds; therefore, we apply valid depth masks before both alignment and evaluation. We evaluate the geometric reconstruction performance under different numbers of input views. For each scene, we randomly select 3, 10, and 20 input images for evalua-tion. During training, RGB and depth maps of the Gaussian scene are rendered using stochastic solid volume render-ing. During evaluation, we render the generated Gaussian scenes using both vanilla 3DGS rasterizer [15] and stochas-tic solid volume rendering. As shown in Table 1 and Fig-ure 4, the Gaussian scenes regressed by our method achieve substantially higher geometric accuracy than SOTA base-lines across most datasets and multi-view settings. Pixel-aligned Gaussian scene regression methods, represented by DA3, often suffer from overlapping artifacts caused by the misalignment of multi-view Gaussian primitives due to cou-pled errors in depth prediction and camera pose estimation, which are detrimental to scene geometry. ## 5. Conclusion 

We present VG 2GT, a voxel-Gaussian Splatting visual geometry grounded transformer. By introducing a differ-entiable multi-scale voxel module, VG 2GT effectively mit-igates the overlapping artifacts commonly arise in pixel-aligned feed-forward Gaussian Splatting methods. Dur-ing training, we further supervise scene geometry with stochastic solid volume rendering, enabling accurate scene reconstruction and novel view synthesis from multi-view unposed images. Moreover, by designing the network as a lightweight add-on to a visual foundation model (VFM), our method substantially reduces the training cost while remaining plug-and-play compatible with different VFMs. Extensive experiments across multiple datasets show that, VG 2GT reconstructs geometrically accurate Gaussian scenes under different input settings, achieves state-of-the-art performance in both RGB and depth novel view synthesis, and effectively controls the otherwise linear growth of Gaussian primitives as the number of input views increases. A.1.2. Model Evaluation 

Since all datasets used in our evaluation (Replica [33], DTU [1], Tanks and Temples [16], and ScanNet [4]) consist of sequential frames, we sample input views within a con-strained range. The training scenes and evaluation scenes are kept strictly disjoint. For the 3-view evaluation on DTU, we follow the commonly used sparse-view sampling proto-col (23, 24, and 33) [11, 44] to ensure a fair comparison. During evaluation, for all VGGT-based feed-forward methods, we use the default resolution of 518 × 518 for scene regression and rendering. As shown in Table A1 and Ta-ble A2, our method consistently outperforms the baselines in both Gaussian-scene novel view synthesis and depth ren-dering across different resolution settings. For the ablation studies, to reduce training cost, we do not train each ablated variant from scratch. Instead, each variant is initialized from the full model and further trained for an additional 30K iterations. For all modules except the self-splitting Gaussian MLP, the network archi-tecture remains unchanged after ablation. Therefore, ini-tializing from the full model and continuing training does not introduce additional architectural or parameter differ-ences. For the ablation of the self-splitting Gaussian MLP, the final prediction branch has a different output dimen-sion and therefore needs to be reinitialized. However, this branch contains only a lightweight two-layer MLP, while the preceding PointMLP-based feature extractor remains unchanged. In this setting, 30K iterations are sufficient for the reinitialized branch to converge again. As shown in Ta-ble 4, ablating any individual component leads to perfor-mance degradation during this continued training process, which further verifies the effectiveness of each proposed module.
